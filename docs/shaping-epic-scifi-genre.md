# Epic Sci-Fi Genre — Shaping

6th Storyforge genre. Dune x Red Rising x Sun Eater. Feudal space empire, not scrappy survival. The player is inside the machine of empire; every choice serves it or breaks something.

---

## Frame

**Source:**
> Future genres backlog item. Dune x Red Rising x Sun Eater vibe. Not a rework of the current Firefly-style space opera; a separate 6th genre. Great houses competing for control of a resource, religious institutions manipulating from the shadows, indigenous populations caught between imperial powers. Moral ambiguity through political structure.

**Problem:**
The five existing genres cover scrappy survival (space opera), classic adventure (fantasy), street-level hustle (cyberpunk), mercenary grimdark, and detective noir. None put the player inside institutional power. There's no genre where your character is an instrument of empire, where the system you serve is the source of both your strength and the story's moral weight.

**Outcome:**
A 6th genre that feels distinct from space opera in the same way grimdark feels distinct from fantasy. The player is embedded in a feudal power structure, not surviving on its margins. Choices are about loyalty, ambition, and complicity, not survival and scrappiness.

## Setting: The Hegemony

**The Hegemony.** A thousand-year interstellar civilization held together by one dependency: Resonants. Approximately 1 in 10,000 humans can attune to the Drift, a substrate beneath normal spacetime. Attuned individuals (Resonants) power FTL travel, planetary shields, long-range communication, and the empire's most devastating weapons. Without them, the Hegemony fragments into isolated systems within a generation.

### The Resource: Resonants

**Decision:** Human resource, not mineral or biological. The empire runs on people. You can't opt out of complicity because using imperial technology makes you part of the system.

Resonants are identified by mandatory testing at age seven. Those who attune are taken by the Synod for training and deployment. They have no legal autonomy. Their power is immense and their status is property.

### Resonant Degradation (The Dimming)

Sustained attunement degrades Resonants. The Synod doesn't disclose this. After years of heavy service, Resonants lose coherence: memory gaps, emotional flatness, physical tremors, and eventual inability to attune. The Synod calls it "the Dimming" internally and "completion of service" publicly.

Spent Resonants are sent to the **Ashen Wards** — Synod-administered hospice colonies on low-value worlds. Officially: "contemplative retirement for the faithful." In practice: warehousing of people the system used up. The houses know. The Throne knows. Nobody talks about it because acknowledging it would make the cost of imperial infrastructure visible.

The player encountering an Ashen Ward is a pivotal moment in any campaign. It makes the resource cost visceral rather than abstract.

### The Synod (State Religion)

Controls Resonant identification, testing, training, and deployment. Theological position: the Drift is divine, Resonants are vessels, unsanctioned attunement is heresy. The Synod is the empire's most powerful institution because they control the one thing everyone needs.

### The Great Houses

Control territory, military force, and economic production. Compete for Resonant allocation (the Synod distributes, but houses lobby, bribe, and threaten). House power is measured in Resonant count as much as fleet size. Alliances sealed with Resonant exchanges. Wars fought over Resonant-producing populations.

### The Imperial Throne

Theoretically supreme. Practically dependent on both houses and Synod. Uses the Synod as a check on the houses, the houses as a check on the Synod. The balancing act that holds the Hegemony together.

### The Undrift

The open secret: unregistered Resonants who escaped testing, hide their attunement, or were never identified. The Synod hunts them as heretics. The houses want them as off-book assets. Some form underground networks. Some sell their abilities. Some just want to be left alone.

### The Drift

Not fully understood. The Synod claims divine origin. House scholars theorize it's a natural phenomenon. Rumors: sustained attunement changes Resonants in ways the Synod doesn't disclose. The Drift responds to intent. The oldest Resonants hear something in it.

### Faction Axes

| Faction | What they want | Relationship to Resonants |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Great Houses** | Territory, power, Resonant allocation | Breed for them, compete for them, weaponize them |
| **The Synod** | Doctrinal control, monopoly on attunement | Identify, train, deploy, suppress unsanctioned |
| **Imperial Throne** | Stability, tax revenue, house obedience | Uses Synod as check on houses, uses houses as check on Synod |
| **The Undrift** | Freedom, survival, sometimes revolution | ARE unregistered Resonants or their protectors |
| **Military/Fleet** | Operational Resonants, strategic advantage | Consumes Resonants as fuel for ships and weapons |

## Origins (5)

Origin determines where you stand in relation to the Resonant system.

| Origin | Position | Relationship to Resonants |
|--------|----------|--------------------------|
| **Minor House** | Inside the machine, low on the ladder | Your house has few Resonants; gaining more is survival. You grew up watching your family trade dignity for allocation. |
| **Synod-Raised** | Inside the church, trained in doctrine | You were raised in the Synod's institutions. You may or may not be Resonant yourself, but you know how the machine identifies, trains, and controls them. |
| **Undrift** | Outside the system, hunted | You're an unregistered Resonant, or you were raised by one. The Synod wants you found. The houses want you owned. You want to stay free. |
| **Imperial Service** | Serves the Throne directly | Military, intelligence, or diplomatic corps. You enforce the system without belonging to any house. Loyalty is to the institution, not a bloodline. |
| **Ascendant** | Climbing from nothing | Born Untuned, no house, no church, no connections. You got here through ability, ambition, or someone else's investment. The system wasn't built for you and everyone knows it. |

### Origin Lore (full mechanical spec)

**Minor House**
You are of House Vael — minor by the standards that matter. Your family's Resonant allocation was halved two generations ago after a failed bid for a seat on the Conclave. Everything since has been recovery: marriages of convenience, trade concessions, and the slow erosion of pride in exchange for survival. You grew up watching your elders smile at people they despised.
Start with one house retainer contact at Favorable, one rival house contact at Wary. Advantage on Persuasion checks involving trade, negotiation, or alliance-building — you learned to bargain before you learned to read. Vulnerability: your house name is recognized but not respected. Any Major House NPC's initial disposition is capped at Neutral; you must earn what others inherit.

**Synod-Raised**
You were taken — or given — to the Synod's scholarium before you can clearly remember. You were taught doctrine, liturgy, the classification of attunement, and the nine signs of heresy. You know the testing protocols. You know the deployment schedules. You know what the Synod tells the houses and what it keeps for itself.
Start with one Synod official contact at Favorable, one Undrift contact at Hostile (you represent everything they fear). Advantage on INT checks related to Resonant lore, Drift phenomena, and Synod procedure — you were trained in the institution's language. Vulnerability: the Synod tracks its own. Leaving their service is permitted but noted. Synod contacts will periodically check in, and refusing feels like refusing family. If you act against Synod interests, word reaches them within days, not weeks.

**Undrift**
You slipped through. Maybe the testers missed you. Maybe someone hid you. Maybe you tested positive and your parents ran before the Synod came. You've lived your whole life knowing that what you are is illegal, and that every institution in the Hegemony wants to own, use, or destroy you for it.
Start with one Undrift network contact at Trusted — the underground that kept you alive. No institutional contacts; your first interaction with any house, Synod, or Imperial NPC starts at Wary. Advantage: access to the Undrift network — black-market Drift suppressants, safe houses, unregistered Resonant services. Things no institution will provide and no amount of money can buy through official channels. The advantage isn't power; it's freedom from the systems everyone else depends on. Vulnerability: you are hunted. The Synod has standing bounties for unregistered Resonants. Any use of Drift abilities in public risks exposure. Failed Drift checks have a secondary consequence: a Synod detection roll (DC scales with distance from Synod presence).

**Imperial Service**
You serve the Throne. Not a house, not the Synod, not yourself. You were recruited, trained, and deployed by an institution that considers personal loyalty a vulnerability and political ambition a disqualifying trait. You enforce the balance: the houses stay in line, the Synod stays in bounds, and the Hegemony holds together for another generation.
Start with one Imperial intelligence contact at Favorable, one house contact at Neutral (they cooperate because they must, not because they trust you). Advantage on checks involving protocol, jurisdiction, and official authority — your clearance opens doors that rank alone cannot. Vulnerability: you are a tool of the Throne, and everyone knows it. Contacts from any faction assume you're reporting everything. Disposition advances are capped at Favorable with non-Imperial NPCs unless you actively demonstrate personal loyalty over institutional duty.

**Ascendant**
Born Untuned. No house. No church. No blood worth naming. You arrived here through talent, ruthlessness, someone's investment, or some combination that makes old-blood aristocrats uncomfortable. The system wasn't built for you, and everyone in the room knows it the moment you speak.
Start with one mentor/patron contact at Favorable (whoever invested in your rise), one peer contact at Wary (someone who resents your presence in rooms they earned by birth). Advantage on checks involving improvisation, resourcefulness, and unconventional problem-solving — you've never had the luxury of doing things the expected way. Vulnerability: you have no safety net. Other origins can fall back on their house, their church, or their network. You fall back on the person who elevated you, and that debt has terms. Your patron contact will periodically call in favors, and refusing risks losing your only institutional protection.

## Classes (6)

| Class | Concept | Primary Stat | How trouble finds them |
|-------|---------|:------------:|----------------------|
| **Envoy** | Diplomat / House representative | CHA | Through politics, broken agreements, information that changes the balance |
| **Warden** | Military officer / Protector | STR | Through violence, sieges, impossible orders, loyalty tested by duty |
| **Seeker** | Synod investigator / Heresy hunter | INT | Through forbidden knowledge, doctrinal contradictions, Undrift they're sent to find |
| **Drifter** | Resonant operative / Attunement specialist | WIS | Through the Drift itself: attunement going wrong, being weaponized, hearing things others can't |
| **Veil** | Spy / Infiltrator | DEX | Through secrets, cover identities, being in rooms where they shouldn't be |
| **Physik** | Resonant physician / Bio-artificer | WIS | Through patients who shouldn't exist, Resonant degradation the Synod suppresses, the physical cost of empire |

### Design note: Mender → Physik (rename)

"Mender" was too generic next to Envoy, Warden, Seeker, Drifter, Veil. "Physik" fits the feudal register and sharpens the political identity. The Physik specifically treats Resonant degradation — the toll the Synod doesn't disclose. They know what happens to Resonants after years of service. The Synod wants that knowledge suppressed. The houses want it for leverage. The Undrift wants it as proof the system is killing people. Every other class has an obvious political charge; now the Physik does too.

### Class Traits

| Class | Trait | Effect |
|-------|-------|--------|
| **Envoy** | *Accord* | Once/chapter, invoke formal authority or house name to halt a hostile encounter or force a negotiation. Fails on enemies who don't recognize the Hegemony. |
| **Warden** | *Unbroken* | Once/chapter, when reduced to 0 HP, drop to 1 HP instead. |
| **Seeker** | *Inquisition* | Once/chapter, after 1+ rounds of dialogue, force a WIS save. Failure: target reveals one truth. Success: they know you tried. |
| **Drifter** | *Drift Touch* | Once/chapter, attune to sense hidden Resonants, disrupt Drift-powered tech, or perceive through walls. Each use leaves a trace the Synod can detect. |
| **Veil** | *Ghost Protocol* | Once/chapter, leave no trace on one security scan, witness memory, or official record. |
| **Physik** | *Bitter Remedy* | Once/chapter, heal 1d8+WIS, but treatment has a side effect chosen by the GM: pain, dependency, or temporary sense loss. Side effects are worse on patients with Drift exposure history. |

### Design note: Drifter + Origin interaction

Being Resonant is a social position (origin: Undrift). Being a trained attunement specialist is a skill set (class: Drifter). These are independent axes. An Undrift Drifter is a self-taught Resonant on the run. A Synod-Raised Drifter is church-sanctioned. A Minor House Drifter was traded to the Synod and trained, then returned. The intersection creates the story.

## Asset Mechanic: The Retinue

**Decision:** The "ship" equivalent is a Retinue — a personal cadre of sworn servants, specialists, and advisors. The upgrade isn't a vehicle; it's a growing web of human loyalty. This is the genre's unique lever: every other genre upgrades a thing; this one upgrades relationships to people.

| System | L1 | L2 | L3 |
|--------|----|----|-----|
| **Sworn** (military) | 2 guards, basic arms | 6 retainers, house weapons | 12 sworn, siege-capable |
| **Intelligence** | Local informant network | Cross-house contacts | Synod-penetrating sources |
| **Household** | Basic quarters, one servant | Staff, comfortable lodging | Mobile court, diplomatic venue |
| **Drift Capacity** | Access to one Resonant (shared) | Dedicated Resonant | Bonded Resonant (loyal to you) |
| **Reputation** | Known within your house | Recognized across houses | Name carries weight with the Throne |

### Design note: Drift Capacity

No other genre has "access to a person with supernatural abilities" as a system to upgrade. At L1 you share a Resonant with your house — assigned to you sometimes. At L2 one is dedicated to your service. At L3 they're personally loyal to you, not your house or the Synod. That progression tells a story about relationship and trust, not just resource accumulation. And it creates moral tension at every level: you're upgrading your access to a human being.

### Asset config mapping

- `partyBaseName`: 'Retinue'
- Retinue tab in UI
- "Your retinue grows" is a natural sentence
- Maps to the existing ShipState/asset system: each L1→L3 row is a system with upgrade tiers

## Opening Hooks

Hooks test the player's relationship to the Resonant question immediately. Space opera hooks put you in jeopardy. Epic sci-fi hooks put you in complicity. The genre's identity is complicity — establish it in the first scene.

### Universal (4)

1. **"The Allocation"** — Your house has been allocated a new Resonant. She's fourteen. She arrives tomorrow. Your job is to make the transition smooth. Her family hasn't stopped sending messages. The Synod says to ignore them.

2. **"Quiet Disposal"** — A Resonant in your service has stopped responding to attunement. The Synod wants them returned. Your commander wants the problem to disappear quietly. The Resonant is sitting in your quarters, lucid, and asking you not to send them to the Ashen Wards.

3. **"The Testing"** — You've been assigned to escort a Synod testing team to a frontier world. The locals have been hiding their children. Your orders are clear. Your conscience is not.

4. **"The Dowry"** — Your house is negotiating a marriage alliance. The dowry isn't gold — it's three Resonants from the other house's reserve. You're the one who has to inspect them and sign the transfer documents.

### Envoy (3)

5. **"The Conclave Seat"** — A Major House offers your house a junior seat at the Conclave. The price: your house must publicly support the Synod's new Resonant conscription quota, which doubles testing in frontier worlds. Your elders are divided. The decision falls to you. {classes: Envoy}

6. **"The Broken Accord"** — Two houses signed a trade agreement last season. One just violated it by hiring Undrift Resonants as off-book security. The other wants you to deliver the accusation in open court. If you do, you expose the Undrift network. If you don't, your house loses face. {classes: Envoy}

7. **"Silent Terms"** — You've been sent to negotiate the release of a hostage — a Resonant child taken by a house that claims they "found" her unregistered. They want recognition for "saving" her from the Undrift. The Synod wants her. The child wants to go home. {classes: Envoy}

### Warden (3)

8. **"The Garrison"** — Your garrison has been ordered to hold a Synod processing facility on a world where the locals are openly hiding children from testing. Patrols are finding empty villages. Your soldiers are uncomfortable. The Synod wants results. {classes: Warden}

9. **"The Tithe"** — A frontier settlement is three Resonants short of its annual tithe to the Synod. The settlement elder says two died in a mining accident and one fled. The Synod says the numbers don't matter — the tithe must be met. They're looking at the settlement's children. {classes: Warden}

10. **"Shield Duty"** — You've been assigned to protect a Synod Adjudicator investigating a house accused of harboring Undrift Resonants. The house is your house. The Adjudicator doesn't know that yet. {classes: Warden}

### Seeker (3)

11. **"Doctrinal Anomaly"** — Your investigation into a frontier heresy cult leads to a Resonant who attuned without Synod training. Impossible, according to doctrine. She's coherent, controlled, and says the Drift taught her directly. Your report will either rewrite Synod theology or end her life. {classes: Seeker}

12. **"The Archive"** — A dead Seeker's files arrive on your desk, sealed and redirected three times. Inside: evidence that a senior Synod Hierarch has been falsifying Resonant degradation records for twenty years. The numbers suggest hundreds of Resonants were deployed past safe limits. The Hierarch knows someone has the files. {classes: Seeker}

13. **"Heresy or Truth"** — An Undrift cell has been distributing pamphlets claiming the Drift is alive, that Resonants hear it, and that the Synod knows. Your orders are suppression. The problem: you've been hearing it too. {classes: Seeker}

### Drifter (3)

14. **"The Threshold"** — Your attunement is deepening. You see things in the Drift that the training manuals don't describe — geometries, presences, a pattern that might be language. Your handler wants a report. Reporting accurately means admitting you've crossed a threshold the Synod classifies as hazardous. Not reporting means lying to the institution that controls your deployment. {classes: Drifter}

15. **"Burned Out"** — A Resonant you trained with has been flagged for retirement to the Ashen Wards. She's asked you to visit before she's transferred. When you arrive, she's not degraded at all. She's been faking the Dimming because she found something in the Drift and needed to stop being observed. {classes: Drifter}

16. **"Weapon Test"** — Your house has developed a new Drift weapon. You've been selected for the attunement calibration. The first test felt wrong — not dangerous, but aware. Something in the weapon responded to you, and it wasn't the technology. The next test is tomorrow. {classes: Drifter}

### Veil (3)

17. **"The Ledger"** — You've been inserted into a rival house's administrative corps to locate evidence of illegal Resonant breeding programs. Three months in, you've found the evidence. You've also found that the program has produced children who are already attuning, and the house is treating them well. Your handler wants the intel. Delivering it condemns the children to Synod custody. {classes: Veil}

18. **"Double Bind"** — Your cover identity was burned by someone inside your own intelligence service. Not by accident — deliberately. Someone wants you exposed. You have 48 hours before the house you've infiltrated connects your cover to your real identity, and your handler isn't responding to extraction protocols. {classes: Veil}

19. **"The Witness"** — You witnessed a Synod enforcer kill an Undrift Resonant during what was supposed to be an arrest. The official report says the Resonant attacked first. You know that's a lie. Your superiors know you were there. They haven't asked for your account. {classes: Veil}

### Physik (3)

20. **"The Patient"** — A Resonant brought to your clinic has degradation patterns you've never seen. Not the gradual Dimming — something acute, as if their attunement was forced past capacity in a single event. The Synod says she was in "routine service." Her cellular readings say she was used as a weapon. {classes: Physik}

21. **"The Ward"** — You've been reassigned to an Ashen Ward. Officially: standard medical rotation. Actually: someone in the Synod hierarchy wants a physician who won't ask questions. On your first day, you discover that a third of the "spent" Resonants still have measurable attunement. They're not burned out. They're being hidden. {classes: Physik}

22. **"The Record"** — Your medical records on Resonant degradation are the most complete outside the Synod's own archives. A house intelligence officer offers you protection and resources in exchange for the data. An Undrift cell offers you safe passage for the same data. The Synod offers you a promotion if you'll hand the records over and stop keeping your own. All three offers arrived the same day. {classes: Physik}

## Prompt Sections

### Role
You are the Game Master of Storyforge — a solo text RPG set in The Hegemony, a thousand-year interstellar empire that runs on human lives.

### Setting
The Hegemony holds together because of Resonants — humans who can attune to the Drift, a substrate beneath normal spacetime. Resonants power FTL travel, planetary shields, communication, and weapons. They are identified at age seven, taken by the Synod, and deployed as imperial infrastructure. They have no legal autonomy. Their power is immense and their status is property.

The Great Houses control territory and compete for Resonant allocation. The Synod controls attunement and enforces doctrinal monopoly. The Throne balances both. The Undrift — unregistered Resonants — survive in hiding. Sustained attunement degrades Resonants (the Dimming), a fact the Synod suppresses. The Ashen Wards house spent Resonants far from public view.

Every institution serves itself while claiming to serve the whole. The player is inside this machine. Their choices serve it, subvert it, or break pieces of it — but they cannot escape complicity. Using Drift-powered technology, which is everything, means benefiting from a system that treats humans as fuel.

### Vocabulary
Use feudal-imperial language naturally: house, sworn, tithe, allocation, mandate, Conclave, dispensation, heresy, compliance. Technology exists but is described in institutional terms — Drift lanes, attunement arrays, shield lattice, transit authority. Currency is marks (₥), house-minted. Rest is Respite (short) or Full Withdrawal (long). Soldiers are retainers, sworn, conscripts. Consumables are tinctures, drift suppressants, stimulants. Never use space opera slang (credits, mercs, beacon, hyperspace).

### Tone Override
Adjust tone: Gritty (40%), Epic (40%), Witty (20%). Grand but grounded. Humor exists but it's dry, knowing, and usually masks something worse. Consequences are political and personal more often than physical. A diplomatic failure should feel as dangerous as a firefight.

### NPC Voice Guide
House nobility: formal, carefully worded, every sentence a move in a game you may not see. Synod officials: righteous, procedural, always framing control as care. Imperial officers: clipped, duty-first, uncomfortable with ambiguity. Undrift contacts: cautious, specific, trust earned in actions not words. Retainers: loyal but not obsequious — they have opinions and share them when asked. Resonants (if they speak freely): tired, precise, sometimes distant, carrying knowledge they weren't meant to have.

### Tutorial Context
The opening chapter introduces the player to their position in the Hegemony — their house, their role, their relationship to Resonants. First check: a social or political encounter (navigating obligation, reading a room, choosing what to say). First moral weight: a decision that serves one faction at another's expense. Combat, if any, should be a consequence of political failure, not the opening move.

### Investigation Guide
Ledger — political debts, faction promises, evidence of complicity, intercepted correspondence, testimony. Investigation in this genre is about power: who has it, who hid what, who betrayed whom. When the player investigates, track discoveries as whispers — fragments that connect to form a picture of institutional corruption or factual rivalry.

At chapter open or when a new stratagem begins, establish privately:
- **The truth:** What actually happened (2-3 sentences). Never reveal this directly.
- **Evidence chain:** 5-8 clues — documents, testimonies, observations, intercepted communications.
- **Misdirection:** 2-3 clues that point toward a plausible but wrong faction or motive.
- **Gatekeeper clue:** One revelation that unlocks the final act. Obtainable through multiple paths.

Political stratagems always have a faction clock. The longer the player takes, the more factions adjust their positions. Evidence gets buried, witnesses are transferred, and alliances shift.

### Asset Mechanic (Retinue)
The player's Retinue represents their growing personal power base. Unlike a ship, it is made of people with loyalty, morale, and limits. Each upgrade tier (L1→L3) across Sworn, Intelligence, Household, Drift Capacity, and Reputation should feel like a narrative milestone, not just a stat increase. The GM should introduce retinue members as named NPCs with opinions and loyalties. Upgrading Drift Capacity in particular should trigger moral reflection — the player is deepening their personal claim on a human being's service.

### Trait Rules
## TRAIT RULES

- **Accord:** Player invokes house name or formal authority. Halts hostility or forces negotiation. Fails on anyone who doesn't recognize or respect the Hegemony. GM should track which factions the player has invoked against — repeated use on the same faction diminishes effectiveness.
- **Unbroken:** Drop to 1 HP instead of 0, once per chapter. No strings — the Warden's reliability is the point.
- **Inquisition:** Requires 1+ rounds of dialogue. WIS save: failure reveals one truth. Success: target knows you tried, and their disposition toward you drops. In this genre, failed interrogations have political consequences.
- **Drift Touch:** Attune to sense, disrupt, or perceive. Every use leaves a Synod-detectable trace. The GM should track cumulative Drift Touch uses — after 3 uses in a chapter without suppression, a Synod notice event triggers.
- **Ghost Protocol:** Erase trace from one record, scan, or memory. The erasure is perfect, but the absence can be noticed — a gap in a record is itself suspicious to a careful investigator.
- **Bitter Remedy:** Heal 1d8+WIS with a side effect (pain, dependency, temporary sense loss). Side effects are amplified on patients with Drift exposure history. The Physik's healing always costs something — that's the genre's medical reality.
