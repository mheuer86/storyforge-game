HARROW SPUR — DOCKING ARM 4, CLAMP KIOSK
The strip-light above kiosk seven flickers twice, catches, holds. It's been doing that since you docked — three days ago, when the hold first appeared in your queue notification and you told yourself it was a processing error.

It is not a processing error.

The display reads 40,000 CR — ADMINISTRATIVE HOLD — DOCKING ARM 4 — CLAMP ACTIVE. Behind the scratched observation panel to your left, the Driftrunner sits exactly where you left her: sealed, grounded, going nowhere. The clamp housing on her forward strut has a green status light. Green means locked.

Behind the kiosk window, Port Assessor Coll — Harrow Spur's procedural face for everything involving clamp authority on arm four — pulls up a second screen without looking at you. She's mid-fifties, uniform pressed out of habit rather than pride, and she has the particular stillness of someone who has explained fee codes to desperate people every day for twenty years and stopped feeling the weight of it around year three.

"Hold reference four-dash-seven-nine-one." She reads it aloud even though you didn't ask. "Administrative. Forty thousand, two hundred credits. Fee cycle refreshes—" she checks the second screen— "in approximately four hours."

Your comm has one unread message. Flagged urgent. Sender listed as The Sable Margin.

You haven't opened it yet.

Ask Coll what 'administrative hold' actually means — who filed it and under what authority. [Investigation]
Coll doesn't look up from the second screen right away. One finger moves across a physical keypad — a habit, not a need — and then she pulls the main display half-around so you can see it from your side of the counter.

"Administrative hold." She reads it the way she'd read a transit manifest: flat, no inflection. "Filed under Port Authority Ordinance 7.2, sub-clause C. That's a general commercial lien. Means someone with filing standing submitted a claim against the vessel before departure clearance could process."

Her finger taps the screen twice, scrolling to a sub-header. "Who filed it. That's in the originating-party line. Right here." She tilts the screen another ten degrees. "Name, filing timestamp, reference jurisdiction. All there."

The text is dense — fee codes stacked on fee codes, the kind of document designed to be skimmed past. You're going to have to actually read it.

HARROW SPUR — DOCKING ARM 4, CLAMP KIOSK
Read the Sable Margin's message — the broker knew the number, and they knew it before the fee cycle opened.
You turn away from the counter and pull out your comm.

The message is from an address you don't recognize — a routing string that dead-ends if you try to trace it back. But the header reads The Sable Margin, and the timestamp reads 0209 this shift.

Eight minutes before Veyne Logistics filed the clamp.

The message itself is short:

---

Driftrunner — you have a hold problem. Forty thousand, two hundred. We have a solution. Cargo-side corridor, Arm 4, before the fee cycle refreshes. Come alone. — L.V.

---

The number is exact. Not forty thousand — forty thousand, two hundred. The same figure Coll read off the hold file without blinking.

Lira Voss knew the number before the hold was public record. Before the fee cycle had even opened. Before, technically, you'd had any reason to stand at this kiosk reading Veyne Logistics filing codes at whatever hour this is.

Behind you, Coll's keypad makes two soft clicks. Routine entry — her second screen, same as always. She hasn't moved.

The cargo-side corridor is thirty meters down the arm. The fee cycle refresh is four hours out. And someone built this trap with enough precision to quote you the exact number before the jaws closed.
