# Covenant Fantasy Starter Rework Map

Status: implemented in `content/sf2/campaign-briefs/fantasy/covenant.md`.

Target file: `content/sf2/campaign-briefs/fantasy/covenant.md`

## Goal

Rework The Covenant starter toward:

- more deep-lore wonder in the vein of ancient metaphysics, contradictory histories, ruins, and old cultures
- more DnD-style creature play through bespoke recurring monsters with ecology, tactics, and motives
- preserving the starter's existing fantasy-adventure promise: wonder-forward, heroic, propulsive, curious, magical

This should not turn The Covenant into Grimdark or Pale Flame. The Covenant remains about a capable first-posting mage entering a magical problem where curiosity, competence, and heroism matter.

## Chosen Shape: Deep-Lore Wonder Plus Bespoke Monster Ecology

The rework should layer beneath the existing starter rather than replacing it.

Current Covenant spine:

> A junior Covenant mage arrives in Ashford because the Silence expanded, three children vanished, and a seal under Treswick is being broken.

Reworked Covenant spine:

> A junior Covenant mage arrives in Ashford because the Silence expanded, three children vanished, and a seal under Treswick is being broken. The seal does not merely contain a spell fragment. It is part of an older law-architecture that other cultures remember differently, and something adapted to the Silence is feeding on the breach.

## Section-by-Section Change Map

### 1. Header and Opening Promise

Current:

- "DnD adventurer"
- "Wonder-forward, propulsive, the world as a place to explore"
- "Something in the Silence is moving toward the town"

Change:

- Keep the DnD adventurer register.
- Add one sentence making the deeper weird explicit:
  - "The old world did not leave ruins. It left instructions, and some of them still obey."
- Make "something in the Silence" more concrete but still mysterious:
  - "Something in the Silence has learned to move where magic cannot."

Why:

- Signals the Elder-Scrolls-like layer immediately.
- Keeps the core adventure hook intact.

### 2. GM Style

Keep:

- Wonder first
- Curiosity rewarded
- Movement and momentum
- Heroism possible
- Dynamic combat
- Dice before response

Add two GM style notes:

#### Deep Lore Is Playable

Lore should not arrive as exposition. It should arrive as:

- inscriptions that disagree with Covenant doctrine
- local names for the same phenomenon
- ruins that still enforce an old rule
- monster behavior that reveals what the old rule was for

#### Monsters Are Discoverable

Creatures are not hit point bags. Every major creature has:

- habitat
- desire
- hunting method
- tell
- weakness
- what a Discovery Roll reveals
- one spell or clever cast that can change the fight

Why:

- Converts "more Elder Scrolls" and "more DnD monsters" into GM-operable behavior.

### 3. The World - The Sundered Kingdoms

Current world cause:

- Varek tried to rewrite reality.
- The Unmaking shattered magic into Surges and Silences.

Add a pre-Unmaking layer:

#### The First Architectures

Before the Five Kingdoms, before Varek, and before the Covenant, several cultures understood magic as law rather than force. Covenant scholars call their remains the First Architectures.

They did not all agree:

- Aurenian scholars say they were pre-kingdom engineers.
- Thornwood Weavers say they were oath-bound spirits wearing human shape.
- Iron Coast sailors say their towers were built to keep things below the sea asleep.
- Covenant doctrine says they are dangerous but classifiable Old Magic.

The truth should stay plural at first. Each tradition knows one useful piece and one wrong thing.

Concrete old-law fragments to seed:

- Permanence: what refuses to die, decay, or change
- Name: what binds a thing to itself
- Distance: what makes near and far meaningful
- Shape: what lets bodies remain bodies
- Hunger: what all living systems owe the world

Why:

- Gives ruins and lore that "deep time" feeling.
- Makes Varek's permanence fragment part of a larger structure, not a one-off macguffin.

### 4. Surges and Silences

Keep:

- Surges overcharge magic.
- Silences deaden magic.
- Ashford's Silence grew too fast.

Add:

#### Silences Are Not Empty

The Covenant teaches that Silences are magic-dead zones. That is mostly true. But some Old Magic predates the current magical field and does not behave like modern spells. Inside a Silence, modern magic weakens, but old law may become easier to perceive.

Operational rules:

- Covenant spells fail or degrade.
- Old-law inscriptions may become clearer.
- Some creatures are sluggish in magic-rich areas but strong in Silences.
- A mage can still reason, observe, improvise, and use the environment.

Why:

- Makes Silences scary without making the mage useless.
- Creates gameplay where spell choice, artifacts, and discovery matter.

### 5. Magic in the World

Keep the four approaches:

- Covenant Craft
- Thornwood Weaving
- Wild Magic
- Old Magic

Refine Old Magic:

Old Magic is not just stronger spellcasting. It is interaction with laws that current magic sits on top of. It can:

- bind a name to a door
- make a wound permanent
- teach a road to remember where it goes
- let a body survive without the thing that should sustain it
- allow creatures to persist where modern magic cannot

Why:

- Gives the starter more distinct fantasy identity.
- Makes discovery rolls richer than "identify spell effect."

### 6. The Covenant

Keep:

- The Covenant trains, watches, and holds.
- The Covenant is responsible but incomplete.
- The mage is newly sworn and capable.

Add a blind spot:

The Covenant is excellent at post-Unmaking magic. It is less excellent at interpreting cultures that understood magic before the current field existed. Its categories are useful enough to save lives and incomplete enough to get a junior mage into trouble.

Change "It holds":

Current:

- The Covenant maintains seals on Varek's remnants.

Rework:

- The Covenant believes it maintains seals on Varek's remnants.
- Some seals are older than Varek and were repurposed after the Unmaking.
- Varek may have found a system, not invented one.

Why:

- This is the Elder-Scrolls-like move: history becomes contested and layered.

### 7. Discovery Roll

Keep current table.

Add "Triangulated Discovery":

When investigating old sites, the mage can compare:

- Covenant doctrine
- local testimony
- Thornwood/Iron Coast/folk tradition
- physical ruin evidence
- creature behavior

Beating the DC by 5+ should give not just "more facts," but a contradiction the mage can use.

Example:

- Covenant doctrine says the Silence suppresses all magical creatures.
- The tracks show a creature moved better inside the Silence.
- Practical implication: this thing is not using modern magic as metabolism.

Why:

- Makes lore actionable.

### 8. Spellbook Finds

Keep:

- Spellbook finds are treasure.
- Old Magic can be copied but may behave differently.

Add:

Old Magic finds may be:

- a full spell
- a broken theorem
- a ritual diagram
- a naming key
- a creature countermeasure
- a law-fragment the mage cannot cast yet but can recognize later

Why:

- Opens Elder-Scrolls-style artifact discovery without flooding the first session with loot.

### 9. New Section: Creatures of Surge, Silence, and Old Law

Add a compact bestiary philosophy before Ashford or after Magic.

Core rule:

> Monsters exist because magic changed ecology. Some are natural creatures altered by Surges. Some are old-law remnants. Some are intelligent enough to want things.

Three starter creature categories:

#### Surge-Touched

Living animals or plants altered by magic-rich zones.

- Wonder-forward, colorful, physical
- Good for early adventure encounters
- Examples: glass-antler deer, lantern moth swarms, bramble hounds that root after death

#### Silence-Adapted

Creatures that survive where magic fails.

- Sparse, unsettling, tactical
- Good for Ashford and Silent Reaches
- Examples: hush-wolves that hunt by vibration, pale root-things that mimic footsteps, creatures that freeze when named

#### Old-Law Intelligences

Rare intelligent entities that predate current magical categories.

- Campaign-scale threats or allies
- Need specific motives and rules
- The mind-flayer-like slot lives here

### 10. New Recurring Monster: The Mnemonauts

IP-safe replacement for mind-flayer-like pressure.

Working name: Mnemonauts.

What they are:

- Intelligent old-law predators that navigate by memory, name, and prepared spell-patterns.
- They do not eat brains. They feed on coherent identity: names, rehearsed spells, oaths, and memories with emotional weight.
- They are half-starved in magic-rich places and strong in Silences.

Why they exist:

- They may have been created by the First Architectures as custodians, executioners, or navigators.
- Or they may be what came through when the First Architectures made doors too far down.
- The starter should not answer this immediately.

Behavior:

- They avoid crowds and loud sensory chaos.
- They isolate one mind.
- They repeat a victim's own memories back with one detail wrong.
- They prefer mages because prepared spells are structured, nourishing memory.

Tactics:

- Steal a prepared spell for one scene.
- Induce false familiarity: "you know this corridor."
- Make allies misremember a direction, name, or promise.
- Create a silence-within-Silence where speech feels too heavy.

Tells:

- Repeated phrases slightly misquoted.
- A person forgets a proper noun but remembers the emotion.
- Written notes remain correct while memory drifts.
- Animals refuse to cross a threshold even after the Silence passes.

Weaknesses:

- New names freely chosen.
- Written records made before contact.
- Contradictory testimony spoken aloud by multiple people.
- Spells cast from improvisation rather than rote pattern.
- Noise, bells, running water, or other phenomena that break coherent thought.

Discovery Roll outputs:

- Meet DC: this is not a normal Silence effect; it interacts with memory.
- Beat DC: it feeds on structured identity and spell-patterns.
- Beat DC by 5+: the mage can protect one target by anchoring them with a fresh name, written proof, or improvised spellcasting.

Why:

- Gives us a mind-flayer-style recurring monster with a distinctive Covenant identity.

### 11. Ashford's People

Keep current NPCs.

Small additions:

#### Healer Ossith

Her theory is partially right because she has seen memory symptoms among Treswick evacuees:

- people forget names
- a child remembers a song with wrong words
- a farmer insists the eastern road was always crooked

#### Old Verath

His amulet protected him from the Silence, but it also protected his name. He may have forgotten something else in exchange.

#### Maret

Maret's attunement does not only accelerate the permanence seal. It makes her legible to whatever is feeding near the breach.

Why:

- Keeps the existing cast, deepens their function.

### 12. What the GM Knows

Current:

- "What's in the Silence: Not a creature."
- Seal fragment is permanence.
- Sable Thorn is breaking it.
- Children are alive.

Rework:

#### What's in the Silence

Not a single creature and not a natural expansion. The Silence has grown because Sable Thorn is breaking a seal under Treswick. But the breach has also attracted a Mnemonaut, or awakened one already caught in the old architecture.

Sable Thorn believes she is working with a fragment of permanence. She does not understand the intelligence feeding at the edge of her work.

#### The children

Still alive. Still in the chamber. Maret still has her hand on the seal.

Add:

- Pip has forgotten Dall's name twice.
- Dall remembers the route out, but only if no one asks directly.
- Maret can hear a voice that sounds like her own older self.

#### Sable Thorn

Keep her human, sympathetic, and dangerous.

Add:

- Sable's notes contain gaps she does not notice.
- She has repeated one sentence in three different letters with one noun changed.
- The Mnemonaut may be using her theory as a path.

Why:

- Sable remains the antagonist, but not the whole explanation.

### 13. First Session Shape

Implementation note: the starter now uses six beats so the first encounter has room before the chamber.

Keep the existing opening spine.

Modify beat 4:

Current:

- Into the Silence: exploration sequence, wrongness, something moving.

Rework:

- Into the Silence: exploration sequence, wrongness, old-law traces, memory drift, something that reacts to prepared spells rather than sound.

Split the original beat 5:

Current:

- Chamber, children, seal, Sable arrives.

Rework:

- Chamber, children, seal, Maret's attunement, first clear sign that something is feeding through the breach. Sable arrives convinced she can control it.

Chapter 1 close target:

Close when the mage has done most of:

- reached the chamber
- stabilized or rescued the children
- learned the seal is older/more complex than expected
- identified Sable Thorn as a human pressure
- seen evidence of a Silence-adapted intelligence

Do not require resolving the Mnemonaut. That is successor-chapter material.

## Open Questions

1. Keep the name "Mnemonauts" or choose a more fantasy-forward name?
   - Alternatives: Name-Eaters, The Hollow Choir, Silence-Lords, Thought-Lectors, The Remembered.

2. How explicit should Chapter 1 be?
   - Decision: first encounter in Chapter 1.
   - Shape: not a boss fight, and not the full intelligence revealed. Use a lesser Silence-adapted manifestation or servant that demonstrates the ecology.
   - Purpose: prove the Silence contains an active predator, teach one actionable weakness, and make the mage's spell choices matter before the seal chamber.

3. Should Sable Thorn know about the intelligence?
   - No: she is being used by it.
   - Partly: she thinks it is a tool.
   - Yes: she believes alliance with it is worth the cost.

## Recommendation

Use encounter-level explicitness without turning Chapter 1 into a monster hunt:

- Chapter 1 should include a first creature encounter.
- The encounter should be a lesser expression of the Mnemonaut ecology, not the campaign-scale intelligence itself.
- The mage should rescue or stabilize the children and understand the seal is not only a Covenant problem.
- The monster ecology should appear through memory drift, a discoverable tell, and one non-boss Silence-adapted creature.
- Sable Thorn should not fully understand what she is enabling.

That keeps Ashford playable, preserves the human antagonist, and opens Chapter 2 on richer ground.

## Chapter 1 Encounter Shape

Working creature: Mnemonaut Echo.

This is not a full Mnemonaut. It is a shed hunting pattern or memory-parasite left in the Silence by the larger intelligence.

Where it appears:

- During "Into the Silence," before the chamber.
- The mage and any companions hear a remembered sentence spoken in the wrong voice.
- Tracks loop back over themselves even when the party walked straight.
- One child briefly answers from ahead, but the voice uses a phrase the child could not know.

What it wants:

- To separate the mage from companions.
- To taste prepared spell-patterns.
- To delay rescue long enough for Maret to keep touching the seal.

Tactical behavior:

- Opens by stealing or blurring one prepared spell name for the scene, not removing all casting.
- Creates a false route or false child-voice.
- Avoids direct damage at first; it pressures orientation, concentration, and trust.
- If forced into form, appears as a person-shaped absence filled with remembered voices.

Discovery Roll:

- Meet DC: this is not the Silence alone; something is shaping memory and direction.
- Beat DC: it reacts to prepared spell names and rehearsed mental patterns.
- Beat DC by 5+: fresh improvisation, written anchors, bells/running water/noise, or a newly chosen name can disrupt it.

Spell/Clever Cast affordances:

- Divination: identify the false memory route.
- Abjuration: anchor one person's name or memory against alteration.
- Illusion: feed it a decoy pattern.
- Evocation: force it into visible outline with sudden sensory overload.
- Conjuration: mark the true path with created matter that memory drift cannot edit.
- Transmutation: alter the terrain cue so the false loop stops matching.

Outcome:

- Success: the mage reaches the chamber with one clear weakness and one unnerving proof that the Silence is inhabited.
- Costly success: someone loses a name, prepared spell, route memory, or emotional memory until treated.
- Failure: the mage still reaches the chamber, but late, disoriented, or with the Echo attached as a successor pressure.

Chapter close implication:

- Chapter 1 can close after the chamber if the children are stabilized/rescued, Sable Thorn has arrived or been identified, and the Echo encounter has established the nonhuman pressure.
- Do not require defeating or explaining the full Mnemonaut intelligence in Chapter 1.
