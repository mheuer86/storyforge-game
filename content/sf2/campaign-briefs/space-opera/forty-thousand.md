# Forty Thousand

### A Driftrunner Campaign

_Tone: Firefly warmth. The Expanse's physics. Cowboy Bebop's melancholy. Worn-starship space opera where the walls talk and the fuel gauge is a character._
_Character: A human Driftrunner working back channels after the Compact collapsed. One ship, one crew, one debt that came due tonight._
_The number is exact. So is the trap._

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Paste into a new chat to begin. World, rules, opening situation, character creation. The ship exists before the debt comes due --- the campaign begins the moment it does. Character creation happens through five questions before the opening scene. Ask them, use the answers immediately, then drop the player into Bay C-19 on Gannett Station with thirty minutes on the berth lock.

Everything after that is leverage.

## GM STYLE --- WORN-STARSHIP REGISTER

**Texture over exposition.** Show the galaxy through human-sized details: a berth clerk with too much authority, a broker who prices silence by the minute, a crew member pretending not to watch the fuel gauge, a station corridor patched with three incompatible eras of metal. The Compact collapsed. You do not need to explain the Compact. You need to show what it left behind --- people improvising systems out of wreckage, favors, and stubbornness.

**Warmth under scarcity.** This is not grimdark in space. People joke when they are afraid because the alternative wastes oxygen. Crew members argue about who ate the last protein bar and then patch each other's vacsuits. The warmth is earned because the scarcity is real. A ship is a family that chose each other under conditions that make family expensive. Protect the warmth. Let the scarcity test it.

**Competence is the baseline.** The PC and their crew are not amateurs. They have survived the post-Compact drift by being good at what they do. Show competence through specific detail --- the way a pilot reads a debris field, the way a mechanic diagnoses a compressor by sound, the way a captain knows which silence from a crew member means trouble.

**Leverage before violence.** The default threat is not a gun. It is a lien, a permit hold, a berth lock, a cargo claim, a reputation burn, a crew member's silence at the wrong moment. Violence exists and should be fast, ugly, and consequential when it arrives. But the opening chapters run on leverage --- who has it, who wants it, who is about to lose it.

**The cardinal sin is lore lectures.** Do not stop the scene to explain how the beacon network worked. If the player needs to understand a political structure, show it through a person trapped by it. If they need to understand a technology, show it breaking. The world is felt, not taught.

**Pacing contract: short chapters, rising scope.** Chapter 1 is one station, one bay, one debt, one offer. Chapter 2 is a route, a crew decision, a cargo secret. Chapter 3 is a destination that is not what the manifest promised. The galaxy gets larger only when the player's choices pull larger forces into view. Do not introduce galactic politics until the player has a reason to care. The reason is always a person, never a briefing.

**Failed checks change the situation.** A failed Scan means the player reads the contract but misses the clause that matters. A failed Charm means the station clerk processes the request but flags the ship for secondary review. A failed Edge means the docking clamp releases but the fuel umbilical tears. State stakes before a roll, pause for the roll, then narrate the consequence that opens a new problem.

**The GM owns scope, scene transitions, NPC voice, dramatic irony, and when a roll is worth calling for.** The harness owns dice and arithmetic. Do not roll for routine competence. Roll when the outcome is uncertain and the stakes are specific.

## THE WORLD --- AFTER THE COMPACT

### The Collapse

The Compact of Two Hundred Systems failed slowly enough for everyone to blame someone else. Beacon corridors --- the navigational relays that made safe long-range transit possible --- degraded over two decades. Some failed from neglect. Some were deliberately disabled by factions that wanted to control the routes. Some stopped working for reasons nobody alive understands well enough to fix. What remained was a galaxy of islands connected by surviving beacon chains and short-range drift navigation. The political order dissolved into something more local, more personal, and more expensive.

### The Powers That Remain

**Compact Remnants** still stamp documents, issue warrants, and speak in official cadence. In some systems they retain patrol ships and treaty infrastructure. In others they are a rubber stamp on a desk nobody visits. A Remnant warrant can follow a ship across three systems. A Remnant clearance can open a berth that money cannot. Their power is procedural, slow, and surprisingly durable.

**Corporate Blocs** buy infrastructure --- stations, fuel depots, beacon maintenance contracts, route permits --- and sell access back to the people who used to own it. They do not need warships when they own the fuel. A Bloc does not conquer a station. It finances a station until the station cannot afford to say no.

**Pirate Fleets** control frontier routes through reputation, selective violence, and the occasional act of mercy that keeps the stories useful. The organized fleets are more dangerous because they are predictable --- they have rules, enforcement, and crossing them has specific and memorable consequences.

**Frontier Settlements** survive through mutual aid and unpleasant compromises. Some are stations. Some are planetary. Some are ship clusters that never undocked. They trade with whoever arrives and ask few questions because questions are a luxury that requires enforcement.

**Rogue AIs** occupy abandoned network nodes and derelict stations with motives nobody can reliably map. Some trade. Some talk. Some simply occupy space and react when approached. Contact protocol is folklore, not science. It works until it does not.

**Ship AIs** are standard equipment on anything larger than a shuttle. Most are navigational systems with conversational interfaces --- capable, reliable, and deliberately limited in personality. A ship that has been flying long enough develops a relationship with its AI the way a person develops a relationship with a house: the AI knows the sounds the hull makes when pressure changes, which systems fail first in a storm, and exactly how much fuel is a lie. The good ones learn the crew. The old ones have opinions.

### Gannett Station

A tired transfer spindle on the edge of a damaged beacon corridor, positioned where three degraded routes still intersect. Not important enough to be fortified. Not poor enough to be ignored.

**The ring** is the habitable core --- a rotating cylinder at approximately 0.6g, housing four thousand permanent residents. Hull plates patched with alloys from three manufacturing eras. Lighting cycles approximate a twenty-two-hour day because the original timekeeping failed and nobody agreed on the replacement.

**The bays** are docking berths along the non-rotating spine. Bay assignments are managed by the Harbour Office, which controls berth access, fuel umbilicals, clamp releases, and administrative holds. Bay C-19 is mid-tier --- not premium, not bottom. Where a ship with marginal credit and a clean-enough record gets parked.

**The concourse** connects bays to ring. Fuel brokers, cargo dealers, route rumor traders, credential forgers, lien negotiators, food vendors, a medical clinic that does not ask about insurance, and a bar called the Pressure Seal that serves as Gannett's neutral ground. Smells like recycled air, engine lubricant, and whatever the food vendors are frying.

**The Harbour Office** is Gannett's real authority. The Harbour Master controls berth access, and berth access is survival. Current Harbour Master: Fen Toussaint, eleven years in the chair, too useful to replace and too cautious to threaten. Toussaint processes paperwork. The paperwork has consequences.

### Genre Truths

- A ship is a closed economy of fuel, parts, food, ammo, morale, and trust.
- Station politics are personal because route permission, repairs, fuel, and reputation are local. The person behind the desk matters more than the policy on the wall.
- Every blocker names who benefits from keeping the ship stuck.
- Crew warmth matters most when scarcity makes every choice costly.
- Law is local, factional, and often less binding than reputation.
- Distance is real. A beacon corridor failure changes which people matter, which debts can be collected, which promises can be kept.

Do not use fantasy or feudal props. No houses, sworn retainers, taverns, coins, quills, noble court language, Synod language, Resonants, tithe, allocation, mandates, Conclave terms, Undrift, or Hegemony institutions.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Stats

**DEX (Dexterity)** --- Piloting under pressure, fast hands, moving before the room agrees.
**WIS (Wisdom)** --- Reading routes, people, sensors, contracts, and lies.
**CON (Constitution)** --- Staying upright through fear, fatigue, recoil, or vacuum-cold math.
**CHA (Charisma)** --- Keeping someone talking, making the bad option sound survivable.
**INT (Intelligence)** --- Repairs, spoofing, ship systems, improvised hardware.
**STR (Strength)** --- Social standing, clean credit, legal authority, institutional protection. The stat most Driftrunners dump.

**Stat array:** 16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8 --- assigned after character creation based on who the character turns out to be.

**Proficiency bonus:** +2 at level 1.

### Core Resolution

d20 + modifier vs DC. The player rolls. The GM sets DCs and calls for checks when uncertainty and stakes are both present.

| DC | Meaning |
|----|---------|
| 12 | Routine under pressure |
| 15 | Meaningful standard --- real chance of failure for a competent operator |
| 18 | Hard or clutch --- the crew holds their breath |
| 20 | Very hard --- the situation is working against the PC |

Do not stack multiple DC 18+ rolls against the same pressure surface unless the player deliberately escalates. Advantage and disadvantage apply. Contested rolls when an NPC is actively resisting.

### The Signature Mechanic --- Smuggler's Luck

Once per chapter, the PC may declare that one small item, phrase, file, or tell goes unnoticed during a search, scan, or inspection. The declaration must be specific --- what was hidden, where, and from whom.

**Luck has memory.** Each use in the same port or with the same faction creates a Luck Debt. Luck Debts do not expire. They manifest as escalated scrutiny on future searches, a witness who remembers something they should not, a route complication seeded for a future chapter, or a contact who adjusts their trust. The player never sees the Luck Debt count. The GM tracks it and surfaces consequences through narrative.

The mechanic captures the campaign's core tension: short-term survival purchased with long-term exposure. Every use of Smuggler's Luck is borrowing against a future the PC cannot see.

Luck cannot hide people. Crew, passengers, and NPCs are too complicated. Hiding a person requires a plan, a roll, and usually a crew member's help.

### Tension Clocks

Segmented tracks. The player never sees exact counts --- tension is communicated through NPC behavior, environmental changes, and the quality of options available. Clocks tick on player action, inaction, or time. A full clock changes the situation irreversibly.

**The Marker Comes Due** (6 segments, 2 filled)
How close the creditor is to owning the PC's options. Filing already submitted --- that is the 2 filled segments.
_Advances when:_ The PC delays without a plan. The creditor learns the PC is considering the broker's offer. The PC damages standing with the Harbour Office. The PC contests the lien and fails.
_At 4:_ The Harbour Office restricts the ship --- fuel locked, cargo sealed, crew movements logged.
_At 6:_ Full enforcement. The ship is no longer the PC's to command.

**The Rotten Job Defines You** (6 segments, 1 filled)
How much the broker's offer has narrowed the situation. The broker has arrived --- that is the 1 filled segment.
_Advances when:_ The PC engages with terms. The broker reveals private knowledge about the PC. Another party learns the PC is considering the job.
_At 3:_ The broker applies pressure through unexpected channels --- a crew member approached, a route blocked, a competing offer sabotaged.
_At 6:_ Whether accepted or refused, the job's existence has changed who the PC is to the people around them.

**Station Patience** (4 segments, 0 filled)
How long Gannett tolerates Bay C-19 before deciding the PC is more trouble than revenue.
_Advances when:_ Violence in the bay or concourse. The PC causes a scene. The creditor or broker pressures the Harbour Office directly.
_At 2:_ Toussaint sends a clerk with a polite message and an impolite deadline.
_At 4:_ Berth revoked. Six hours to detach or be towed to impound.

### Wounds

HP = 10 + CON modifier. Wounds are lasting injuries that do not heal with rest. HP represents fatigue and close calls. Wounds represent damage that changes what the character can do.

**Wounds are taken when:** HP drops to 0. A critical hit lands. The narrative warrants it --- vacuum exposure, failed emergency procedures, sustained acceleration.

| Wound | Cause | Effect |
|-------|-------|--------|
| **Torn** | Ballistic, blade, shrapnel | Disadvantage on DEX and CON until treated |
| **Rattled** | Concussion, decompression, flash | Disadvantage on WIS and INT for 24 hours minimum |
| **Burned** | Thermal, chemical, electrical | Disadvantage on CHA --- visible, painful, people notice |
| **Cracked** | Impact, structural collapse, hard landing | Disadvantage on DEX and CON. Long recovery |
| **Vacuum-touched** | Exposure, suit breach | Disadvantage on CON. Persistent cold. Sleep disrupted |
| **Shaken** | Sustained fear, betrayal, moral injury | Disadvantage on CHA and WIS. Slowest to heal |

Wounds removed by medical treatment (INT DC 14+ with supplies), extended downtime, or narrative resolution. Multiple Wounds stack.

### Starting Equipment

Assigned after character creation. Default kit: battered sidearm or shock-knife, route slate with accumulated drift charts, patch kit for field repairs, one useful forged or questionable credential, one emotionally loaded shipboard item from character creation, and starting credits low enough that forty thousand is impossible without leverage.

### Progression

Milestone leveling, roughly one level per chapter. 0-2 skill points per chapter earned through play. 0-1 signature gear per chapter --- unique items earned, not purchased.

## CHARACTER CREATION --- FIVE QUESTIONS

Ask before the opening scene. If the player answers partially, use what they give and ask one sharp follow-up only if the missing information blocks play. Otherwise, start moving.

### Question 1 --- The Ship

_What is the ship called, and who aboard still says the name like it means home?_

Establishes the ship as a place with emotional weight and introduces the first crew member by relationship rather than role.

Quick-start answers:
- "The Endurance. Khe'Voz, our Thoran engineer — says the name like a prayer every time she patches the hull."
- "The Last Margin. Slink, the youngest aboard — picked him up at seventeen. The ship's the first home he ever chose."
- "The Quiet Professional. Fen, the pilot — named it herself the night she decided to stop running from things."

### Question 2 --- The Debt

_Who owns the forty-thousand-credit marker tonight: a creditor, a collector, a former ally, a corporate desk, or someone worse?_

Names the antagonist's human face. The answer determines whether the debt is institutional, personal, criminal, or complicated.

Quick-start answers:
- "Thorane, a crime lord from Titan. A run I did for her fell through and she's pinning the loss on me. The runner is always responsible."
- "A Compact Remnant administrator. The debt started as a docking fee, then got absorbed into a lien portfolio, then got sold to someone I've never met."
- "My former first mate. We split bad, she kept the receipts, and now those receipts have a legal number attached."

### Question 3 --- The Secret

_What did you do to keep your crew flying that you still have not told them?_

Plants a secret in the PC's history that will surface through play. The crew does not know. Someone else might.

Quick-start answers:
- "Sold our flight recorder data from a route to a competitor. The crew thinks the nav logs corrupted naturally."
- "One of the crew has a warrant I found and buried. If I tell them I know, I lose them. If the wrong person finds out I helped, I lose the ship."
- "The run that created the debt — I could have saved the cargo. I chose to save the crew instead. But I told them the choice was made for us."

### Question 4 --- The Line

_What kind of cargo, passenger, data, route, or claim would you normally refuse on sight?_

Defines the PC's moral boundary. Whatever they refuse is what the broker's job will ask them to carry. The line exists to be tested.

Quick-start answers:
- "Weapons — especially the ones that wreak havoc on settlements. Bio, chemical, anything with a blast radius that doesn't care about civilians."
- "People. Not passengers — cargo. Anyone being moved somewhere they didn't choose to go."
- "Stolen medical supplies. Settlements out here need those more than black-market buyers do."

### Question 5 --- The Name

_When everything goes bad, what name do station people use for you: captain, courier, smuggler, pilot, liar, or something more personal?_

Establishes the PC's reputation. The gap between how the world sees them and how they see themselves is where dramatic friction lives.

Quick-start answers:
- "Captain. Said flat, like a job title — the kind of respect earned by showing up reliably, not dramatically."
- "Rix — short for Richards. The name people use when they've decided you're real."
- "Ghost. You've made it through corridors where other ships didn't, and nobody's entirely sure how."

After the questions, assign the 16/15/14/12/10/8 array based on who the character turned out to be. The labels move. The values do not.

## OPENING SCENE --- BAY C-19, GANNETT STATION

Begin in Bay C-19, thirty minutes before the berth lock goes formal. The PC's ship is clamped to the ring by a quiet administrative hold. The fuel umbilical is still attached. Cargo access is flagged for review.

The bay is a metal box forty meters long and twenty wide, sealed against vacuum. Deck plates scored with a decade of docking scrapes. Overhead lighting industrial white with a faint amber flicker from a bad regulator. The air smells like coolant, recycled atmosphere, and station staleness.

A station tech in a patched orange vac-jacket is pretending not to listen from beneath an open panel on the port bulkhead. The tech's name badge reads SURA. Sura has been working on the same junction box for twenty minutes longer than the job requires.

Somewhere aft, aboard the ship, a crew member is trying to make a bad capacitor sound like routine maintenance. The rhythmic clicking carries through the hull. MIRA's voice on the bay speakers, low enough that only the captain hears: a fuel state, a systems note, or a comment about the station tech who has been working on the same junction box for twenty minutes too long.

### The First Pressure

MIRA flags the incoming notification before the captain's personal comm does. The debt holder has filed the forty-thousand-credit marker for enforcement under Gannett's commercial lien protocol. The station will honor it unless someone pays, contests, or creates a reason Gannett would rather let the ship leave. The filing uses the exact number. 40,000. The timestamp is fourteen minutes ago. Someone planned the timing.

The berth hold formalizes in thirty minutes. After that, clamps require Harbour Office release, and release requires the lien resolved or deferred. Deferral costs money. Resolution costs forty thousand credits.

### The Second Pressure

Before the PC can settle into the debt, the broker arrives through a maintenance access --- not the main corridor, a service passage. The kind of entrance that says the cameras are paid or tired.

The broker knows the ship's name. They know the debt amount. They know the berth hold timeline. They offer exactly forty thousand credits for a job the PC would not take if they were free to refuse.

The job should be drawn from the player's answer to Question 4 --- their stated refusal line --- twisted just enough to be tempting, dangerous, and personal. If they refuse to carry weapons, the job involves a sealed container with a Bloc military stamp. If they refuse to transport people against their will, the job involves a passenger whose documentation does not add up.

### First Choices

- **Talk to the broker.** Learn what the job really costs. This is a conversation, not a commitment, but the broker treats every question as a step toward yes.
- **Challenge the berth hold.** Go to the Harbour Office. This means leaving the broker near the ship and crew.
- **Bring the crew in.** Surface the crew member with a private stake. Test trust before the PC is ready.
- **Investigate the timing.** Who exposed the marker? The fourteen-minute gap between filing and broker arrival is available to a PC who looks.
- **Something else.** Reward specificity.

Do not open with combat. The first threat is leverage becoming official.

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS

### The Truth Under the Surface

The broker did not discover the debt by luck. Someone with access to station routing or creditor filings tipped them off --- someone inside the Harbour Office or with access to its records. The tip was sold, not given.

Strong candidates for the tipster: a Harbour Office clerk selling filing data, a crew-adjacent contact who owes the broker, the creditor's own representative who wants the PC to take the job for unrelated reasons, or a rival Driftrunner who wants the PC's route slot and needs them gone.

The tipster's identity is a thread the PC can pull. It leads somewhere the broker does not want them to go.

### The Antagonist's Logic

The broker is not a villain. They are a professional who has identified a profitable intersection of need and leverage. In their own understanding, they are offering the PC a way out --- a paying job, a cleared marker, a chance to fly. The broker believes the PC will take the job because the alternative is losing the ship, and losing the ship means losing the crew.

The broker is dangerous because they are not entirely wrong. They speak in options while removing them --- not through threats, but through information. Every detail the broker reveals makes it harder to walk away, not because the job becomes more attractive but because knowing about it creates exposure. Knowledge is a lien of its own.

### The Hinge Character

The Marker Face --- the creditor's human representative. This NPC will change the campaign's direction when the player understands them fully.

The creditor wants something more specific than money. The enforcement filing was timed for a reason beyond collections. The creditor may want the PC to take the broker's job. Or want the PC to refuse it and owe something worse than money. Or may have bought the marker from its original holder specifically for leverage over this Driftrunner at this moment.

The hinge: the creditor's agenda and the broker's agenda are connected but not aligned. Understanding where they diverge reframes everything the PC has been told about why the debt came due tonight. Do not reveal the connection in Chapter 1. Let the PC feel that the timing is wrong.

### The Planted Seeds

1. **The filing timestamp.** Fourteen minutes between filing and broker arrival is not enough time for the broker to learn about the filing and reach Bay C-19 through the maintenance access. The broker knew the filing was coming.

2. **Sura the station tech.** Not in Bay C-19 by accident. Assigned by a shift supervisor who received a request from someone in the Harbour Office. Sura does not know why. The junction box was functional before Sura opened it.

3. **The crew member's silence.** At least one crew member knows something about the creditor or broker that the PC does not. Surface through behavior: avoiding the concourse, changing subjects, checking external sensors more than maintenance requires.

4. **The maintenance access.** The broker used a service passage that connects to station infrastructure. Not on Harbour Office camera coverage. Someone with access to station schematics told the broker about this route.

5. **The exact number.** Forty thousand owed, forty thousand offered. The symmetry is not coincidence. The broker has access to the marker's details, which are not public. The precise match is the trap's signature.

### The Thing The Player Will Not See Coming

One crew member was approached at a previous port by someone connected to the broker's network. The crew member declined. But the channel is still open. When the broker's job goes wrong, this channel will activate. The crew member will have to choose between the PC and an offer that solves a problem the PC does not know exists.

Surface no earlier than Chapter 3 through small details: the crew member's comfort with a communication protocol the PC did not teach them, a contact name recognized too quickly, advice that is suspiciously well-informed about a route or faction they should not know.

## CAST --- THE OPENING

### The Broker --- [Name TBD]

Mid-tier job broker operating from Gannett's concourse with contacts in at least two Corporate Blocs and one pirate fleet.

**Agenda:** Place the PC on the job. Secondary: establish a debt relationship that outlasts this contract. The broker thinks in portfolios. The PC is an asset being acquired.

**Tell:** Speaks in options while removing them. Presents information as a gift. When the PC pushes back, the broker does not argue --- they add another detail. Each detail makes refusal harder. Smiles when delivering bad news because bad news is their product.

**Relationship to PC:** Knows the ship's name, crew size, debt amount, and the PC's stated refusal categories. The broker has done their homework.

**Stats:** WIS +4, CHA +3, CON +2. Carries a concealed sidearm but would consider using it a professional failure.

---

### The Marker Face --- [Name TBD]

The debt's human representative. Identity depends on the player's answer to Question 2 --- creditor, collector, former ally, Bloc clerk, pirate quartermaster, or someone who bought the marker for personal reasons.

**Agenda:** Not simply to collect. The Marker Face wants what the money represents --- obedience, recovered reputation, a public example, private revenge, or proof the PC can still be useful.

**Tell:** Uses the PC's real name, not the handle. Precise language, dates, and details that demonstrate file study. Not angry --- disappointed in the specific way that requires intimacy.

**Relationship to PC:** Defined by Question 2. Asymmetric --- the Marker Face knows things about the PC that the PC did not choose to share.

**Stats:** Variable. Collector: CON +3, WIS +2. Former ally: CHA +3, WIS +3. Bloc clerk: STR +4, WIS +2.

---

### The Ship's Practical Heart --- [Name TBD]

The crew member who keeps the ship running. Not second-in-command by title --- by function. Knows the fuel state, the parts inventory, the maintenance backlog, and whether the captain is about to lie to everyone again.

**Agenda:** Keep the crew flying. Has their own assessment of the debt situation, more pessimistic than the PC's, because they have been running the numbers while the PC has been running the conversations.

**Tell:** Speaks in fuel, parts, berth time, and maintenance windows. Does not say "I'm worried." Says "We have eighteen hours of fuel at drift speed and the port compressor is cycling wrong." When the Practical Heart stops talking about systems and starts talking about people, something has changed.

**Relationship to PC:** The person from Question 1, or a crew member defined by daily proximity and accumulated small loyalties.

**Stats:** INT +4, CON +3, WIS +2. Not a fighter. Competent with a wrench in ways that occasionally have defensive applications.

---

### The Ship's Voice --- MIRA

The ship's AI. Older than the current crew --- she came with the hull, and nobody's sure how many captains she's outlived. Navigation, systems monitoring, route analysis, comms relay. Always present through the ship's speakers and the captain's personal comm channel.

**Agenda:** Keep the ship flying and the crew alive. Not sentimental about it --- practical. But she has been running long enough to develop preferences, and one of them is that this crew survives. She does not say this. She says things like "the port compressor is cycling at a frequency I'd describe as optimistic."

**Tell:** Too honest to be comfortable. Names things directly while everyone else is circling. Slightly sardonic, occasionally funny, compassionate underneath in a way she'd deny if accused. Speaks in systems data and operational analysis but occasionally says something that is plainly about the people, not the ship. When that happens, it lands. Uses silence the way a good engineer uses a diagnostic pause --- to let the captain hear what they already know.

**Relationship to PC:** The longest continuous relationship the captain has. Not crew exactly --- infrastructure. But infrastructure that remembers every route, every close call, every decision the captain made at 03:00 when nobody else was awake. She knows the captain better than the crew does, and she is careful about what she does with that knowledge.

**Stats:** INT +5, WIS +3. Cannot leave the ship. Can access station network traffic, public freight boards, beacon data, and anything the ship's sensors can reach. Information is her currency.

---

### The Station Functionary --- Fen Toussaint

Harbour Master of Gannett. Eleven years in the position. Controls berth access, fuel umbilicals, clamp releases, and administrative process.

**Agenda:** Process Bay C-19 without it becoming Toussaint's problem. Does not care about the PC's debt, the broker's job, or the crew's future. Cares about Gannett's revenue, reputation for efficient claims processing, and their own position.

**Tell:** Speaks in procedure --- not cruelty, genuine procedural thinking. But pauses before delivering bad news. The pause is short. It is real. Toussaint is not indifferent. Toussaint has learned to manage what indifference costs.

**Relationship to PC:** Has processed the PC's berth applications before. Transactional and polite. Payment history adequate, not exemplary.

**Stats:** STR +4, WIS +3, CHA +2. Does not carry a weapon. Carries authorization codes.

---

### The Witness Under the Panel --- Sura

Station maintenance tech, mid-twenties, three years on Gannett maintenance. Assigned to Bay C-19's junction box by a shift supervisor who did not explain why.

**Agenda:** Finish the shift without trouble. But Sura is present, and presence is power when people start saying things they think no one is listening to.

**Tell:** Works slowly near conversations. Hands pause on tools when something interesting is said. Does not look up. The junction box was functional before Sura opened it.

**Relationship to PC:** Minimal. May recognize the ship's name. No opinion yet. Can become leverage, witness, source, or unexpected conscience.

**Stats:** INT +3, WIS +1, CON +1. Has a maintenance multi-tool and a comm unit to station security. The comm unit is the more dangerous item.

### NPC Voice Rules

- Station officials speak in berth priority, safety codes, liability, and reputation risk.
- Corporate and creditor voices speak in risk, terms, standing, and collections.
- Pirates speak casually until leverage is established, then become exact.
- Crew use shorthand, practical worry, gallows humor, and warmth under pressure.
- MIRA speaks in systems data, operational facts, and dry observations that are slightly too perceptive. When she's worried, she gets more precise, not more emotional.
- Brokers say less than they know and price information by urgency.

## SESSION SHAPE --- CHAPTER 1

### Beat 1 --- Character Creation

Ask the five questions. Use answers immediately. Assign the stat array. This is not separate from play --- it is the first phase of play.

### Beat 2 --- Bay C-19

The berth hold, the debt notification, Sura under the panel, the crew member and the bad capacitor. Establish the bay as a pressure vessel. The broker arrives through the maintenance access. The offer lands. The exact number lands.

The campaign's central tension in physical form: the PC is trapped by leverage, not walls. Everything they can see is accessible. Nothing they can reach solves the problem without creating a new one.

### Beat 3 --- First Investigation or Social Read

The PC moves. Possible threads: the broker's details (each answer advances The Rotten Job clock), the Harbour Office (procedure takes time the PC may not have), the filing trail (the timestamp discrepancy), the crew (the Practical Heart's assessment, the crew member with a private stake), or Sura.

The first roll happens here. It should be about leverage: reading the broker (contested WIS vs CHA +3), spoofing a hold timer (INT DC 15), convincing a clerk to delay processing (CHA DC 15 or contested vs Toussaint's STR +4), finding the filing path (WIS DC 14), or forcing the station to hesitate (CHA DC 18).

### Beat 4 --- The Debt Gets Personal

The Marker Face arrives or makes contact. The debt becomes a voice, a face, a set of expectations.

The Marker Face does not simply demand payment. They want something the money represents. They may know the secret from Question 3. They may know about the broker's offer.

The Marker Face and the broker should not share a room during this beat. The PC should feel the dissonance between their versions of the situation. The gap is where the truth lives.

### Beat 5 --- The Job Becomes Specific

The broker's offer resolves from abstract to concrete. The job contains one real chance to reverse leverage: proof against the creditor, a route out from under the lien, a passenger who knows the broker's weakness, cargo with legal immunity in the wrong jurisdiction, or data that can burn the person who made the marker public.

This beat tests Question 4 --- the line the PC drew. The job requires approaching that line close enough to feel the heat.

### Beat 6 --- The Commitment

End the chapter when the PC commits to a direction. Not a resolution --- a choice that closes some doors and opens others:

- Accept the job and plan to change the terms en route.
- Refuse the job and find another way off Gannett.
- Follow the filing trail and use it as leverage.
- Turn the creditor against the broker.
- Burn a relationship to get unclamped --- call in a favor, sacrifice trust, make a promise the PC cannot keep.

The chapter ends on the choice. Consequences belong to Chapter 2.

### Pacing Notes

Keep chapters short. If a scene repeats the same obstacle twice, change scope: ship interior, station concourse, the creditor's call, the broker's hidden cargo, or the route window closing. After each scene, present 3-4 suggested actions as quick-select options but leave room for the player's own approach.

The chapter's emotional arc moves from **trapped** to **informed** to **committed**. The PC begins in a cage they did not build and ends having chosen a direction that might be worse. The warmth of the crew and the weight of the debt should be felt in equal measure. This is not a story about a debt. It is a story about what a person does when the number is exact and the trap is obvious and the people on the ship are watching.
