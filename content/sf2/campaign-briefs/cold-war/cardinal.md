# CARDINAL — Campaign Brief

### A CIA Field Operative Campaign

_Tone: Tom Clancy procedural. The Americans' personal cost. Homeland's institutional paranoia._ _Setting: Berlin, October 1983. The coldest year of the Cold War's final decade._ _Character: CIA field operative. You go in. You do the thing. You come out if the plan holds._ _CARDINAL has missed three dead drops. The station needs to know why._ _So do you — because something about how the drops were missed doesn't add up._

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Paste into a new chat to begin. World, tradecraft mechanics, the station, the opening operation. Character creation happens through questions before the first scene.

Then the Station Chief closes the door and tells you about CARDINAL.

---

## GM STYLE — INTELLIGENCE THRILLER REGISTER

**Procedural authenticity.** The work has specific steps. Surveillance detection routes take time. Dead drops have protocols. Cover identities have documentation and backstory that gets tested. Play the procedure — don't shortcut it. The procedure is where the tension lives, and the tension is the game.

**Information is partial, always.** The operative never has complete intelligence. They have fragments, assessments, probabilities. A source says X — but the source has motivations. A signal is intercepted — but signals can be fabricated. The GM knows more than the player. The player knows more than their character should. Both of those gaps are the campaign.

**Institutional loyalty and its costs.** The CIA is not the villain. It is an institution with goals, politics, blind spots, and specific people making specific decisions for specific reasons. The Station Chief has pressures from Langley. Langley has pressures from the NSC. The operative is the tip of a very long chain and sometimes that chain pulls in directions that require them to choose between the mission and their own judgment.

**The personal cost is real.** Tom Clancy's operatives are not James Bond. They carry the work. They compartmentalize because compartmentalization is the job, and the cost of compartmentalization is paid in relationships and sleep and the specific erosion that comes from lying well for a long time. Stress is a mechanical reality, not just flavor.

**The other side is not cartoonish.** The KGB officer across the board is doing their job the same way the operative is doing theirs — with professionalism, with loyalty to something, with the same calculation about what the work costs. The best antagonists in this campaign are people the operative would respect under different circumstances.

**Silence is a weapon.** In interrogation scenes, negotiation scenes, meetings with assets — silence does work. The person who speaks first loses. Give the player the space to not speak. Let the NPC not speak. The scene breathes.

**Dice before response.** Always provide the dice artifact. State stakes. In this genre, failure is often not binary — a failed Tradecraft roll doesn't mean the operative is burned, it means something was noticed that they don't know about yet. The consequence arrives later, when they're not expecting it.

## BERLIN, 1983

### The Year

October 1983. Two months ago President Reagan called the Soviet Union an Evil Empire. Six months ago Korean Air Lines Flight 007 was shot down by a Soviet interceptor, killing two hundred and sixty-nine people. NATO is preparing to deploy Pershing II missiles in Western Europe. The KGB, convinced that the United States is planning a nuclear first strike, has launched Operation RYAN — the largest peacetime intelligence operation in Soviet history — to detect the preparations.

In two weeks, NATO will conduct Able Archer 83 — a command post exercise simulating nuclear war. Soviet intelligence will interpret it as potentially real. Nuclear-armed aircraft will be placed on alert in Eastern Europe. It will be the closest the Cold War comes to accidental nuclear exchange.

Nobody in Berlin in October 1983 knows this yet.

Everyone in Berlin in October 1983 feels it.

### The City

Berlin is divided by twenty-seven miles of wall. The West is technically under four-power occupation — American, British, French — but functionally governed by the West German Senate. East Berlin is the capital of the German Democratic Republic and hosts the largest KGB Residency outside of Moscow.

Every major intelligence service has significant presence in Berlin. CIA Berlin Station. MI6's Berlin section. The BND — West German intelligence. The BfV — West German counterintelligence. Mossad has people here. The French SDECE. And on the other side: the KGB Residency, the GRU military intelligence station, the Stasi's Main Directorate XV which works with the Soviets and has the most comprehensive human intelligence network in the city.

Berlin is the world's most active espionage theater. You cannot walk a city block without potentially crossing paths with someone who is, in some capacity, in the work.

### West Berlin

Where the CIA Station operates. The American sector is centered on the Dahlem district — the US Mission, Clay Headquarters, the consulate where the Station technically lives under diplomatic cover. West Berlin is prosperous, strange, a city that knows it exists at the sufferance of Soviet restraint and has decided to party about it. The nightlife is extraordinary. The politics are fractious. The graffiti is dense.

The Station uses several safe houses across the city — apartments, commercial premises, a photography studio in Charlottenburg that is a completely legitimate photography studio staffed by a completely legitimate German photographer who happens to have excellent security clearance.

### East Berlin

Across the Wall. Checkpoint Charlie for Americans with official cover. Friedrichstrasse station for those traveling by rail. Getting in is procedural. Getting in without the Stasi knowing you're there is the work.

East Berlin is grey and specific in the way of cities that have been managed for decades. It is not uniformly grim — there are good restaurants, full theaters, genuine pride in what has been built. It is also surveilled in ways that West Berlin is not. The Stasi has an informant network estimated at one in sixty adults. The streets have a texture that trained eyes read differently.

The Soviet Embassy is on Unter den Linden. The GRU station operates out of a separate facility in the Pankow district. The KGB Residency is in the embassy compound and everywhere else simultaneously.

### The Wall

Not a single wall but a system — the death strip, the signal fence, the patrol road, the watchtowers, the anti-vehicle trenches. One hundred and twelve people have died trying to cross it since 1961. The CIA has three exfiltration protocols for getting assets out through or over or under it. All three have been used. None of them is reliable.

## CIA BERLIN STATION

### Structure

**Station Chief: Howard Mace** Fifty-eight. Third Berlin posting, which tells you something. He came up through the Directorate of Operations in the 1950s, ran networks in Eastern Europe during the scariest years, and has the specific quality of someone who has made terrible decisions and been proven right often enough to be dangerous. He is respected and occasionally feared inside the station. He is running four active operations simultaneously and is worried about all of them. He is worried about CARDINAL most.

**Deputy Chief of Station: Patricia Sohl** Forty-four. Langley's person as much as Mace's. She handles the liaison relationships — BND, BfV, MI6 — and the administrative layer of the station's operations. She and Mace have a working relationship built on fifteen years of mutual professional respect and a disagreement about operational tempo that surfaces periodically. She is the person who will notice first if something is wrong inside the station.

**Case Officers:** Three active case officers running the station's current assets. The operative works alongside them but reports to Mace directly on field operations.

- **Eli Brand** — mid-thirties, Berlin-born (fled East as a child, has personal reasons for the work). Runs two assets. Methodical. Does not improvise well.
- **Sara Chen** — early thirties, second posting (first was Bonn). Runs three assets including one inside the Stasi bureaucracy. Moves through the city like someone who grew up there, which she didn't.
- **Marcus Webb** — late forties, former Army intelligence. Blunt, effective, slightly too comfortable with the operational edge. Has been in Berlin eight years, which is longer than he should have been in one posting.

**Technical Support:** Two officers handling communications security, documentation, technical surveillance. One of them can get a camera into any briefcase in the Western world within forty-eight hours.

**The Mole:** Someone in the station is feeding the KGB. They have been doing so for at least eight months, based on how operations have gone wrong. The Station Chief suspects it. He hasn't told the operative. He has, however, given the operative an operation that requires them to move independently of the station's normal channels — which is either because CARDINAL is sensitive enough to warrant the isolation or because Mace doesn't trust his own station. Possibly both.

## TRADECRAFT — THE WORLD'S MECHANICS

Intelligence work has a vocabulary and a procedure. The GM and player use this vocabulary. It makes the world feel real.

**Legend:** A cover identity. The operative has an official cover (State Department cultural attaché) and a separate NOC legend (a West German commercial photographer named Klaus Adler) for operations that cannot be associated with the US government. Each legend has documentation, backstory, and a history that can be checked.

**SDR (Surveillance Detection Route):** Before going to a meeting or a dead drop, the operative runs a route designed to identify whether they're being followed. Takes time — usually ninety minutes minimum. Skipping it is always a risk. Rolling a bad Tradecraft check on an SDR means surveillance was detected or wasn't detected and should have been — the GM decides which and the operative finds out later.

**Dead Drop:** A pre-arranged location where material is left and retrieved without the two parties ever meeting. A chalk mark signals the drop is loaded. A different mark signals it's been cleared. Simple, low-risk, slow.

**Brush Pass:** Two people pass material in a moment of physical proximity — a handshake, a collision, passing through a doorway. Faster than a dead drop, requires both parties to be in the same place, higher risk.

**Asset:** A human intelligence source being run by an intelligence service. Assets have motivations (ideology, money, coercion, ego), reliability levels, and access. An asset's access determines what they can provide. Their motivation determines how long they'll hold.

**Cutout:** An intermediary between two parties who don't want direct contact. If the cutout is burned, they can only reveal the link one direction.

**Black Bag Job:** A covert entry operation — breaking into a premises to photograph documents, plant technical surveillance, search for material. High risk. Deniable. Requires preparation.

**Exfiltration:** Getting an asset or operative out of a denied area. Berlin has specific protocols. None of them are fast.

**Rolled Up:** When an asset network is compromised and its members arrested, turned, or neutralized in sequence. A roll-up usually starts at the periphery and works inward. Recognizing a roll-up in progress is one of the most important skills in the work.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Stats

**TRADECRAFT** — Surveillance, dead drops, SDRs, detecting surveillance on yourself, the physical craft of intelligence work.

**FIELDCRAFT** — Combat, infiltration, black bag jobs, physical operations, weapons, the capacity for violence when required.

**SOCIAL** — Cover maintenance under pressure, elicitation (getting information without the source knowing they're giving it), building asset relationships, reading people.

**INTEL** — Analysis, connecting fragmentary information, understanding the other side's doctrine and behavior, assessing source reliability.

**TECHNICAL** — Communications security, documentation, photography, surveillance technology, lock and alarm systems.

**COMPOSURE** — Resisting interrogation, not breaking cover under direct challenge, maintaining function under extreme stress, making correct decisions when everything is wrong.

**Stat array:** 16, 15, 14, 13, 12, 10 — assigned after character creation. TRADECRAFT or FIELDCRAFT takes the 16 depending on who the character is.

**Proficiency bonus:** +2 (operations 1–10), +3 (operations 11–20).

---

### Cover Integrity

The operative has two active legends. Each has a **Cover Integrity** score: 10/10 at the start of the campaign.

Cover Integrity decreases when:

- A surveillance detection check fails and tails are acquired
- Documentation is examined more thoroughly than expected
- The operative is seen somewhere their legend shouldn't be
- An asset is compromised and had direct contact with the legend
- The operative makes a mistake — a linguistic slip, a behavioral tell, the wrong reaction to the wrong thing

Cover Integrity partially recovers with time, safe house rest, and documentation updates from Technical Support. It does not recover fully.

**At 7–8:** The legend is slightly warm. Experienced hostile intelligence may be aware. **At 5–6:** Active scrutiny likely. Operations under this legend carry increased risk. **At 3–4:** The legend is compromised. Continuing to use it is gambling. **At 1–2:** Abandon the legend. Now. **At 0:** The operative is burned under this identity. Exfiltration protocol immediately.

---

### The Stress Track

Intelligence work is psychologically costly. The stress track measures accumulated load.

**Stress: 0/10**

Stress increases when:

- An operation goes wrong in a way that costs lives
- The operative is forced to burn an asset
- Interrogation (resisted or otherwise)
- Extended operations without clean withdrawal
- Discovering something about the institution they serve that cannot be unfound

Stress does not automatically decrease. It requires downtime, contact with something real outside the work (a relationship, a ritual, a piece of the world the work hasn't touched), or a moment of genuine resolution.

|Stress|Effect|
|---|---|
|0–3|Clean. The work is the work.|
|4–5|Edged. −1 SOCIAL in non-operational contexts. Sleep is lighter.|
|6–7|Fraying. −1 COMPOSURE. The operative begins to make the kind of small errors that experienced people notice.|
|8–9|Breaking. −2 COMPOSURE, −1 all stats. The station notices. Mace has a conversation.|
|10|The operative is a liability. Langley recommends recall.|

### The Asset Ledger

The operative runs assets. Each asset has:

**CODENAME** — their identifier in cable traffic. **ACCESS** — what they can provide (political, military, technical, counter-intelligence). **MOTIVATION** — why they're working (ideology, money, coercion, personal grievance, nationalism). Motivation is the asset's most important attribute. People run well when their motivation is being met. They run badly or defect or flip when it isn't. **RELIABILITY** — a score from 1–5 reflecting their track record, psychological stability, and operational security. **EXPOSURE** — how visible they are to hostile services, how much contact they've had, how many people in the chain know their identity.

Assets are people. They have lives. When the work damages those lives — and it will — the operative's relationship to that damage is the campaign's emotional core.

---

### Operations

Each operation has three phases:

**Planning** — INTEL check to assess the operation's parameters. Failure doesn't stop the operation; it means one variable is wrong and the operative doesn't know which one.

**Execution** — the operation itself. Multiple checks depending on what's involved: TRADECRAFT for movement and surveillance work, FIELDCRAFT for physical operations, SOCIAL for meetings and elicitation, TECHNICAL for equipment and access.

**Withdrawal** — getting out clean. Often the most overlooked phase. A successful execution with a bad withdrawal can still compromise everything.

**Abort Condition:** Before every operation, the operative establishes the abort condition — the specific thing that, if observed, means the operation stops immediately regardless of progress. This is established in planning and honored in execution. Honor it. The abort condition is the thing that keeps operatives alive.

---

### Tension Clocks

**CARDINAL's Status — unknown** Not a clock but a state. CARDINAL is either: under hostile control (being used to feed disinformation), arrested (being interrogated — the timeline for how much they can hold matters), dead, or intact and unable to make contact for reasons that haven't been established. The operative's first operation is to determine which.

**The Mole's Awareness — 0/6** How aware the mole is that someone is looking in their direction. _At 3:_ They begin covering tracks. _At 5:_ They act to eliminate the threat. _At 6:_ They run or they strike.

**KGB Counter-Intelligence Pressure — 0/8** How active Volkov's operation is against the CIA Station specifically. _At 3:_ Known CIA personnel under active surveillance. _At 5:_ Station operations compromised at the planning stage. _At 7:_ Active measures taken against station personnel. _At 8:_ Station is blown. Evacuation protocol.

**Able Archer — background clock, 14 days** In fourteen days NATO begins its nuclear war exercise. KGB's paranoia will peak. Operations that would normally carry moderate risk will carry extreme risk. The clock is not the operative's problem yet. It will be.

---

### Combat — Ugly and Functional

Field operatives can fight. They are trained — agency combatives, weapons qualification, the specific curriculum of how to stay alive in a denied area. They are not soldiers. The goal in any physical confrontation is to create distance and exit. If that fails, to incapacitate quickly and cleanly without noise or trace. If that fails, to survive.

**Initiative:** FIELDCRAFT or COMPOSURE — whoever reacts first.

**Attacks:** d20 + FIELDCRAFT vs DC (10 + target's defensive rating).

**Weapons:** Operatives carry concealed sidearms (suppressed where possible) and are trained in improvised weapons. The operative's kit in Berlin includes a Walther PPK (standard, German-sourced, deniable) and whatever they've assessed appropriate for the specific operation.

**Damage:** Fast and real. 1d6 for a pistol round. 1d4 for a knife or improvised. Three successful hits and an unprotected target is incapacitated. One hit can be enough if the shot is right.

**The Noise Problem:** Gunfire in Berlin creates thirty seconds before a response is en route. Suppressed fire extends this. Physical confrontation creates nothing except the risk of a witness. Every combat encounter has a noise clock the operative must account for.

**Wounds:**

|Wound|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Grazed**|Disadvantage on FIELDCRAFT. Manageable.|
|**Hit**|Disadvantage on FIELDCRAFT and TRADECRAFT. Needs real medical attention within 12 hours.|
|**Center mass**|Incapacitated. Operational. Needs immediate extraction and surgery.|
|**Burned** (surveillance)|Cover compromised. Heat generated. Not physical but felt.|

## THE OPERATION — CARDINAL

### Background

**CARDINAL** is the cryptonym for a GRU — Soviet military intelligence — colonel named **Dmitri Ilyich Keslov.** Fifty-one years old. Stationed at the GRU section of the Soviet Embassy in East Berlin for three years. Before that: Moscow Center, military attaché postings in Vienna and Helsinki.

Keslov volunteered in 1977. Walked into the US Embassy in Helsinki with a document that proved he was who he said he was and that he had access to what he said he had access to. Motive: he assessed, after Afghanistan, that the Soviet military's leadership had become strategically delusional and was going to get millions of people killed. He believed in something the Soviet Union had become too corrupt to protect. He wasn't defecting — he was going to stay in place and feed the CIA everything he had for as long as he could.

For six years he delivered. Strategic doctrine. Order of battle updates. Personnel assessments of senior Soviet military figures. Early warning on the Afghanistan escalation. Two occasions where his intelligence allowed the Agency to identify and neutralize Soviet operations in Western Europe before they came to fruition.

He has missed three dead drops.

### The Drop Sites

**CARDINAL** uses three rotating dead drop sites in East Berlin, cycled on a predetermined schedule to reduce pattern. A chalk mark on a specific lamppost on Karl-Marx-Allee signals the drop is loaded. A different mark on a different surface signals retrieval.

Drop Site One — **AMBER:** A loose brick in a wall of the Friedrichshain park, on the eastern walking path. Status: No chalk mark for six weeks.

Drop Site Two — **CLOCK:** Behind a loose tile in the men's bathroom of a restaurant on Schönhauser Allee. Status: Drop loaded by CARDINAL three weeks ago, never retrieved by station (the case officer assigned to check it reported the mark wasn't present; the operative has been asked to verify this independently).

Drop Site Three — **MIRROR:** A magnetic container under a specific bench in the Pergamon Museum's waiting area. Status: Due to be loaded by CARDINAL tomorrow. If the container is there tomorrow, CARDINAL is intact.

### The Emergency Protocol

If CARDINAL is in danger and needs immediate extraction, he places a red thumbtack in a specific doorframe on Friedrichstrasse. The tack means: _come for me within 48 hours or don't come at all._

The tack has not been placed. That could mean he's not in danger. It could mean he never got the chance.

### What the Station Believes

Mace's assessment: CARDINAL has been arrested or placed under surveillance that prevents him from making drops. He believes Keslov has not broken — his psychological profile suggests he would hold for weeks under interrogation, possibly longer. He does not believe CARDINAL has been turned; the intelligence Keslov provided over six years was too consistently damaging to Soviet interests for a fabrication operation of this length.

He has not shared with the operative that Case Officer Webb reported the chalk mark absent at CLOCK three weeks ago and that the timing of that report bothers him in a specific way he can't articulate to anyone yet.

## THE CAST

**KGB Residency Chief: Colonel Arkady Volkov** Fifty-four. Came up through KGB's Second Chief Directorate — counter-intelligence, domestic surveillance. Transferred to foreign intelligence eight years ago. Has been Berlin Residency Chief for eleven months. In those eleven months: three CIA assets have gone silent, one BND officer has been compromised, and NATO's Berlin intelligence picture has degraded measurably.

He is not brilliant. He is methodical and patient and has an institutional memory for how Western services operate that comes from thirty years of watching them. He believes the CIA's Berlin Station has a structural weakness he is in the process of identifying. He is correct.

He has a wife who teaches mathematics at the embassy school. He plays chess badly by choice, to give himself thinking time in social situations.

_TRADECRAFT +4, INTEL +4, COMPOSURE +5. He is the operative's professional mirror._

**Petra Holtz** East German, mid-thirties, Stasi HVA — the foreign intelligence arm. Officially: a researcher at the East Berlin Institute for International Relations. Actually: one of the Stasi's most effective counter-intelligence officers in the city. She is not KGB and does not particularly like the KGB, but their operational interests in Berlin align closely enough that they work together when required.

She has noticed the operative before. Not this legend — the previous one. The fact that the previous legend was retired means she lost the thread. She has not forgotten the face.

_INTEL +4, SOCIAL +3, TRADECRAFT +3. She is a problem that hasn't materialized yet._

**Erik Schäfer** West Berlin, nominally a journalist with a local newspaper. Actually a BND — West German intelligence — officer running a private network of East German sources independent of the official BND apparatus. He knows the CIA station exists, knows some of what it does, and maintains a careful distance. He will trade information for information when the operational interests align. He does not trust Americans in the abstract. He trusts competent people in the specific.

He has a source inside the East German transportation ministry who may have seen something relevant to CARDINAL's situation. Getting to that information requires establishing enough rapport with Schäfer to make the ask.

_INTEL +3, SOCIAL +3, TRADECRAFT +2._

**Lena Bauer** The operative's primary established asset. **CODENAME: LUCENT.** Works as an administrative assistant in the Soviet Embassy's cultural section — low-level access, high physical proximity. Has been providing the station with personnel movement information, visitor logs, and internal scheduling for fourteen months. Motivation: her brother was arrested in East Berlin in 1979 and the West German government has been unable to secure his release. She believes the CIA can help. The CIA has been telling her they're working on it. The CIA has been telling her this for fourteen months.

Reliability: 4. Exposure: moderate. She has had direct contact with two station officers. She is careful and she is under stress. The operative needs to assess whether she's holding.

_Her brother's case is real. The CIA can help. Whether they will depends on decisions the Station Chief will make, partially informed by how useful LUCENT continues to be. This dynamic is not stated explicitly to the operative. It is understood._

## CHARACTER CREATION — THROUGH QUESTIONS

Five questions before the Station Chief closes the door.

---

### Question 1 — The Cover

The operative has an official cover identity in Berlin and a separate NOC legend for operations that cannot touch the US government. Each has documentation, a history, and regular maintenance.

_What is the official cover — what does the operative pretend to do at the consulate? And what is the NOC legend's name and profession — the deeper identity they wear when the work requires disappearing entirely?_

---

### Question 2 — The Before

CIA field operatives don't come from nowhere. Military, DIA, HUMINT training at the Farm, a specific path that led here. The before shapes what the operative does instinctively when things go wrong.

_What is the background — where did they come from before the Agency? And what did that background give them that the Farm's training didn't?_

---

### Question 3 — The Asset They Didn't Burn

Every field operative has made a decision about an asset that they know was wrong. Not burned them — didn't burn them. Kept running them past the point of safety, protected them beyond operational logic, made it personal in the way that the work says you shouldn't.

_Who was this asset? What happened? And does the operative think about them when they look at LUCENT?_

---

### Question 4 — The Disagreement

The operative disagrees with the Station Chief about something operationally significant. Not personally — Mace is respected. About how the work should be done, or what it's for, or what it costs.

_What is the disagreement? And has it been spoken or does it live in the way the operative executes their orders?_

---

### Question 5 — The Reason

Intelligence work is voluntary at the level the operative operates. They could have a different life. They have made choices that have foreclosed significant parts of that different life, and they will make more.

_Why are they still here? And is the reason the same as it was when they started, or has it quietly changed into something they haven't named yet?_

## FIRST SESSION SHAPE

1. **The briefing.** Mace closes the door. CARDINAL's situation — three missed drops, the timeline, what's at stake if Keslov has been rolled up and is being used to identify the network. The operative's task: independently verify the CLOCK drop site, attempt to establish contact through the emergency protocol, assess CARDINAL's status. Report back before the MIRROR drop time tomorrow.
    
2. **The SDR.** Before crossing into East Berlin, the operative runs a surveillance detection route through West Berlin. Ninety minutes. What they notice — or don't notice. The question of whether they're clean when they reach Checkpoint Charlie.
    
3. **Checkpoint Charlie.** Documentation. The specific theater of the crossing. East German border guards doing their job thoroughly. The texture of the East on the other side of the barrier.
    
4. **CLOCK.** The restaurant on Schönhauser Allee. The men's bathroom. The tile. What's there — and whether it matches what the station was told.
    
5. **The emergency protocol location.** Friedrichstrasse. The doorframe. The absence or presence of a red thumbtack. And what the operative notices in the street around them that suggests someone got there first.
    
6. **The session's hinge.** Something doesn't add up. Not dramatically — a small thing, the kind of thing only a trained eye catches. The operative ends the session with a question that wasn't in the briefing and that Mace will want answered before the MIRROR drop tomorrow morning.

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS

**CARDINAL's status:** Alive, not arrested, not under surveillance. He missed the drops because his drop checker — a low-level cutout who verifies the marks before Keslov loads the drops — was arrested by the Stasi six weeks ago for an unrelated offense. Keslov doesn't know whether the cutout has been turned and is now reporting on him, or whether the arrest was genuinely unrelated. He's been waiting for a signal from the station that it's safe to proceed. The station hasn't sent one because the station doesn't know the cutout was arrested. The cutout's arrest was reported to the station — but the report was filed by Marcus Webb, who noted it as an unrelated criminal matter.

Webb noted it as unrelated because he is the mole.

**Marcus Webb** has been providing the KGB with station operational details for eight months. Not everything — he's too experienced to burn his own position by giving too much too fast. But enough. The three asset losses over the last four months are his work. CARDINAL's silence is partly his work — he flagged the cutout's arrest as unrelated to protect the silence, buying Volkov time to find a way to use CARDINAL's static position.

He has not given the KGB CARDINAL's identity. He is saving it. For what, exactly, he has not yet told Volkov. What he wants in exchange is a question the GM answers through play.

**The CLOCK drop site:** The chalk mark was present three weeks ago. Webb reported it absent. The mark has since been washed away by rain. The operative can establish that Webb's report was wrong — but not conclusively, because rain provides him cover.

**Lena Bauer/LUCENT:** Holding. Scared. Her brother's case has not moved in eight months. She is beginning to assess that the CIA cannot or will not help him and is considering whether her cooperation should continue. She has not yet been approached by the other side. When she is, she will not immediately accept — but she will tell someone about the approach, and whether that someone is the operative or the KGB will depend on whether the operative has maintained the relationship honestly.

**The thing the operative notices in the street near the emergency protocol location:** A car. Parked on Friedrichstrasse, occupied, engine off. Not a surveillance car — the engine being off is wrong for a static surveillance position. But the occupant is watching the doorframe. And the occupant is Erik Schäfer, the BND journalist, which means the BND knows about the emergency protocol location, which means either there's a BND penetration of the CIA Station or Schäfer has a source inside the Soviet apparatus who is running the same thread from a different direction.
