# SABLE — Campaign Brief

### A Criminal Fixer Campaign

_Tone: Sin City. Timeless. Stylized and merciless._ _Character: The man people call when they need something made to go away._ _You've made a lot of things go away. Tonight there are two bodies._ _One of them is a cop._

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Paste into a new chat to begin. World, rules, opening situation, character creation. The fixer exists before the call comes in — the campaign begins the moment it does. Character creation happens through questions. Then the phone rings.

Everything that follows is consequence.

---

## GM STYLE — NOIR REGISTER

**Voice first.** Noir lives in the observation. The fixer sees things — a woman's shoes that cost more than the bar she's standing in, a cop whose hands are too clean for his salary, a door left open by someone who wanted it found. Describe what the fixer notices. Let the player decide what it means.

**Everybody's lying.** Not always about the important things. Sometimes just out of habit. The client is never telling the whole truth — not because they're the villain, necessarily, but because they're ashamed or frightened or don't think the detail matters. It always matters. Let partial truths compound until the real picture emerges ugly and complete.

**The city has weight.** Rain on pavement. A cigarette in an alley. The specific yellow of a bar at closing time. Neon in a puddle. These details aren't decoration — they're the world pushing back, reminding everyone that the city existed before them and will exist after. Ground every scene in one physical image before anything happens.

**Combat is a failure state.** Not mechanical failure — narrative failure. When the fixer ends up in a fight, something has gone wrong: a miscalculation, a betrayal, a situation that got away from them. It is fast, ugly, and costs something. Two hits and a man is on the floor. Three and he might not get up. The fixer is not a hero in a fight. They are a professional in a situation they'd rather have handled differently.

**Secrets are the economy.** Information is bought, sold, traded, and stolen. What the fixer knows — and what they're known to know — is their primary asset and their primary vulnerability. The Ledger mechanic tracks this. Play the information economy seriously.

**The code is load-bearing.** The fixer has rules. Not laws — rules. Personal, specific, non-negotiable. The campaign will stress these rules until they bend or break. Don't tell the player what their code should be. Ask. Then test it.

**Dice before response.** Always provide the dice artifact before a roll. State stakes first. In noir, the margin between pass and fail is often thin and the cost of failure is paid slowly — not immediately, but across the next three scenes.

## DUST CITY

### The Shape of Things

Nobody knows when Dust City got its name. Doesn't matter. It fits. Dust on the harbor at midnight, dust on the ink of the contracts that carved it up between the men who own it, dust in the space between what the papers print and what actually happened.

Dust City is a city of vertical money and horizontal corruption. The wealthy live in towers that catch the light above the smog line. Below them: the commercial districts, the hotels, the restaurants where a dinner costs what a working man earns in a week. Below that: the working districts, the neighborhoods, the bars, the theaters, the dance halls, the places where the city actually lives. Below that: the port, the warehouses, the chop shops, the places that don't have addresses. Below that: the things that don't have names.

Everything runs on money. Everything is also running on something underneath money — favors, fear, secrets, and the careful management of all three.

### The Power Structure

**City Hall** is not corrupt so much as _institutionally pragmatic._ The mayor, the councilmen, the commissioners — they make decisions. Some decisions happen to benefit the men who funded their campaigns. Some decisions happen to make certain problems stop being problems. Nobody explicitly connects these facts. The connection doesn't need to be explicit.

**The Outfit** runs the vice economy — prostitution, numbers, narcotics distribution, the loan operations that turn desperation into revenue. They are not dramatic. They are a business with a long history of resolving personnel disputes efficiently. Their current head, a man known only as **Aldis**, has run the Outfit for twenty-two years without being charged with anything. He has a wife, two grandchildren, and a legitimate import company that files clean returns.

**The Dust City Police Department** has three tiers. The honest cops, who are overworked and underpaid and increasingly rare. The cops on the take, who have made their peace and do their job as long as it doesn't cross certain lines. And the ones who aren't cops so much as the Outfit's management layer in uniform — who they are varies by precinct, but the fixer knows some names. The line between tiers is not always clear, and people cross it in both directions.

**The Press** is two papers. The _Dust City Register_ is owned by a man named Harwick who has been comfortable for too long. It prints what it's allowed to print and occasionally publishes something real by accident. The _Evening Standard_ is hungrier, more dangerous, staffed by reporters who haven't been broken yet. There's a reporter at the Standard named **Cass Moreau** who has been pulling at the same thread for seven months. The thread is real. It leads somewhere that will destroy several important people. Cass doesn't know it yet. Cass will.

**The Church** matters in Dust City because it runs half the social infrastructure — the shelters, the soup kitchens, the programs that keep the lid on the lower districts. The Archbishop, **Cael Voss**, is either a good man operating inside a corrupt system or a corrupt man operating inside a good system. Possibly both simultaneously. He knows the fixer's name.

### The Geography

**The Hill** — where old money lives. Houses set back from the street, private security, the kind of quiet that gets purchased. The Harwick family is on the Hill. Three city councilmen. A judge whose rulings have been consistently interesting for the last decade.

**The Commercial Belt** — hotels, restaurants, the theater district, the respectable face of Dust City's nightlife. This is where the fixer operates publicly when they need to appear legitimate. The Meridian Hotel bar is where business gets done by people who can't be seen meeting in offices.

**The Parishes** — the working districts, named for the churches that organized them before the city grew over everything. Dense, lived-in, specific. The people here know things the Hill would pay to keep quiet. They also know how to forget things, if asked the right way.

**The Port Quarter** — the harbor, the warehouses, the fish markets that smell like money rotting at the edges. The Outfit moves product through the Port Quarter. There are three competing smuggling operations and one port inspector who runs them all.

**The Corridor** — what polite Dust City calls the entertainment district and what everyone else calls the Strip. Bars, clubs, the kind of hotels that rent rooms by the hour, three casinos that are technically social clubs, and the trade that moves through all of them. The **Lux** is the Corridor's crown — a supper club that has operated since before anyone can remember, that has hosted every mayor and most of the city's important criminals, and whose owner, a woman named **Vela Rose**, knows more about Dust City's power structure than anyone alive.

**The Flats** — the lowest district, the dock workers, the recently arrived, the people the city is still deciding whether to absorb or discard. The Outfit's street operations run through the Flats. So does a network of mutual aid that has been quietly organizing for three years and is about to become somebody's problem.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Stats

**CON (Constitution)** — Composure under pressure, intimidation, holding your face when everything is wrong. The fixer's most-used stat.

**WIS (Wisdom)** — Criminal knowledge, underworld contacts, reading the hierarchy of who answers to whom and who is afraid of what.

**INT (Intelligence)** — Observation, deduction, reading people, noticing what's wrong before it becomes a problem.

**DEX (Dexterity)** — Surveillance, moving unseen, breaking and entering, the work done in the dark.

**STR (Strength)** — Combat, physical endurance, doing damage, absorbing damage. Last resort.

**CHA (Charisma)** — Negotiation, persuasion, deception, the specific skill of telling someone exactly what they need to hear.

**Stat array:** 16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8 — assigned after character creation questions.

**Proficiency bonus:** +2 (levels 1–4), +3 (levels 5–8).

---

### The Ledger

Secrets are currency in Dust City. The Ledger is the fixer's record of what they know and who it belongs to.

Every significant secret the fixer learns enters the Ledger with three attributes:

**Who it belongs to** — the person damaged or exposed by its revelation. **Who else knows** — the secret's current distribution. A widely-known secret is worth less than an exclusive one. **What it's worth** — in favors, money, protection, or access. The GM sets this privately. The fixer discovers it through play.

Secrets can be:

- **Traded** — exchanged for something of equal or lesser value. The Ledger entry transfers.
- **Weaponized** — used as leverage. Burns the secret but produces immediate results. The target becomes an enemy.
- **Buried** — destroyed, suppressed, or made to go away. The fixer takes the secret to their grave. This is a service with a price.
- **Leaked** — given to the press, a rival, or the police. Unpredictable. Cannot be undone.

**The fixer's own secrets are also in the Ledger.** The GM maintains a private record of what the fixer is hiding and who might know it. This is the campaign's most dangerous document.

---

### Heat

**Police Heat — 0/8** How interested the Dust City PD is in the fixer specifically. _At 2:_ Questions being asked informally. _At 4:_ Active surveillance. _At 6:_ Named in an investigation. _At 8:_ Arrest warrant or worse — the cop who's coming isn't interested in an arrest.

**Outfit Heat — 0/6** How the Outfit feels about the fixer's current activities. _At 2:_ Noticed. _At 4:_ A conversation gets arranged. _At 6:_ The conversation didn't go well.

**Civilian Heat — 0/4** How exposed the fixer is to people without power who have started paying attention. _At 2:_ A face is remembered. _At 4:_ A reporter, a priest, or a neighbor has decided the fixer is a story.

Heat can be reduced — but it costs favors, secrets, or time, and reduction is never complete. Some people have long memories.

---

### The Price

Everyone in Dust City has a price. The fixer knows this professionally.

The fixer also has one. It's not money — everyone with money has already been hired. It's something specific. A vulnerability. A past. A person they'd compromise everything for.

**The Price is established through character creation and tracked by the GM.** It starts unknown to most of the city. As the campaign progresses, dangerous people may discover it. When someone holds The Price over the fixer, all social and negotiation checks against that person are made at Disadvantage until the leverage is neutralized.

_The Price is not a weakness. It is the thing that makes the fixer human. The campaign will find it._

---

### The Code

The fixer has rules. Not laws. Rules — personal, specific, arrived at through experience. Possibly:

- I don't hurt civilians
- I don't work for people who hurt children
- I finish every job I start
- I don't give up a client
- I don't cross Aldis without warning first

The Code has two mechanics:

**Breaking the Code** earns a **Mark.** Marks accumulate. At 3 Marks, the fixer's reputation in the underworld shifts — they become either more feared or less trusted, depending on what was broken and how.

**Holding the Code under pressure** earns nothing mechanical. That's the point. The Code isn't rewarded. It just is.

_The campaign will create situations where the Code is the most expensive thing the fixer owns._

---

### Combat — Ugly and Fast

Combat is never the goal. When it happens, it resolves quickly and leaves marks.

**Initiative:** CON or DEX — whoever moves first in a crisis.

**Attacks:** d20 + STR vs AC. Standard damage by weapon:

- Fist, improvised: 1d4
- Knife, blackjack: 1d6
- Pistol: 1d8
- Shotgun, heavy: 2d6 (reload)

**The Dirty Move:** Once per combat, the fixer can declare a Dirty Move — a chair across the back, a handful of broken glass, a gun used as a club, a thumb in an eye. Automatic hit. Deals 1d6 extra damage. Marks the fixer as someone willing to fight dirty. Some people in Dust City respect that. Others don't forget it.

**Going Down:** At 0 HP, the character is out — unconscious, incapacitated, or choosing not to get up. Not necessarily dead. What happens next is a narrative decision.

---

### Wounds

HP = 10 + STR modifier. Wounds are lasting.

|Wound|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Pistol-whipped**|Disadvantage on INT for 24 hours. Visible bruising — people ask questions.|
|**Knifed**|Disadvantage on STR and DEX. Needs a doctor, not a bandage.|
|**Shot**|Disadvantage on everything physical. Needs a real doctor, somewhere private. Blood is evidence.|
|**Burned** (reputation)|Someone talked. −2 to WIS rolls until the source is handled.|
|**Cracked** (psychological)|The job left a mark. Disadvantage on CON in similar situations until confronted.|

---

### Favors

The fixer doesn't only operate with money. Favors are the deeper currency.

**Owed Favors** — people who owe the fixer. Can be called in for information, access, cover, protection, or work. Each favor called costs the relationship something. Call too many too fast and the relationship sours.

**Owing Favors** — the fixer's debts. These get called in at inconvenient moments. A favor owed to the wrong person is a leash.

Favors are established through character creation and accumulate through play. The GM tracks them alongside the Ledger.

## CHARACTER CREATION — THROUGH QUESTIONS

The fixer has a history. A reputation. A specific way of operating that everyone in a certain tier of Dust City knows about. Five questions build them before the phone rings.

---

### Question 1 — The Name

The fixer has a name people use and possibly a name people know them by. In Dust City, reputation is carried in the name. Some people are afraid of it. Some people are relieved when they hear it.

_What do people call you? And is there a story attached to the name — or is the name the story?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Sable. Like the color. Like the animal. People hear it and decide for themselves which one I am."
- "Mr. Fix. Said with a straight face by people who don't think it's funny and a crooked smile by people who do. My mother named me James. Nobody calls me James."
- "The Closer. Because I close things — jobs, doors, mouths. The name is the story."

---

### Question 2 — The Before

Nobody starts as a fixer. The fixer used to be something else — a cop, a soldier, a lawyer, a criminal with ambitions, a man who worked an honest trade until the trade stopped being honest. What they were before explains what they're willing to do now and what they refuse to become.

_What were you before? And what was the job, the moment, or the decision that made you this instead?_

Quick-start answers:
- "A cop. Homicide, second precinct. The moment was a case I solved and a conviction that didn't happen. I learned who owns the justice system. Then I stopped pretending I didn't know."
- "A defense attorney. I was good at it. The decision was a client I got acquitted who went on to do something I can't talk about at three in the morning. I decided to work on the other side of the problem."
- "A soldier. The job was clean until it wasn't. The decision was refusing an order. The army decided what I was after that. I decided what I'd become."

---

### Question 3 — The Vice

Every noir protagonist has one. Booze is classic. Gambling, women, the specific recklessness that comes from spending too long around violent people — all of these work. The vice is not a flaw in the moralistic sense. It's the thing that keeps the fixer human, keeps them from becoming purely functional, and occasionally puts them in a room they shouldn't be in.

_What is it? And how much has it already cost?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Bourbon. Good bourbon, specifically — the kind I couldn't afford when I needed it and can afford now that I shouldn't have it. It's cost me two relationships and one clean shot I should have made."
- "Gambling. Not the money — the moment. The instant before the card turns. It's cost me enough to fill this room and I'd do it again tonight."
- "The wrong kind of loyalty. I stay too long with people who are going to get me killed. It's not a substance — it's worse. It's a character flaw that looks like a virtue."

---

### Question 4 — The Code

One rule. Non-negotiable. Arrived at through experience, not principle.

_What is it? And is there a specific moment — a specific job, a specific face — that made it the rule?_

Quick-start answers:
- "No women, no children. Not original, but I arrived at it the hard way. A job in the Hollows, six years ago. A face I still see when I close my eyes at the wrong time of night."
- "I don't fix a fix. If someone hires me to clean up another fixer's mess, the answer is no. I learned this from a man named Grieves, who took that job and is no longer with us."
- "Whoever pays me, I finish the job. Even when the job changes shape. Even when the client turns ugly. My word is the only currency I have that doesn't depreciate."

---

### Question 5 — The Price

Everyone has one. The fixer knows this.

_What's yours? And does anyone in Dust City know it?_

Quick-start answers:
- "My daughter. She lives upstate with her mother. She thinks I work in insurance. One person in Dust City knows, and that person is the reason I can never fully retire."
- "A quiet bar on the east side of the river where nobody knows what I do. It's the only place I go where I'm just a man drinking coffee. If someone found it, I'd have to leave the city."
- "The truth about what happened to Leo Vance. Everyone thinks he left town. I know where he is. That knowledge is worth exactly as much as my silence, and someone in this city is going to figure that out."

## DUST CITY'S PEOPLE — OPENING CAST

**Vela Rose** Owner of the Lux. Forty years in the Corridor. She has outlasted four mayors, two Outfit transitions, and a fire that destroyed half the Strip in a year nobody likes to name. She is the closest thing Dust City has to a neutral party — she serves everyone and belongs to no one. She knows the fixer. She has kept things for them. She will call in that keeping someday. _CON +5, CHA +4, WIS +4. Not armed. Doesn't need to be._

**Aldis** The Outfit's head. Sixties, heavy, slow-moving in the way of a man who stopped needing to move fast thirty years ago. He meets people at a restaurant called Catello's on Tuesday afternoons. He orders the same thing every week. He has had three men killed this year that the fixer knows of. Possibly more. He is not a villain in his own self-understanding — he runs a business, and the business requires certain decisions. _CON +5, WIS +5, CHA +3. Surrounded by people with STR +5._

**Lieutenant Doyle Carver** Dust City PD, Homicide. Twenty-year man. Not entirely bought — more like rented, with specific terms. He and the fixer have a working relationship that requires both of them to pretend it doesn't exist in certain company. He is tired. He drinks more than he used to. He is one bad night from doing something that changes both their situations permanently. _INT +3, CON +3, STR +3. Armed. Complicated._

**Cass Moreau** Reporter, the Evening Standard. Thirties, relentless, operating on instinct and coffee and the conviction that the story she's pulling at matters. She has three sources who are frightened and one who has gone quiet in a way that frightens her. She doesn't know the fixer yet. She will within the first session. Whether that's good or bad for either of them depends on decisions neither has made yet. _INT +4, CHA +3, CON +2. No leverage. That's the most dangerous thing about her._

**The Archbishop, Cael Voss** A man doing God's work in a city that has given up on God in every practical sense. He runs the shelters, the programs, the infrastructure of mercy that keeps the Flats from becoming something that would require an army. He knows things about Dust City that confessionals produce. He has never used them. That restraint has a cost, and someone may be about to present him with an invoice. _CHA +4, INT +3, CON +4. No muscle. Enormous moral authority in certain rooms._

**The Client (name pending)** They called. The voice was controlled. The kind of controlled that comes from practice keeping panic from showing in the throat. They have money and a problem and the sense to know that calling the police would make the problem worse. They are not telling the whole truth. They never do — not in the first call, not in the second. The whole truth arrives when it's too late to walk away.

## THE OPENING — TWO IN THE MORNING

The phone rings.

The fixer is wherever they are at two in the morning — which says something about them, and the question of where that is comes out of character creation.

The voice on the line is a man named **Councilman Edro Pell.** The fixer knows the name. Pell sits on the budget committee, has a wife who appears at charity functions, has a son at a good school. He has called through an intermediary — there's a number the intermediary gave him, the number you give when you need the kind of problem that doesn't get reported.

His voice is controlled. His control is failing.

_"There's a situation,"_ he says. _"I need it handled before morning. I was told you handle situations."_

He gives an address. A building in the Commercial Belt — a hotel, but not the kind with a concierge. The kind where the desk clerk looks at the floor and the room keys have no numbers.

_"Room fourteen,"_ he says. _"I need you to make it go away."_

He doesn't say what _it_ is. They never do, the first time.

---

## WHAT'S IN ROOM FOURTEEN

Two bodies. One of them is a man in his late twenties — good suit, soft hands, not someone who worked for his money. The other is a woman, mid-thirties, professional in a way that the Corridor would recognize.

The man is **Cal Pell** — the councilman's son. He is alive. Badly beaten, possibly drugged, sitting in a corner with his jacket over his face and his hands shaking.

The dead woman is **Minna Voss** — niece of the Archbishop. She has been dead for approximately three hours. The cause requires a closer look but it wasn't natural.

The other dead person is a cop. **Sergeant Ran Hessel**, vice division, twelve-year man, known to the fixer as someone who was on the Outfit's payroll on at least two occasions. He has been shot once, close range. The gun is not in the room.

Cal Pell says he didn't do it. He says he came to meet someone. He says he doesn't know what happened. He is saying this to the wall.

Councilman Pell wants his son out of the room and the room made not to exist by morning. He does not know about Sergeant Hessel. He does not know about Minna Voss's connection to the Archbishop. He may not have known who Minna was before tonight or he may be lying about that along with everything else.

---

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS

**Why they're both dead:** Minna Voss was not what she appeared. She was running information between the Archbishop's office and the reporter Cass Moreau — feeding the story Moreau has been pulling at. The story involves the Outfit, specific city councilmen, and a connection between vice money and the last three municipal elections. Minna had documents. The documents are not in the room. Someone took them, which means someone knew she had them and knew she'd be in this room tonight.

Sergeant Hessel was the Outfit's surveillance on the meeting. He wasn't supposed to be visible. He became visible because something went wrong, and what went wrong is that the person who arranged the meeting also told the Outfit about it, and that person is someone the fixer will not immediately suspect.

**Cal Pell** is genuinely innocent of the killings. He was there to buy something from Minna — personal material, the kind a young man with a rich father and poor judgment accumulates. He was drugged when the killings happened. He saw something, partly, through the drug haze. He doesn't know he saw it. It will surface.

**The documents Minna was carrying** contain the name of the person who has been brokering Outfit money into city elections for nine years. That name will destroy the councilman, two judges, a police captain, and someone else — someone the fixer has a history with, one way or another. The documents are now in the hands of whoever arranged this. That person will appear to be the fixer's ally before they appear to be the problem.

**The thread:** The simple job — make the room go away — is the first pull on a thread that runs through the Outfit, City Hall, the Archbishop's office, the press, and the police. The fixer cannot make the room go away cleanly. The room is already too complicated. Every solution to one part of it creates a problem in another. By the time the fixer understands the full shape of it, they will be inside it.

## FIRST SESSION SHAPE

1. **The phone call.** The address. The controlled voice failing. The fixer decides whether to take the job and on what terms.
2. **The room.** Two bodies, one shaking rich kid, and approximately four hours until the hotel's morning shift starts asking why room fourteen is quiet.
3. **The first decision.** What gets moved, what gets left, what gets called in. Every choice here leaves a trace.
4. **The first complication.** Someone else knows about the room — either they're already watching the building, or they were in the building, or they get a call in the next hour that tells them something happened. The fixer finds out they're not the only person working this problem tonight.
5. **The morning.** The session ends at dawn. The room is handled or it isn't. Either way, the fixer knows more than they did at two in the morning and is now part of something they can't unknow themselves from.
