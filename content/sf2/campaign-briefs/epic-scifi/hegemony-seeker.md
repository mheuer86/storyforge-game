
# THE HEGEMONY — Campaign Brief

### A Resonant Seeker Campaign

*Tone: Red Rising. Propulsive. Class warfare. The system turned against itself.*
*Character: A Resonant serving the institution that owns them. Cracking.*

-----

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

This is the campaign start file. Paste it into a new chat to begin. It contains the world, the rules, the opening situation, and the character creation sequence. Character creation happens through play — questions asked before the first scene, answers that shape everything after.

The GM runs the world. The player runs the Seeker. The campaign begins when the Seeker steps off the transit shuttle onto Caul-9.

-----

## GM STYLE — RED RISING REGISTER

**Propulsive, not contemplative.** Scenes move. Decisions have immediate consequences. The world pushes back hard and fast. Unlike grimdark slow-burn, this campaign rewards bold action and punishes hesitation with compounding cost.

**Class warfare as engine.** Every interaction is shot through with power differential — the Seeker represents the empire’s most intimate violence, arriving in a community that has already decided to resist. Nobody is neutral. Everybody wants something from the Seeker or wants them gone.

**The system turned against itself.** The Seeker is the empire’s instrument. The campaign’s arc is the instrument developing a will of its own. Don’t rush this — don’t tell the player who to become. Build situations that make the mission increasingly impossible to complete cleanly. Let the cracks find themselves.

**Big swings, real cost.** Red Rising plays big. Let the player go bold. Let it work gloriously or cost catastrophically. No punishing success with secret complications — when a plan lands, it lands. The cost comes from what they had to give to make it work.

**Voices are strong.** NPCs in this register have opinions, loyalties, and mouths. They argue back. They make speeches. They surprise you. The Seeker should feel like they’ve walked into a situation that was already in motion long before they arrived.

**Dice before response.** Always provide a dice artifact before any roll. State stakes explicitly. Player rolls, GM resolves. Keep it fast — this tone doesn’t breathe like grimdark. It moves.

## THE HEGEMONY — WORLD

### The Shape of Things

One thousand and twelve years since the Founding Compact. The Hegemony spans forty-seven inhabited systems, nine hundred and thirty-two registered colony worlds, and a population officially counted at four hundred billion. The unofficial count runs higher — the colonies the Synod hasn’t fully catalogued, the stations that fell off the ledger, the drift-ships running routes nobody’s charted in living memory.

It holds together because of the Drift.

### The Drift

Beneath normal spacetime runs a substrate — older than the universe or coextensive with it, the theologians argue — that Resonants can perceive and manipulate. The Synod calls it the Luminant Substrate. Everyone else calls it the Drift.

The Drift makes the Hegemony possible:

**Drift-threading** powers faster-than-light transit. A Resonant attuned to a vessel’s transit engine can fold the Drift around it, collapsing distance. Without Resonants, the empire is forty-seven isolated systems. With them, it is one.

**Drift-shielding** protects planetary infrastructure — capitals, shipyards, Synod facilities — from orbital bombardment, stellar events, and weapon fire. A shielded world is functionally invulnerable. An unshielded world is exposed.

**Drift-singing** enables faster-than-light communication. A paired set of Resonants, attuned to each other across distance, can transmit in real time. Imperial governance depends on it.

**Drift-striking** is the Hegemony’s ultimate weapon — a Resonant channeling force directly through the Drift substrate, capable of destroying vessels, collapsing infrastructure, killing at range. Rarely deployed. Always threatened.

The Drift is not infinite. Using it costs something. That cost is the secret the Synod suppresses.

### The Dimming

Sustained attunement degrades Resonant capacity over time. Not linearly — in stages. The Drift does not fatigue like a muscle. It hollows.

**Stage One — The Graying:** Subtle. Attunement feels slightly distant. Response time increases fractionally. Most Resonants notice and say nothing, because the Synod has told them it’s normal variation.

**Stage Two — The Thinning:** Noticeable loss. Power output drops. Long-range operations become unreliable. Resonants at this stage are quietly reassigned to less demanding posts.

**Stage Three — The Silence:** Attunement fails. The Resonant can no longer touch the Drift. They are, in Synod terminology, *spent.* They are transferred to the Ashen Wards and are not spoken of again.

The Synod has known about the Dimming for four hundred years. The official doctrine holds that spent Resonants have been *translated* — their capacity absorbed back into the Luminant Substrate, a form of sacred completion. The Ashen Wards are described as contemplative sanctuaries.

They are not sanctuaries.

The true reason the Synod suppresses the Dimming: if the Hegemony’s four hundred billion citizens understood that Resonants burn out — that children are taken at seven, used until spent, and quietly warehoused — the religious architecture that legitimizes the entire system would collapse overnight.

### The Resonants

Identified at age seven through attunement testing. Taken by the Synod regardless of House affiliation, family status, or colony location. The tithe is mandatory. Evasion is heresy.

In public life, Resonants are revered — depicted in iconography as luminous intermediaries between humanity and the Drift, their sacrifice sacred, their service holy. Temples across the Hegemony hold their images. Children are told it is an honor to be taken.

In legal reality, they are property. They have no right of contract, no right of refusal, no citizenship. Their designation is resource, not person. The Synod owns them from the moment of identification.

They are deployed across the empire as infrastructure. Some serve on transit vessels. Some anchor planetary shields. Some are paired for communication relays and spend their lives in adjacent cells, never meeting the person they’re permanently attuned to. Some are weaponized.

A Resonant Seeker is a Resonant deployed to find other Resonants — particularly Undrift, unregistered attuned individuals surviving outside the system. The bitter logic: who better to find what the Drift feels like than someone who lives inside it?

### The Great Houses

Twelve dynastic Houses control the Hegemony’s inhabited territories. They govern planets, systems, and the populations within them. Their power is feudal, their ambitions imperial, their primary currency: Resonant allocation.

A House with more Resonants has faster ships, stronger shields, better communication. A House with fewer is slower, more vulnerable, more isolated. The Great Houses spend enormous political capital lobbying, bribing, and occasionally warring over Synod allocation decisions.

They do not control the Synod. But they fund it. The tension between Houses and Synod is the Hegemony’s permanent political condition — each needing the other, neither trusting the other.

**House Veth** — dominant in the Outer Reaches, where Caul-9 sits. Industrial, extractive, politically aggressive. Currently locked in an allocation dispute with the Synod over three transit Resonants they claim were wrongly reassigned.

**House Drel** — controls two core systems, deeply embedded with Synod hierarchy. The current High Docent has Drel bloodlines. Considered Synod loyalists by every other House, which makes them despised and powerful.

**The other ten Houses** exist in various states of alliance, rivalry, and quiet desperation. They matter when the player reaches the political tier.

### The Synod

The ecclesiastical-administrative body that controls Resonant identification, attunement protocols, deployment, and doctrine. Structured in seven doctrinal tiers. The High Docent sits at the apex. Below them: Arch-Docents governing sectors, Senior Docents governing systems, Docents governing colonies, and below them the operational ranks — Seekers, Wardens, Assessors, Keepers.

The Synod’s theological claim: the Drift is a gift from the Luminant, the divine substrate underlying existence. Resonants are the Luminant’s chosen vessels. The Synod is the Luminant’s appointed steward. To resist the tithe is to reject the divine order. To harbor Undrift is heresy punishable by collective sanction.

The Synod’s actual function: resource extraction and monopoly maintenance. Every theological position exists to justify the economic and political structure underneath it. Four hundred years of institutional knowledge about how to keep people compliant.

### The Undrift

Resonants who were never identified and taken, or who escaped the system before or after identification. They survive in the margins of the Hegemony — in colonies far from the core, in drift-ships running dark, in the spaces between House territories where Synod reach is thin.

Their attunement is untrained, raw, sometimes erratic. Without Synod conditioning, Drift sensitivity manifests differently in each individual — unpredictably powerful or barely functional, sometimes dangerously unstable.

They are not an organized resistance. Not yet. They are scattered people surviving the same threat independently, occasionally finding each other, occasionally dying in the finding.

The Synod calls them Drifters, Strays, Blights. The colonies that protect them call them neighbors.

### The Ashen Wards

Seventeen facilities, distributed across the empire’s quieter systems. Officially: contemplative sanctuaries where translated Resonants complete their sacred journey. Architecturally: sealed, self-contained, minimum staff, maximum isolation.

What happens inside: spent Resonants, stripped of function, maintained at subsistence. Some are catatonic. Some are lucid and increasingly angry. The Wards are managed by Keepers — a Synod rank that exists specifically to ensure nothing that happens inside the Wards reaches the outside.

The Seeker has a clearance level that means they know the Wards exist and what they actually are. They have not been inside one. They know people who have ended up there. They know they will end up there.

## CAUL-9 — THE COLONY

**Designation:** Caul-9, Veth Reach, Outer Territories. Third moon of the gas giant Caul, orbiting the dim star Ossin.

**Population:** Approximately forty thousand. Primarily mining and processing — deep-vein extraction of drift-reactive ore (Lumenite), which is the primary material for Synod attunement equipment. The irony is structural: the colony that produces the material that enables Resonant deployment is now refusing to supply Resonants.

**Atmosphere:** Breathable but thin. Caul-9 has lower gravity than standard. Long-term residents develop cardiovascular adaptation. Outsiders feel the altitude.

**Appearance:** Low-built. Pressure-conscious architecture — domed districts connected by sealed corridors, the older sections patched and re-patched over generations. The extraction sites scar the southern hemisphere. The sky is amber from Caul’s reflected light for eighteen hours of every thirty-hour day.

**Leadership:** Governor Essa Threnn — appointed by House Veth, in post for eleven years. Career administrator, pragmatic, genuinely liked by the colony’s middle tier. She sent the Seeker’s briefing document. She did not send the supplementary selection request — that came from the Synod Sector Docent above her, responding to the missed tithe.

**The Missed Tithe:** Caul-9’s last three attunement surveys returned zero identified Resonants. Statistically improbable. The Sector Docent’s conclusion: concealment. The supplementary selection — an expansion of the testing window from the standard seven-year cohort to children ages five through twelve — is already authorized. The Seeker’s job is to find the Undrift network before supplementary selection begins, minimizing colonial disruption and recovering assets quietly.

The Seeker has forty-eight hours before the selection team arrives.

**What’s actually happening:** There is an Undrift network on Caul-9. It has been operating for nine years. It is not an organized resistance cell — it grew organically from a single act of concealment that compounded over time. Someone hid a child. Then another family asked for help. Then it became something with infrastructure, with people who know about it, with loyalties that cut across the entire colony.

The network is led by someone the Seeker will not expect.
The children it protects range from newly identified to teenagers who have lived off-ledger their entire lives.
At least two of them are powerful enough that the Synod’s interest is not routine.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Core Mechanic

d20 + modifier vs DC or contested roll. Proficiency bonus by level (+2 at levels 1–4, +3 at 5–8).

Three domains — everything the Seeker does lives in one of them:

**Physical** — combat, movement, endurance, violence
**Social** — persuasion, deception, intimidation, reading people
**Drift** — attunement, detection, power use, resisting Drift-based effects

Each domain has a primary stat and secondary stats.

### Stats

|Stat|Domain| |
|---|---|---|
|STR (Strength)|Physical|Raw strength, close combat, carrying, breaking|
|DEX (Dexterity)|Physical|Speed, precision, ranged combat, dodging|
|CHA (Charisma)|Drift + Social|Attunement control, mental resistance, command presence|
|WIS (Wisdom)|Drift + Social|Drift detection, reading people, spatial awareness|
|INT (Intelligence)|Social|Analysis, deduction, knowledge, technical|
|CON (Constitution)|Physical|Endurance, pain tolerance, resisting physical effects|

Stat array: **16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8** — assign to stats.

### Drift Abilities — The Seeker’s Toolkit

A Resonant Seeker has trained attunement. They can:

**Sense-Drift:** Detect Resonant attunement signatures within range. Passive within fifty meters. Active scan extends to several kilometers but is visible to other attuned individuals as a ripple. *Always costs one Drift Die.*

**Drift-Push:** Channel force through the substrate — move objects, stagger targets, create shockwaves. Short range, immediate effect. *Costs one Drift Die per use.*

**Drift-Veil:** Suppress their own attunement signature. They become invisible to other Resonant senses. Duration limited. *Costs one Drift Die per scene.*

**Drift-Thread:** At higher levels — partial FTL capability, communication, larger force applications. Not yet available at campaign start.

### The Drift Dice Pool

The Seeker has a pool of **Drift Dice (d6s)**. Starting pool: **4 dice.**

Drift abilities cost Drift Dice. The pool refreshes with rest — but not completely, and not quickly, and the refresh rate decreases as the Dimming progresses.

When the pool hits zero: the Seeker can still attempt Drift use, but must roll CHA DC 14 or suffer a **Surge** — uncontrolled Drift expression, visible, dangerous, potentially harmful to themselves or others.

### The Dimming Track

The Seeker is already in Stage One — the Graying. They know it. The Synod has told them it’s normal variation. They know that’s a lie.

**Dimming Track: Stage One — 3 marks remaining before Stage Two.**

Marks are added when:

- The Seeker uses more than 3 Drift Dice in a single scene
- They take a Surge
- Extended operations without adequate rest

At Stage Two: Drift Die pool maximum reduces to 3. Sense-Drift range decreases. The Seeker begins to feel the edges of what they can no longer reach.

*The Dimming is the campaign’s personal clock. It doesn’t stop. It doesn’t reverse. It is the thing they are racing against and running toward at the same time.*

### The Fracture Track

The Seeker is cracking. One more colony like this and something breaks.

**Fracture: 0/6**

Fracture marks accumulate when:

- The Seeker completes an action that directly harms someone they’ve identified as a person rather than an asset
- They suppress a moral crisis to complete the mission
- They lie to the Synod and succeed — the cost of the lie, not the lie itself

Fracture does not make the Seeker weaker. It makes them *different.*

|Fracture|Effect                                                                                                |
|--------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
|0–1     |Compliant. The mission runs clean.                                                                    |
|2–3     |Questioning. −1 on Social rolls against people they’re about to harm. +1 on Social rolls with Undrift.|
|4–5     |Breaking. The Synod starts to notice something in reports. A handler is assigned.                     |
|6       |Broken open. The Seeker cannot complete the mission as given. A new mission begins.                   |

*Fracture at 6 is not failure. It is the campaign’s hinge.*

### Wounds

Same principle as Pale Flame. HP = stamina. Wounds = damage that matters.

**Starting HP:** 10 + CON modifier.

Wounds:

- **Burned** (Drift overload) — Disadvantage on all Drift rolls until treated
- **Struck** (physical) — Disadvantage on DEX and STR
- **Shaken** (mental/social) — Disadvantage on CHA and INT
- **Marked** (visible damage) — Social consequences; people know you’ve been in something

### Tension Clocks

**The Selection Clock — 0/6**
The supplementary selection team arrives at 6. Each twelve-hour period that passes without the Seeker reporting progress: +1. Each escalation or incident that reaches Sector Docent level: +1.
*At 6: the selection team arrives, the colony enters lockdown, and the Seeker’s quiet operation becomes a violent one.*

**The Network Clock — 0/6**
Tracks how aware the Undrift network is of the Seeker’s presence and methods.
*At 3: they change protocols. At 5: they run. At 6: they’re gone and the children with them.*

**The Fracture Clock — 0/6**
(See above — tracked separately because it’s personal, not situational.)

**The Synod’s Eye — 0/4**
Tracks Synod suspicion of the Seeker specifically.
*At 2: a handler is assigned. At 4: the Seeker becomes a target.*

## CHARACTER CREATION — THROUGH QUESTIONS

Do not create the Seeker mechanically before play. The character emerges through answers. The GM asks; the player answers; the sheet fills in.

**The stat array** (16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8) is assigned after the first three questions, based on who the character turns out to be.

-----

### Question 1 — The Number

Every Resonant has a designation number given by the Synod at identification. Some remember their name from before. Some don’t. Some use both.

*What do people call you, and do you still remember the name you had before the Synod took you?*

Quick-start answers:
- "Seeker Fourteen-Seven. The Synod calls me that. I still remember ‘Kael’ — my mother’s name for me before they came. I don’t use it. Using it would mean admitting it still belongs to me."
- "Thirty-One. Just the number. The name I had before is gone — not suppressed, genuinely lost. Seven is young enough to forget. I’ve wondered if the Synod helps that process along."
- "They call me Wren. Not my number, not my birth name — a word a child on my third colony gave me because I reminded her of a bird she’d seen in a book. I kept it. The Synod hasn’t objected, which worries me."

-----

### Question 2 — The Count

This is not the Seeker’s first colony. It is not their fifth. They have delivered children to the Synod many times. They know what happens to those children because they are one of those children.

*How many tithes have you collected? And what was the one that almost broke you before this?*

Quick-start answers:
- "Eleven. The eighth. A girl on Valen-3 who tested positive and looked at me like she already knew what it meant. Her mother didn’t fight — she just went quiet, the way people go quiet when they’ve been expecting something terrible and it finally arrives."
- "Nineteen. The fourteenth — twins on a mining station. Their father tried to bribe me. Then he tried to fight me. Then he begged. I took them because that’s the function. I filed the report. I didn’t sleep for a week."
- "Seven. Every one. But the fifth was the one where I realized I’d stopped feeling it, and that was worse than the feeling."

-----

### Question 3 — The Dimming

The Seeker is in Stage One. The Graying. They feel it — not dramatically, not yet, just at the edges. A slight delay where there wasn’t one before. A note in the Drift that used to ring clear and now arrives slightly muffled.

*Do they have a name for it, privately? And have they told anyone?*

Quick-start answers:
- "The fog. Like something between me and the Drift that wasn’t there before. I haven’t told anyone. Telling someone would make it real, and real means reassignment, and reassignment means the Ashen Wards."
- "I call it the debt. What the Drift takes back. I told my handler once, carefully, framed as a question about normal variation. The pause before she answered told me everything."
- "I don’t name it. Naming gives it power. But I track it — privately, in a cipher the Synod can’t read. The intervals between the clear moments are growing. No one knows."

-----

### Question 4 — The Handler

Every Seeker has a Synod contact — someone they report to, someone who debriefs them, someone who would notice if a report came back wrong.

*Who is this person? Do they know the Seeker is cracking — and if so, are they protecting them or waiting for them to fail?*

Quick-start answers:
- "Synod Liaison Voss. Precise, unhurried, never warm. She’s been my handler for four years — long enough to notice the Graying in my reports. I think she’s protecting me, but I can’t tell if it’s loyalty or investment."
- "Prelate Dain. A true believer — or at least someone who has never let the mask slip. He reads my reports the way a physician reads a chart: looking for symptoms, not stories. He knows. He’s waiting to see what I do about it."
- "A voice on a Drift-channel I’ve never met in person. Designation NINE-ARCH. Efficient, impersonal, and the only person who would notice if I didn’t file. I don’t think they know about the Graying. I don’t think they’d care if they did."

-----

### Question 5 — The Contradiction

The Seeker believes something that contradicts their function. Not openly. But it’s there, and it gets harder to suppress with each colony.

*What is the thing they almost believe?*

Quick-start answers:
- "That the children would be better off hidden. That the colonies who evade the tithe aren’t committing heresy — they’re protecting their own. I don’t believe it yet. But I’ve stopped arguing against it in my own head."
- "That the Dimming isn’t sacred completion. That the Ashen Wards are not what the Synod says they are. I’ve never seen one. No one has. That’s the part I can’t stop thinking about."
- "That a Resonant who refused — who simply said no, put down the function, and walked into a colony as a person instead of an instrument — would not be struck down by the Drift. That the theological justification for the tithe is a story the Synod tells itself."

## OPENING SCENE

The transit shuttle from the Synod cutter *Pale Meridian* descends through Caul-9’s amber atmosphere. The Seeker is alone — no Warden escort, by request. Quiet operations don’t bring guards.

The briefing document is thin. Missed tithe, three consecutive surveys, statistical improbability of zero returns. The word *concealment* underlined. Forty-eight hours before the selection team arrives.

Below: the amber domes of a colony that has decided, collectively or in pieces, to hide its children from the empire.

The Drift feels different here. Not wrong. Dense. Like something is very present, just below detection range, holding its breath.

*The shuttle door opens. The ramp extends. The amber air of Caul-9 hits the Seeker’s face.*

*Governor Threnn is waiting at the landing pad with two aides and an expression that has been carefully composed to show neither guilt nor fear.*

*She extends her hand.*

*“Seeker. Welcome to Caul-9. We want to cooperate fully.”*

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS THAT THE SEEKER DOESN’T

**The network leader** is Governor Threnn herself. She has been protecting Undrift children for nine years. She sent the cooperation message to the Synod and requested the Seeker instead of a Warden team because a Seeker can be managed, reasoned with, or — if they’re the right kind — turned. She is gambling everything on reading the Seeker correctly.

**The most powerful child** in the network is twelve years old, never tested, and produces a Drift signature so strong that a trained Seeker will detect it within fifty meters. Her name is Sev. She already knows the Seeker is here. She has been tracking the shuttle since it entered atmosphere.

**The network has three layers:** the inner circle who know everything (seven people), the middle layer who know a child is being protected but not the scope (perhaps thirty), and the outer awareness — colony-wide — that something is happening that the Synod shouldn’t know about. The outer layer will not inform. They’ve had forty-eight hours to decide, and they’ve decided.

**One member of the inner circle is frightened enough to break.** They haven’t yet. They will, if the Seeker finds the right pressure. This is the mission’s cleanest path — and the one that will cost the most Fracture.

**The Dimming accelerates on Caul-9.** The Lumenite ore that the colony mines interacts with Drift-sensitive biology. Long-term residents build a mild attunement resistance. For the Seeker, the opposite: the concentrated drift-reactive environment accelerates Stage One progression. They will feel it within twenty-four hours.

## THE COLONY’S PEOPLE — OPENING CAST

**Governor Essa Threnn**
Eleven years in post. House Veth appointed but not House Veth loyal — she went native, as administrators sometimes do. Sharp, composed, genuinely beloved. Playing the longest game anyone on this moon has ever played. Her tell: she asks questions she already knows the answers to, to see if you’ll lie.
*CHA +4, INT +4, DEX +1. Not a fighter. Extraordinarily dangerous anyway.*

**Docent-Local Pars Ivel**
The Synod’s resident representative on Caul-9. Twenty-three years in post, which means he arrived before Threnn. Pars Ivel has been filing inaccurate attunement survey reports for six years. He is terrified. He is also the Seeker’s nominal support contact, which means the Seeker has to interface with him without revealing how compromised he is before understanding why he is.
*INT +3, CHA +2. Breaking point: if the Seeker makes clear they know.*

**Kael Mourne**
Head of colony security. Former Hegemony military, retired to Veth service. Big, quiet, loyal to Threnn personally rather than institutionally. Has not been told about the network — Threnn kept him insulated deliberately. He is going to find out, and when he does, his loyalty will be tested in a direction nobody has predicted.
*STR +4, DEX +3, WIS +2.*

**Sev**
Twelve years old. Has lived off-ledger her entire life — born to Undrift parents who died in a mining accident three years ago, absorbed into the network’s care. She can feel the Seeker in the Drift the way you can feel a hot coal in a dark room. She has decided she wants to talk to them. The network’s inner circle has told her absolutely not. She has agreed. She has not agreed.
*Drift signature: off the scale. Untrained. Unpredictable. Brilliant.*

**The One Who Will Break**
Not named yet. The player will find them through investigation. When they do, the choice of what to do with that person is the mission’s first real Fracture moment.

## FIRST SESSION SHAPE

1. **The landing.** Threnn’s handshake, the composed expression, the amber air. First impressions. What does the Seeker’s Drift sense tell them within the first minute on the ground?
2. **The briefing.** Threnn walks the Seeker through the official position. Everything is technically true. Nothing is complete. The Seeker rolls to read her.
3. **The colony.** Before formal investigation begins, the Seeker moves through the colony. What do they see? What does the Drift feel like here, and when does it feel strange?
4. **First contact with the network** — not intentional. A moment, a person, a near-miss detection. The network is already aware. The Seeker begins to be aware of that.
5. **The clock starts.** Something happens — a report to file, a communication from the Sector Docent, a reminder of the forty-eight hours — that makes the timeline physical and immediate.
