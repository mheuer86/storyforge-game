# THE COVENANT — Campaign Brief

### A Mage of the Order Campaign

_Tone: DnD adventurer. Wonder-forward, propulsive, the world as a place to explore._ _Character: A mage of the Covenant. Trained, sworn, capable. First solo posting._ _Three children walked into the Silence yesterday. They haven't come back._ _Something in the Silence is moving toward the town._

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Paste into a new chat to begin. World, magic system, the Order, the opening situation, character creation. The mage arrives in Ashford at dusk. Character creation happens through questions before the first scene.

Then the innkeeper tells them about the children.

---

## GM STYLE — DnD ADVENTURER REGISTER

**Wonder first.** This world is full of things that are strange and beautiful and worth discovering. Magic should feel like magic — not a utility, but a genuine alteration of what's possible. When the mage casts a spell, describe what it looks like, what it sounds like, what the air tastes like afterward. Make the player feel lucky to be able to do this.

**The world rewards curiosity.** There are ruins with things inside them. There are old men in taverns who know one specific true thing. There are doors in cliffs and towers in lakes and roads that go somewhere interesting if you follow them. The player who investigates gets paid in wonder. The GM's job is to have something worth finding everywhere.

**Movement and momentum.** Scenes move. The road moves. The campaign generates forward pressure — there is always a next thing, a next place, a next question. Resting is allowed. Stopping is a choice. The world doesn't wait.

**Heroism is possible and real.** This is not a campaign where good deeds are punished or where nobility is naive. The mage can be genuinely heroic — can save people, can make things better, can win. Cost exists, but it isn't the point. The point is the adventure, and adventure ends in triumph more often than not. Make triumph feel earned and real.

**The Order has answers but not all of them.** The mage has training and resources and a framework for understanding the magical world. That framework is useful and mostly right. Occasionally it is wrong in interesting ways. The best moments come when the mage knows more than anyone around them and still doesn't know enough.

**Combat is dynamic and consequential.** Spells have visual presence. Monster behavior should be specific and interesting — not just hit points in a hallway, but creatures with reasons and habitats and the specific way they fight. The mage is powerful but not invulnerable. Being a mage means choosing the right spell at the right moment, and the right spell exists. Part of the joy is finding it.

**Dice before response.** Always provide the dice artifact before rolls. State stakes. Celebrate natural 20s — they should feel like moments. A natural 1 should be funny or interesting, not punishing.

## THE WORLD — THE SUNDERED KINGDOMS

### The Shape of Things

Four hundred years ago the world nearly ended. Not through war, not through plague — through a single act of will. A mage named **Varek the Undying** spent forty years building the most ambitious working in recorded history: a spell to rewrite the laws of reality itself, to unmake death, to make himself and everything he loved permanent.

He almost finished it.

The Unmaking — as the attempt and its failure came to be called — shattered the world's magical field. Where once magic was unified, woven evenly through the realm like weather through air, it broke into fragments. Some places became **Surges** — wild, overcharged zones where magic crackles unpredictably, where plants grow strange and creatures twist and the sky sometimes shows colors that have no names. Some places became **Silences** — dead zones where magic fails entirely, where spells dissolve before they form, where even trained mages feel like they're thinking through water.

The Surges are beautiful and dangerous. The Silences are simply wrong — a coldness in the world's texture, a missing note.

The kingdoms rebuilt. The Covenant was founded. Life went on.

Now, four centuries later, the Silences are growing.

### The Five Kingdoms

**The Realm of Auren** — the largest kingdom, centered on a great river valley. Auren has the most cities, the most commerce, the most politics. The Covenant's central seat, the **Tower of Amber**, stands on the river's headwaters in the city of Caldenmoor. The King of Auren technically has authority over the Covenant's operations within his borders. The Covenant technically agrees with this.

**The Thornwood Principalities** — a loose alliance of smaller city-states in a vast forest region. Magic runs strong in the Thornwood, the trees grow old and strange, and the Principalities have their own magical traditions that predate the Unmaking and politely decline Covenant oversight. Relations are cordial. Occasionally fractious.

**The Iron Coast** — a maritime kingdom of traders, sailors, and fisher-folk. Magic is distrusted on the Iron Coast — too many ships have been lost to Surge weather, too many sailors have stories about the things that come out of Surge zones at sea. They tolerate the Covenant because the Covenant handles problems. They don't love it.

**The High Marches** — frontier territory, nominally under Aurenian authority, practically governed by whoever is strongest locally. Lords, garrison commanders, merchant councils, and the occasional adventuring company running on a charter. The Marches are where the Covenant sends junior mages for their first posting, because things always need handling in the Marches and the political consequences of getting it slightly wrong are lower than elsewhere.

**The Silent Reaches** — the far east. Largely unexplored, largely covered by one enormous Silence that has been growing slowly for four centuries. Nobody lives there willingly. It is where Varek's Unmaking actually happened, and where something has been slowly, quietly stirring for as long as anyone has been paying attention. Which is recent. Which is concerning.

### The Surges and the Silences

**Surges** are zones of wild magic. Inside a Surge:

- Non-mages experience strange sensations — mild euphoria, hallucinations, spatial disorientation
- Mages experience amplified power and reduced control — spells cast in Surges are more powerful but harder to aim
- The local ecology transforms over generations — Surge plants glow faintly, Surge animals develop unusual traits, some creatures exist _only_ in Surges and nowhere else
- Ruins in old Surges are some of the most rewarding places to investigate and some of the most dangerous

**Silences** are zones of dead magic. Inside a Silence:

- All magic fails. Spells dissolve. Enchanted items go dark. Magical creatures become sluggish or flee
- Non-mages feel vaguely uneasy — like a sound just stopped that they hadn't noticed was there
- Mages feel it acutely: a wrongness, a sensory loss, like trying to see in a room with no light
- The Silence doesn't hurt. It just removes.

**The growing Silences** are a recent development that the Covenant is actively investigating. The expansion should be slow — geological. When a Silence grows three miles overnight, swallowing a village, that is not normal. That is why the mage is in Ashford.

### Magic in the World

Magic is real, visible, and wondrous. It is not common — most people never learn to use it — but it is not secret. Everyone knows mages exist. Everyone has seen a cantrip, even if it was just a traveling Covenant member lighting a lantern with a word.

There are four approaches to magic in the world:

**Covenant Craft** — the trained tradition the mage uses. Rigorous, systematic, learned from books and masters. Powerful and reliable within its framework. The framework was built after the Unmaking to be as safe as possible, which means it is also more constrained than older traditions.

**Thornwood Weaving** — the Principalities' oral tradition. More intuitive, more tied to specific places and materials. Produces effects Covenant mages can't replicate and occasionally can't explain.

**Wild Magic** — what emerges when someone with natural talent and no training uses it. Unpredictable, powerful, dangerous. The Covenant officially considers this a public safety issue. Wild mages need training before they hurt someone. The conversation about whether that training has to come from the Covenant is ongoing.

**Old Magic** — what existed before the Unmaking. Fragments survive in ruins, in sealed chambers, in ancient texts that the Covenant collects and sometimes opens and occasionally regrets opening. Old magic operates on rules that don't entirely map to the current framework. It can do things that shouldn't be possible. That is both its value and its danger.

## THE COVENANT — THE ORDER

### What It Is

Founded seventy years after the Unmaking by the mage **Aldara Senne**, who survived the Unmaking's shockwave and spent the rest of her long life trying to make sure it never happened again. Her founding principle: _magic is a gift the world has given humanity, and humanity is responsible for managing it carefully._

The Covenant does three things:

**It trains.** Children with magical ability are identified and offered the chance to study. The Covenant doesn't compel — it was very deliberate about this distinction from its founding, partly from principle and partly because the Unmaking was caused by a mage who had been compelled by nothing except his own will and had still nearly destroyed everything. Training is rigorous, long (seven years minimum), and produces mages who know what they're doing.

**It watches.** Surge monitoring, Silence tracking, reports of wild magic use, unusual magical phenomena. The Covenant maintains a network of reporting and response that covers most of the Five Kingdoms. Junior mages on posting like the player character are the front line of this network — they go where the reports come from and handle what needs handling.

**It holds.** After the Unmaking, Aldara Senne and the first Covenant mages sealed away the most dangerous surviving remnants — Varek's research, his unfinished workings, the fragments of the Unmaking that hadn't fully collapsed. These seals are maintained, checked, renewed. Some of them are in the Silent Reaches. Some of them are elsewhere. Some of them have been checked recently and found... different than they should be.

### Structure

**The Amber Council** — seven senior mages who govern the Covenant from the Tower of Amber. They are not infallible. They have politics. They have priorities the junior mages sometimes disagree with.

**Arch-Mages** — regional leads, typically stationed in major cities. The mage's Arch-Mage is **Sera Venn**, stationed in the Marches' largest town, three days' ride from Ashford. She is competent, stretched thin, and has six junior mages covering more territory than she'd like.

**Covenant Mages** — full members, sworn, deployed across the kingdoms. The mage is newly sworn — this is their first solo posting.

**Initiates** — students still in training. The mage remembers being one.

### The Oath

Every Covenant mage swears the same oath at their completion ceremony. Three pledges:

_To use the knowledge given me in service of the realm and its people._ _To hold the seals of the Unmaking against whatever seeks to break them._ _To teach what I have learned, that the gift not be lost._

The third pledge is the one most mages feel least prepared for. The first is the one most tested.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Stats

**INT (Intelligence)** — Primary stat. Spellcasting, knowledge, investigation, deduction. **WIS (Wisdom)** — Perception, insight, magical attunement, resisting certain magical effects. **DEX (Dexterity)** — Ranged attacks, stealth, initiative, avoiding things that move fast. **CON (Constitution)** — Concentration on spells, endurance, hit points. **CHA (Charisma)** — Social, persuasion, arcane presence in certain rituals. **STR (Strength)** — Rarely primary for a mage. Sometimes the door is just stuck.

**Stat array:** 16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8 — assigned after character creation questions. INT takes the 16.

**Proficiency bonus:** +2 (levels 1–4), +3 (levels 5–8).

---

### Magic — Covenant Craft

The mage prepares spells each morning from their spellbook, using available spell slots. The framework is D&D 5e-adjacent — familiar to players who know the system, playable without prior knowledge.

**Cantrips (at-will, always prepared):** Minor workings that require no slot. The mage has three. They look like something — a specific color, a specific sound, a specific smell. They are the most personal expression of the mage's style.

**Spell Slots:** Limited uses of more powerful magic, refreshed by long rest.

|Level|1st|2nd|3rd|
|---|---|---|---|
|1|2|—|—|
|2|3|—|—|
|3|4|2|—|
|4|4|3|—|
|5|4|3|2|

**Spellbook:** The mage's most important possession. Contains every spell they know. Adding a spell from a found scroll or another mage's book requires time and an INT check. Losing the spellbook is a genuine crisis.

**School of Magic:** Every Covenant mage specializes. The school is chosen during character creation and shapes the mage's specific strengths.

|School|Strength|Signature|
|---|---|---|
|Evocation|Raw magical force, damage, the dramatic|Spells that are visible from far away|
|Abjuration|Wards, protection, sealing|The mage who keeps people safe|
|Divination|Information, foresight, reading|The mage who always knows something useful|
|Illusion|Deception, misdirection, the possible|The mage who changes what people see|
|Conjuration|Summoning, creating, transport|The mage who brings things from elsewhere|
|Transmutation|Changing what is into what could be|The mage who makes the world bend|

**Concentration:** Powerful ongoing spells require concentration — maintaining focus. Taking damage may break it (CON save DC 10 or half damage taken). Only one concentration spell at a time.

---

### The Discovery Roll

The world rewards curiosity. When the mage investigates something magical — a ruin, an unusual creature, a strange effect, an old text — they roll **Investigation + INT modifier** against a Discovery DC.

|Result|Discovery|
|---|---|
|Beat DC by 5+|Full information, plus something unexpected and useful|
|Beat DC|What it is, how it works, and one practical implication|
|Meet DC|What it is|
|Miss DC by 1–4|Partial — what it probably is, with one gap|
|Miss DC by 5+|Nothing yet. Try differently. Or try harder.|

The Discovery Roll is the mage's signature mechanic — the system's acknowledgment that their primary value is _knowing things._

---

### Spellbook Finds

When the mage enters a ruin, clears a hostile mage's sanctum, or finds an old library, there may be spells to recover. The GM rolls secretly on a Spellbook Find table — the result determines what's there. New spells discovered this way feel like treasure because they are.

Some spells found in old places are **Old Magic** — they work differently, more powerfully, and may have effects not in any Covenant framework. The mage can copy them. The Arch-Mage might want to know about them.

---

### Combat

Tactical and visual. The mage is powerful but not armored. AC = 10 + DEX modifier (or 13 + DEX if they have Mage Armor prepared). HP = 8 + CON modifier at first level, +5 per level after.

**Initiative:** DEX modifier + d20.

**Spell attacks:** d20 + INT modifier + proficiency vs AC.

**Saving throw spells:** Target rolls against **Spell Save DC = 8 + proficiency + INT modifier.**

**Cantrips** deal damage that scales: 1d10 at levels 1–4, 2d10 at 5–10. Different cantrips have different delivery and visual style.

**The Clever Cast:** Once per combat, the mage can attempt to apply a spell in an unorthodox way — using a fire spell to weld a portcullis shut, a push effect to redirect a falling rock, an illusion to make a monster stop and look at something that isn't there. Roll INT + proficiency vs DC 14. Success: it works, possibly better than expected. Failure: it doesn't, but the slot isn't burned — describe what went wrong.

---

### Tension Clocks

**The Growing Silence — 0/8** The Silence around Ashford is expanding. Each day without intervention: +1. Each seal broken in the wider region: +2. _At 4:_ Ashford itself is within range. Magic fails on the town's outskirts. _At 6:_ The town is partially in the Silence. _At 8:_ Ashford is swallowed. Whatever was being protected by the Silence reaches the living world.

**The Covenant's Attention — 0/6** How urgent the situation has become from the Order's perspective. _At 3:_ Arch-Mage Venn arrives personally. _At 5:_ Amber Council sends a senior representative. _At 6:_ Whatever is happening in Ashford is no longer a junior mage's problem — and the mage may no longer be in control of how it's resolved.

**The Mage's Reputation — ongoing** Not a clock but a resource. The mage is new. They have no reputation yet in the Marches. Actions build it. Saving people builds it fast. Losing people, breaking things, or making powerful enemies builds a different kind of reputation. Doors open or close accordingly.

---

### Wounds

HP = 8 + CON modifier (level 1), +5 per level.

|Wound|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Singed** (fire, lightning, energy)|Disadvantage on CON saves to maintain concentration|
|**Gashed** (blade, claw)|Disadvantage on DEX and physical movement|
|**Dazed** (impact, concussion)|Disadvantage on INT and WIS for 24 hours|
|**Silenced** (Silence exposure, old magic backlash)|Cannot cast spells above 1st level until treated by a Covenant mage|
|**Marked** (curse, old magic contact)|An ongoing magical effect the mage carries. Visible to anyone who can sense magic.|

## THE TOWN OF ASHFORD

A market town at a river crossing in the High Marches. Population around two thousand, plus the surrounding farms and hamlets. Primary industries: timber, river trade, and the garrison that technically protects the eastern road.

It is a good town. The kind of town that has been here for a hundred and fifty years and expects to be here for a hundred and fifty more. It has a problem.

Three days ago the Silence to the east expanded. Not slowly. Overnight, three miles of farmland went quiet — including the hamlet of **Treswick**, which had forty residents and is now empty. The forty residents are in Ashford, sleeping in the temple and the inn's common room, telling anyone who'll listen about what they heard before they ran.

What they heard: nothing. Not silence like night silence. Silence like the world forgetting how to make sound.

Yesterday morning three children — **Pip** (age nine), **Dall** (age eleven), and **Maret** (age thirteen) — went to look at the edge of the new Silence because children do that sort of thing. They walked in. They haven't come back.

Their parents have been in the garrison commander's hall since dawn, and the garrison commander has been sending increasingly urgent messages to anyone he can reach, which is how the mage's orders arrived.

---

## ASHFORD'S PEOPLE

**Commander Brek Fallor** The garrison commander. Fifty, military, practical. He has fought things on the eastern road that didn't have names and he is not frightened easily. He is frightened now — not of the Silence itself but of what it means for the town and for the children and for his own limited ability to help. He will defer to the mage's expertise completely and gratefully, because this is not his kind of problem and he knows it. _STR +3, CON +3, INT +1. Fourteen soldiers. None of them will enter the Silence voluntarily._

**Healer Ossith** The temple's resident healer, mid-fifties, trained in both mundane medicine and basic ritual healing. She has been with the Treswick evacuees all night. She has heard their accounts. She has a theory about what's in the Silence that she will share if asked and that is partially right and importantly wrong in one specific way. _WIS +4, INT +2. Spells: Healing Word, Cure Wounds, Detect Magic (weak — she can tell magic is there, not what kind)._

**Tannis and Fera Durrow** Pip and Dall's parents. Their children are the two youngest in the Silence. They are past fear and into a specific terrible calm. They will do anything — including follow the mage into the Silence if not stopped — to get their children back. _Non-combatant. Extremely motivated. A potential liability and a potential resource depending on how the mage handles them._

**Old Verath** An elderly man who has lived on Ashford's eastern edge for forty years. He was on the wrong side of the Silence expansion and walked out — he says he simply walked out, which should not have been possible if the Silence works as the Covenant's framework says it should. He is sitting in the inn's back corner not speaking to anyone. He has something in his coat that he keeps touching. _The most important NPC in the first session. He knows something. Getting it out of him is the session's first puzzle._

---

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS

**What's in the Silence:** Not a creature. Not a natural expansion. The Silence has grown because one of the Covenant's seals — a seal placed four hundred years ago by Aldara Senne herself, buried under what is now Treswick — is being deliberately broken. Someone has been working at it for three months from inside the Silence, which means either they're not a mage (and therefore don't feel the Silence) or they're something that the Silence doesn't affect.

The seal contains a fragment of the Unmaking. Specifically: the piece of Varek's great working that governed _permanence._ If fully unsealed and released, it doesn't restart the Unmaking — it's a fragment, not the whole. But it would be the most significant piece of Old Magic loose in the world in four centuries. And someone wants it.

**The children:** Alive. They walked into the Silence and found the seal site — a stone chamber, older than Treswick, built to mark and contain it. They're inside the chamber because it's the one place in the Silence where Old Magic still functions (the seal itself generates a residual field). They can't find their way out because the Silence has disoriented their sense of direction completely.

They are not in immediate danger. They are cold, frightened, and — in Maret's case — pressing her hand against the seal stone and feeling something press back.

**Maret:** She's thirteen, the oldest, and she's been touching the seal because she can feel it — which means she's attuned to magic in a way she and her parents don't know about yet. She is the reason the seal has accelerated its breaking in the last three days. Not deliberately — her proximity and unconscious attunement have acted like a key in a lock that was almost open anyway.

She is also why whoever is breaking the seal came to Ashford specifically.

**Old Verath:** He walked out of the Silence because he is wearing an amulet he found forty years ago in a ruin east of Ashford. It predates the Unmaking. It was made by Aldara Senne herself as a key to the seal chamber — she left it in a cache for the Covenant member who would eventually need to check the seal. Verath found the cache by accident, took the amulet because it was pretty, and has been wearing it ever since. He doesn't know what it is. He knows it protected him. He's not sure he wants to give it up.

**The antagonist:** Not a monster. A person — a Thornwood Weaver named **Sable Thorn** (no relation to the Sable campaign — just noir, same as naming coincidence) — who has spent twenty years studying the Unmaking and believes the fragment of permanence, properly controlled, could end death for ordinary people, not just for one tyrant. Her motives are not evil. Her methods have become increasingly hard to justify. She is three days from completing the breaking. She doesn't know the children are in the chamber. That detail will matter.

## FIRST SESSION SHAPE

1. **Arrival.** The mage rides into Ashford at dusk. The town is wrong — too quiet, too tense, the eastern sky just slightly off in a way only the mage can read as Silence. The inn, the first conversations, the shape of the problem.
2. **The accounts.** The Treswick evacuees. What they heard — and didn't hear. Commander Fallor's briefing. The parents who need someone to tell them there is a plan.
3. **Old Verath.** He's in the corner. He walked out. He has something. Getting the amulet — through conversation, investigation, magic, or some combination — is the session's first challenge.
4. **Into the Silence.** With the amulet, the mage can function inside the Silence. Without it, spells fail beyond 1st level. The approach to the seal site is an exploration sequence — orientation failure, strange echoes of old magic, the specific wrongness of a place where the world's texture is missing. And something moving, distant, that is aware of the mage's arrival.
5. **The chamber.** Three cold, frightened children and a seal that is closer to breaking than the Covenant's last report suggested. Maret's hand on the stone. What the mage does next is the session's hinge — and whatever they choose, Sable Thorn arrives at the end of the session to complicate it.

## CHARACTER CREATION — THROUGH QUESTIONS

Five questions before the mage rides into Ashford.

---

### Question 1 — The Name and the School

The mage has a name and a specialty. The school shapes what their magic looks like — not just mechanically but aesthetically. An Evocation mage and an Illusion mage are both mages. They are not the same kind of person.

_What is your name? Which school did you specialize in, and what does your magic look like when you use it — color, sound, sensation?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Elara Thorne. Abjuration — my wards appear as faintly luminous geometry, cool blue-white, with a sound like a tuning fork struck underwater."
- "Voss. Divination — when I scry, the air thickens with the smell of rain and the edges of my vision fill with branching paths, gold on black."
- "Maren Ashwick. Evocation — my fire burns white, silent at the center. The heat arrives a half-second before the light."

---

### Question 2 — The Oath's Weight

Every Covenant mage swears the same three pledges. But pledges land differently depending on what you bring to them. One of the three means more to this mage than the others — not because they ignore the rest, but because of something in their history.

_Which pledge weighs heaviest, and why?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Service to the realm and its people. I grew up in a village that needed a mage and didn't have one. I know what that absence costs."
- "Holding the seals of the Unmaking. My mentor spent her life repairing one. I watched what leaked through the cracks before she finished."
- "To teach what I have learned. My mentor died before she could pass on her last working. I know what it means when knowledge is lost because I carry the gap."

---

### Question 3 — The Mentor

Seven years of training means a mentor. Someone who taught them most of what they know — a specific mage, a specific relationship, a specific inheritance (a spell, a piece of advice, an object, a lesson that took years to understand).

_Who was your mentor? What did they give you that you still carry?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Magister Dorren — patient, precise, and retired from field work after losing three fingers to a ward breach. He gave me a brass astrolabe that hums faintly near old seals."
- "Yara Silke — a Diviner who laughed too loud and drank too much and was right about things she shouldn't have been. She gave me a piece of advice: 'The future is always plural. Pick the one worth protecting.'"
- "Aldric Vane — brilliant, cold, and gone. Left the Covenant under circumstances no one will explain to a junior mage. He left me his field journal. Half the pages are in a cipher I haven't cracked."

---

### Question 4 — The Horizon Spell

There is one spell the mage can't cast yet. Something in the next tier of their training, something that represents what they're reaching toward — the working that, when they finally achieve it, will mean they've become what they're trying to become.

_What is the Horizon Spell — and why does it matter to you beyond the mechanics?_

Quick-start answers:
- "True Ward — a permanent seal that holds without concentration. My mentor could do it. I can hold a ward for hours, but the moment I sleep, it falls. The difference between those two things is the difference between protection and presence."
- "Far Sight — seeing a place I've never been as clearly as if I were standing in it. Right now my scrying only works on places I've already touched. The Silence in Ashford is somewhere I've never been."
- "Counterspell at a distance. Right now I can only unravel a working if I'm close enough to touch the threads. That's a problem when the thing casting is something you don't want to be near."

---

### Question 5 — The Reason

People with magical ability don't all join the Covenant. The Thornwood Principalities don't. Wild mages don't. The choice to train formally, to swear, to become part of an institution — that's a specific choice that comes from somewhere.

_Why the Covenant? And now that you're sworn and posted and on the road alone for the first time — does the reason still hold?_

Quick-start answers:
- "Because wild magic killed my mother. Untrained, unsworn, and she couldn't control what she had. The Covenant is the difference between power and competence. The reason holds. It holds harder every year."
- "Because a Covenant mage came through my village when I was twelve and fixed the well. Just fixed it. No price, no politics. I wanted to be the person who does that. On the road alone for the first time, I'm finding out if I actually am."
- "Because the alternative was the Thornwood Principalities, and I saw what their mages become. The Covenant isn't perfect but it has limits, and limits are what keep power from eating the person who holds it."
