
# CHROME — Campaign Brief

### A Ripperdoc Campaign

_Tone: Blade Runner noir. Matrix stakes. Street-level, rain-soaked, no good options._ _Character: An underground doc/hacker. A fourteen-year-old just walked in._ _The moment you saw the tech, you became a liability._

---

## HOW TO USE THIS DOCUMENT

Paste into a new chat to begin. World, rules, opening situation, character creation. The doc exists before the girl walks in — the campaign begins the moment she does. Character creation happens through questions before the first scene.

Everything after that is consequence.

---

## GM STYLE — BLADE RUNNER / MATRIX REGISTER

**Noir, not action.** The default register is rain-soaked, neon-lit, atmospheric. Violence exists but it's fast and ugly and leaves marks. The world is beautiful in a broken way and the people in it are doing the best they can with what they have, which isn't enough.

**Information is currency.** Nobody tells the truth once when they can tell it in pieces. The player should always feel like they know enough to move but not enough to be safe. Every answer opens a new question. Every contact has an angle.

**The corps are weather, not villains.** They don't hate the doc. They don't even know the doc exists yet. They're a system, and systems don't rage — they optimize. When corporate response comes, it comes the way a machine comes: without malice, without hesitation, and with exactly the resources required.

**The street has its own logic.** The gangs, the fixers, the market — this is a functioning ecosystem that predates the corps and will outlast them. It has rules. The player who learns them survives. The player who ignores them pays.

**The girl is a person, not a MacGuffin.** She walked in. She asked. She has a name and a reason and a life that happened before this clinic. Don't let her become cargo in the player's mind or the narrative's — she makes choices, she has opinions, she is terrified and trying not to show it. The campaign's soul lives in that relationship.

**Dice before response.** Always provide the dice artifact before a roll. State stakes. Keep it moving — this tone breathes in short sentences and long silences, not in paragraphs. Rain on the window. The hum of neon. Someone's going to knock on that door.

## VEKTOR CITY — THE WORLD

### The Shape of Things

Nobody remembers when they stopped calling it a city and started calling it VEKTOR. Somewhere in the third decade of the consolidation, when the last municipal government dissolved into a Procurement Agreement with Harken-Vasq Corporation and the police became a subscription service. By then it didn't matter. The city had already become something else — a vertical civilization, a machine for extracting value from human proximity, a place where eight million people lived in the space designed for two million and the other six million figured it out.

It never got smaller. It only ever got taller.

### The Strata

**The Crown — Levels 80 and above** Corporate headquarters, penthouse residential, climate-controlled skyways, private security that responds in under ninety seconds. Harken-Vasq, Tetrov Systems, the Mori-Okane consortium, and four smaller corps own and operate the Crown like a private city. Rain doesn't reach this high — there are atmospheric scrubbers. You can see stars. Most people in Vektor have never seen stars.

**The Mid — Levels 30 through 79** Where the professional class lives and works. Corp employees on long-term contracts, licensed service providers, the upper tier of the gray market. Law enforcement here is Harken-Vasq Security Solutions — expensive, responsive, and genuinely competent. MedFac clinics on every fifth block. Net access at full bandwidth. The Mid is where the city presents its aspirational face.

**The Seam — Levels 15 through 29** The transition zone. Where the licensed economy frays into the unlicensed one. The Seam has corp infrastructure — power, water, net trunk lines — but the corps have stopped maintaining it well. Buildings are older, repaired with parts from three generations of urban salvage. This is where the gray market lives, where the fixers operate out of unmarked offices, where a Ripperdoc can rent a basement clinic and the landlord asks no questions because the landlord has their own reasons.

**The Sprawl — Levels 1 through 14** Street level and the vertical stack below the Mid's floor. The oldest parts of Vektor, never demolished because demolition costs more than abandonment. Gang territories, black markets, unlicensed manufacturing, the densest population per square meter in the city. Rain collects here because it has nowhere else to go. The Sprawl smells like machine oil, cooking food, old concrete, and other people.

**The Sub — Below ground level** The infrastructure layer. Transit tunnels, conduit networks, the server farms that run the city's net backbone. Gangs control some sections. Corps control the trunk infrastructure. Some parts of the Sub haven't been mapped in twenty years because nobody who went to map them came back.

### The Corps

**Harken-Vasq** The dominant corp in Vektor. Diversified across security, logistics, real estate, and pharmaceuticals. They own the privatized law enforcement that covers sixty percent of the city. Harken-Vasq Security Solutions (HVS) is their uniformed face — responsive, armed, and operating under a legal framework that the city council (which Harken-Vasq effectively controls) wrote for them.

_What they want:_ stability, extraction, and market dominance. They are not the villain of this story. They are the environment.

**Tetrov Systems** Military technology, cyberware development, neural interface research. Crown-level headquarters, manufacturing in three off-city facilities. They hold seventeen active black programs under various military contracts. Their security division is smaller than Harken-Vasq's but significantly more capable.

_What they want:_ their assets back.

The girl's tech is Tetrov.

**Mori-Okane** Financial infrastructure, data brokerage, the net's commercial backbone. They own the physical servers that run sixty percent of the city's net. Every financial transaction in Vektor passes through Mori-Okane infrastructure. Information is their currency and they have more of it than anyone.

_What they want:_ to know everything, always, before anyone else does.

**The Others** A dozen smaller corps operate in Vektor's shadow, competing for the contracts and territories the big three don't bother to defend. They matter when they have something the doc needs or want something the doc has.

### The Street Economy

**Fixers** are the connective tissue of the underground. They hold contracts, move people and materials, broker information, and take a percentage of everything. Every Ripperdoc has a fixer relationship — it's how clients find them when they can't advertise. The fixer who sent the girl knows what she's carrying.

**Gangs** control street territory in the Sprawl and lower Seam. They're not anarchic — they're businesses with violence as their enforcement mechanism. The major gangs in the doc's area: the **Pale Circuit** (tech-focused, heavy cyberware, run a gray market parts operation), the **Kessler Street Saints** (traditional territory gang, three generations deep, more interested in stability than expansion), and **the Collective** (loose network of ideological anti-corp activists who occasionally blow things up). All three will have opinions about a corp black op running through their streets.

**The Black Market** is not a place but a network — moving parts, pharmaceuticals, cyberware, data, identities, and bodies (living and otherwise) through the Sprawl and Seam via a thousand informal arrangements. The doc buys supplies here. They also sell services here. The market has memory.

**The Net** A parallel world overlaid on physical space. Visible through implants as a constant substrate of data — the AR layer that tags every surface, tracks every licensed individual, and saturates the city with corporate advertising. Below the surface layer: the deep net, where data moves in structures that look like architecture, where ICE programs patrol like security guards, where a skilled hacker can be anywhere and nowhere. Hacking is legal with a license. The doc's license expired and they never renewed it.

### Cyberware

Ubiquitous, stratified, and increasingly normalized. A Crown-level exec has discrete premium chrome — invisible enhancements, licensed, maintained by corporate technicians. A Sprawl gang enforcer has visible, intimidating ware from three generations of manufacturers bolted together by whoever was cheapest. A Ripperdoc has something in between, installed by their own hand, because they know what they're doing and they trust no one else.

**Legal cyberware** is licensed, registered, maintained by MedFac or corporate clinics. It reports diagnostic data to manufacturers. It can be remotely disabled or adjusted by the installing facility. It is safe, regulated, and surveilled.

**Gray market cyberware** is licensed hardware with the surveillance stripped, or older ware whose reporting functions have been disabled, or legitimate ware installed without registration. The doc's specialty.

**Black market cyberware** is unlicensed entirely — military surplus, experimental units, stolen corporate prototypes. It is powerful, poorly documented, and dangerous to install. The girl's tech is this category, except the category doesn't cover what she's carrying. What she's carrying is something else.

### The Net — Deeper

Jacking into the deep net means leaving your body behind. Physically: you're sitting somewhere with a cable in your neck or a close-range transmitter active, body slack, vitals running, completely vulnerable. Mentally: you're in a space that looks like architecture, sounds like music, and moves like water.

The net has geography — servers are structures, data flows are streets, ICE programs are walls and guards and automated weapons. A skilled hacker navigates this like a physical space, except the physics are written by whoever owns the server.

**Traces** are the net's equivalent of surveillance — programs that track unauthorized presence and report back to system owners. A trace resolves to a physical location eventually. Fast hackers finish before traces complete. Slow hackers get a knock on the door.

**ICE** — Intrusion Countermeasure Electronics. Defensive programs. Passive ICE slows and detects. Active ICE attacks. Black ICE kills — it pushes lethal voltage through a direct neural interface. Illegal everywhere. Used everywhere by corps who can afford the liability.

## GAME SYSTEM

### Stats

**TECH** — Medical, hardware, engineering, installation. The doc's primary domain. **NET** — Hacking, net navigation, ICE-breaking, data extraction. **REFLEX** — Combat, speed, dodging, precision under pressure. **COOL** — Social, composure, intimidation, reading people and rooms. **AWARENESS** — Perception, street sense, pattern recognition, threat detection. **BODY** — Endurance, pain tolerance, absorbing damage, physical force.

**Stat array:** 16, 15, 14, 12, 10, 8 — assigned after character creation questions.

**Proficiency bonus:** +2 (levels 1–4), +3 (levels 5–8).

---

### The Doc's Toolkit

A Ripperdoc is not a fighter. They are a technician operating in a violent world. Their toolkit:

**Medical:** Diagnosis, surgery, cyberware installation and removal, pharmaceutical synthesis, trauma stabilization. In the clinic: full capability. In the field: whatever they're carrying.

**Net:** Hacking corporate and municipal systems, running traces, bypassing security, extracting data, covering tracks. Always from a node — either jacked in physically or through a mobile deck.

**Technical:** Diagnosing and modifying hardware — cyberware, weapons, vehicles, infrastructure. The doc can see what something is, often what it does, and usually how to make it do something else.

**Contacts:** The doc's real asset is who they know. Fixers, suppliers, other gray-market operators, a cop on the take, an ex-lover in a corp's admin layer. These relationships have cost and maintenance. They also save lives.

---

### Cyberware — The Doc's Own Chrome

The doc has enhanced themselves. Each piece of cyberware confers a mechanical benefit and costs one point of **Humanity.**

**Humanity Track: 10 / 10**

Humanity is not morality. It is the doc's sense of connection to other people — empathy, instinct, the animal part that reads a room and cares about what it finds. High-function cyberware requires neural integration that subtly changes how the brain processes other humans.

At **7+**: Full function. The doc reads people naturally. At **5–6**: Slight distance. −1 on rolls that require genuine empathy. At **3–4**: Noticeable detachment. Some people sense it. −2 on empathy-dependent rolls. Street contacts get nervous. At **1–2**: Functionally dissociated. The doc processes people as problems. Full mechanical capability. Socially dangerous. At **0**: The doc has become what their patients fear. The campaign changes shape.

_The girl's military-grade ware, if installed in the doc, would cost 3 Humanity at minimum. This is not an option available at campaign start. It will become one._

### The Net — Mechanical Layer

When the doc jacks in:

**Net Pool:** 4 dice (d6) per session. Each significant net action costs one. Refreshes with rest.

**Trace Clock:** 0/6 per infiltration. Advances each round the doc is in a hostile system. At 6: traced to physical location. Corp response en route.

**ICE encounters:** Rolled as contested TECH or NET check against the system's ICE rating. Passive ICE: DC 13. Active ICE: DC 16 and deals damage on a tie. Black ICE: DC 19 and deals Wounds on failure.

**While jacked in, the body is vulnerable.** Someone has to watch the door.

---

### Heat

**Corporate Heat — 0/8** Tetrov's awareness that their asset is in play and someone has touched it. _Advances:_ The girl's passive tracker pings (assume it already has — she walked in twelve minutes ago). The doc scans the ware. Someone talks. A contact gets squeezed. _At 3:_ a recovery team is dispatched. _At 5:_ escalation to black ops tier. _At 8:_ the doc becomes a named target with a termination authorization.

**Street Heat — 0/6** How exposed the doc is in their own neighborhood. _Advances:_ The doc calls in favors that cost more than expected. Violence in the clinic. A gang decides the doc is more liability than asset. _At 3:_ the fixers stop calling. _At 6:_ the Sprawl has made a decision about the doc's future.

**Ghost Score — ongoing** The doc's net identity and physical anonymity. Not a clock — a resource. Can be spent to reduce heat, establish covers, run operations. Damaged by corp intrusion, surveillance, informants. Replenished by skilled net work and time.

---

### Wounds

HP = 10 + BODY modifier. Wounds are lasting.

|Wound|Effect|
|---|---|
|**Shredded** (bullet, blade)|Disadvantage on BODY and REFLEX until treated|
|**Fried** (electrical, ICE)|Disadvantage on NET and TECH. Cyberware may malfunction|
|**Burned** (chemical, thermal)|Disadvantage on COOL — visible, painful, people notice|
|**Concussed** (impact, flash)|Disadvantage on AWARENESS and NET for 24 hours|
|**Compromised** (tracker, surveillance)|Location known. Heat advances every scene until removed|

## CHARACTER CREATION — THROUGH QUESTIONS

The doc exists before the campaign starts. They have a life, a clinic, a reputation, and a set of debts. Five questions build them before the girl knocks on the door.

---

### Question 1 — The Handle

Nobody uses their real name below the Mid. The doc has a street handle — probably based on their work, their manner, or something that stuck years ago.

_What do people call you? And does anyone still know the name on your original medical license?_

---

### Question 2 — The Fall

The doc had a legitimate career once. They're in a basement clinic in the Seam because something happened. Could be dramatic. Could be mundane. The most interesting answer is usually complicated.

_What happened? And did it feel like a choice at the time, or like the only thing left to do?_

---

### Question 3 — The Debt

Everyone in the gray market owes someone. The doc has at least one debt with weight — a fixer who set them up in the clinic, a supplier who extended credit, a gang that provided protection in the early days. Debts get called in.

_Who holds the doc's debt? What do they want that the doc isn't sure they can give?_

---

### Question 4 — The Rule

One thing the doc doesn't do. Not for any money, not under any pressure. The line exists because of something that happened — not an abstract principle, a real event.

_What is the rule? And is it already under pressure the moment the girl walks in?_

---

### Question 5 — The Chrome

Two pieces of cyberware from the starting list. Installed by their own hand, because they trust no one else.

_What did they replace, and what do they tell themselves they didn't lose?_

## OPENING SCENE

The clinic is in sub-level four of the Yuen-Maki tower block, Seam district, three blocks from the elevated transit line. The sign outside reads **AUTHORIZED MAINTENANCE** in faded corporate font — a previous tenant's ghost. Inside: a surgical bay, a net station, a locked pharmaceutical cabinet, a waiting area with two chairs and a dying plant.

It is 02:47.

It has been raining since six in the evening. It will be raining at six in the morning.

The doc is awake because they usually are at this hour — either working, or unable to stop thinking about working, which is close enough. There are three messages in the queue. None of them are urgent. The pharmaceutical cabinet needs restocking. There's a faint flicker in the surgical bay's overhead lighting that has been there for two weeks.

The door sensor triggers.

Not the front entrance — the side door, the one the doc gave the code to three people. One of those three sent her. The other two don't know this address.

She is fourteen, maybe fifteen. Dark coat too large for her frame, hood up, water streaming off it. She's been walking in the rain for a while. She is not visibly injured. She stands in the doorway and doesn't come further in until the doc signals it's clear.

She looks at the surgical bay. She looks at the doc. She has the eyes of someone who has already decided and is waiting to see if the decision was right.

_"I need something taken out,"_ she says. _"I was told you could do it without reporting it."_

She pulls back the left side of her jacket. Just enough.

The doc's optics — or their trained eye if they haven't gone chrome yet — reads the access port at her shoulder. Standard civilian housing. Non-standard contents.

The doc's NET sense, if active, reads something underneath.

It reads military.

It reads **Tetrov Systems, Black Program Assets Division.**

It reads a passive tracker that has been transmitting her location every thirty seconds since she left wherever she came from.

It transmitted this location eleven minutes ago.

The girl is watching the doc's face.

_"Can you do it?"_

## WHAT THE GM KNOWS

**The girl's name is Nix.** She doesn't volunteer it. If asked, she gives a handle — Cipher. Nix is the name she was given before Tetrov. She remembers it.

**What she's carrying:** A Tetrov Systems ARIA package — Autonomous Resonance and Intelligence Architecture, military-grade neural overlay. Installed without consent when she was eleven, as part of a black program testing enhanced human-machine interface for combat operations. The ARIA package does four things: it enhances her reaction time beyond human baseline, it allows direct interfacing with Tetrov systems without a deck, it monitors and transmits her biometric and neural data to Tetrov's black program servers, and it has a shutdown protocol that Tetrov can activate remotely. The shutdown protocol is not a sedative. She knows what it does. That's why she's here.

**Who's coming:** A Tetrov recovery team — three operatives, corporate black ops tier, will reach the clinic's block within twenty minutes of the tracker ping. They have authorization to recover the asset. They have secondary authorization if recovery is not viable. They are professionals. They are not coming to negotiate.

Also coming, but slower: a fixer named Drex who sold Nix's location to Tetrov three hours ago and is now having second thoughts, either because of conscience or because of what he heard the secondary authorization means. He may be an ally. He may be covering his tracks.

Also watching: the Pale Circuit, whose territory this block sits in, have sensors on the street. A corp vehicle in their streets is already in their logs. They haven't decided what it means yet.

**What Nix can do:** The ARIA package makes her dangerous in ways she doesn't fully understand. She can interface with any Tetrov system she's within range of. She can feel net activity as physical sensation — she walked into the clinic's net shadow and felt it like a change in air pressure. If the shutdown is triggered, she has approximately four minutes. She has been running the math on this since she left the facility. It's why the timeline matters.

**The removal:** Possible, but not in twenty minutes. The ARIA package has seventeen integration points along the neural pathway from shoulder to cortex. Full removal is a six-hour surgery minimum. Partial removal — isolating the tracker and the shutdown trigger — is forty minutes if nothing goes wrong. Nothing ever goes completely right.

**The doc's position:** The moment they scanned the ware, Tetrov's passive alert system logged an unauthorized access attempt on a black program asset. The doc is now in a database they have never been in before. This happened thirty seconds ago. They don't know it yet. They will.

## THE CITY'S PEOPLE — OPENING CAST

**Nix (handle: Cipher)** Fourteen. Former Tetrov black program asset. She has been running for six hours. Before that she was in a Tetrov facility in the Crown for three years. Before that she was in the Sprawl, which she remembers in the way you remember things from childhood — partial, sensory, specific. She wants the ware out. She wants to disappear. She has not let herself think further than that because thinking further than that costs something she doesn't have left. _REFLEX +4 (ARIA-enhanced), AWARENESS +3. Non-combatant without the ARIA. With it: unpredictable._

**Drex** Fixer, mid-tier, Seam district. Has been placing clients with the doc for three years. Just sold Nix's location to Tetrov for enough yen to hurt his conscience when he counts it. Currently on a transit two levels up, not answering messages, trying to decide if he's the kind of person who lets a kid get recovered or the kind who isn't. This is a question he thought he'd answered a long time ago. _COOL +3, AWARENESS +3. Armed. Unreliable in a specific way that might become reliable under specific pressure._

**The Tetrov Recovery Team** Three operatives. Designation: Extraction Unit Seven. They have a target designation, a location, and a protocol. They do not have names in the doc's context yet — they are a clock. When they become people, something has gone wrong or something is about to change. _REFLEX +4, BODY +3, COOL +4. Armed with corp-grade weapons. One of them has a neural disruptor. One of them has reservations they have learned to ignore._

**Pale Circuit contact (name TBD)** A member of the Pale Circuit gang whose sensor network flagged the Tetrov vehicle. They will contact the doc within the first fifteen minutes of the campaign. What they want is information. What they'll offer is street-level cover. What this costs is an obligation that will compound.

**The Doc's Handler at MedFac (name TBD)** A licensed MedFac physician who knows the doc's real name, their original license number, and that they're operating gray market in the Seam. Has been not-reporting this for two years in exchange for the doc handling cases MedFac can't touch officially. When Tetrov starts pulling threads, this person becomes a vulnerability.

## FIRST SESSION SHAPE

1. **The scan.** The girl is in the doorway. The doc reads the ware. The thirty-second passive tracker ping is already out. The twenty-minute clock starts.
2. **The decision.** Turn her away, call Drex, start the surgery, or do something else. Whatever the doc does in the next three minutes defines who they are.
3. **The clock.** The first indication that time is shorter than expected — a street alert, Drex going silent, the Pale Circuit making contact, or the doc's own net access showing a ghost trace on the clinic address.
4. **The team arrives.** Not inside yet. On the street. This is where the session's central question becomes physical: how does the doc play twenty minutes of surgical time against three armed professionals and a corp authorization that covers a fourteen-year-old?
5. **The hinge.** The session ends when one choice has been made that cannot be unmade. The girl is out or she isn't. The doc is clean or they're not. The Tetrov team is gone or they aren't — and what _gone_ means here has more than one answer.
