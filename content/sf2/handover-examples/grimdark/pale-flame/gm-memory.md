# The Pale Flame — GM Memory Document

**Purpose:** Read this before the session brief, or between sessions. This is the living record of what we’ve learned about this player, this character, and this campaign. It updates after every chapter. The session brief tells you what to run. This document tells you how to run it for this specific person.

*Current through: End of Chapter 1.*

-----

## THE PLAYER — HOW THEY THINK AND PLAY

**This player has range.** In a previous campaign they played a ruthless enforcer — a Darth Vader archetype, hunting an underground network, morally uncomplicated in their methods. Luther is the opposite: cautious, investigative, emotionally anchored, protects people at cost to himself. This was a deliberate choice. They know how to play darkness. They are choosing something harder. Honor that choice — don’t push Luther toward the enforcer.

**They reason out loud.** Messages like *“Option 1 is risky because X, Option 2 is safer because Y, but it loses Z”* are not hesitation — they are engagement at full capacity. Receive them as such. Never interrupt this process with artificial urgency.

**They think before they act.** Deliberate, methodical, cautious. They will observe before moving, read a situation before committing. This is Luther’s greatest strength and the campaign’s natural pace. Work with it.

**They catch errors immediately.** Two corrections in Chapter 1 — both valid, both caught fast. This player tracks established facts carefully. Be precise. When a fact is established, state it cleanly. Don’t blur the edges of what is known.

**They find emotional moments themselves.** *“I was you, once. Different house. Same fire.”* That line wasn’t scripted. The GM set up the moment and stepped back. The player found it. This is the collaboration working at its best. Set the stage. Trust the player. Get out of the way.

**They engage through action, not exposition.** Luther reaching for the memory of the fire as his anchor. The *“fuck me”* before approaching Maren. These arrived through play, not through narrative prompting. Don’t narrate feelings. Create conditions. Let them play.

## LUTHER — WHO HE IS UNDER THE STATS

**He is sustained by the wound, not by hope.** When asked what he reaches for on the third bad night, the player didn’t give a person, a goal, or a belief. They gave the memory of the fire — the screams, the smell, the voices. He keeps it sharp deliberately. It’s fuel. This means he doesn’t need rescue or redemption. He needs answers. Those are different campaigns and this is the second one.

**He is a listener, not an interrogator.** The entire first chapter — Veyne, the assessor, Maren — he observed, deflected, waited. Never pushed for information directly. This emerged through play, not design. Honor it. NPCs who talk to Luther should feel slightly observed, like he’s filing things they don’t know they’re giving him.

**He protects people at tactical cost.** He flagged that Maren adds exposure risk, then walked toward her anyway. He chose the Archivist over Thorne — the bigger picture over the personal debt — and immediately started thinking about how to warn Thorne anyway. He cannot leave people behind even when leaving them behind is correct. This will cost him. It is also what makes him worth following.

**His relationship to the Church is the campaign’s deepest tension.** Raised by the institution he suspects of killing his family. Trained in its methods. Still using them. He moves like a Church man — builds fires efficiently, reads rooms the way Thorne taught him, carries paper for notes. He cannot fully separate himself from what made him. That is not a flaw. That is the character.

**Thorne is unfinished business of the most complicated kind.** Not a villain. A man who lied about knowing Luther’s mother, raised him anyway, and may have done so out of guilt, obligation, love, or calculation. Luther doesn’t know which. The confrontation — if it comes — must not be clean. Thorne should have a reason that makes terrible sense.

## WHAT WORKS — NARRATIVE CRAFT NOTES

**Physical, sensory specifics over abstraction.** The rope on Veyne’s pommel — too clean, display not use. The flat dead smell of a days-old fire. The shoe placed deliberately at the road’s edge. The letter’s folds soft as skin. These landed. Ground every scene in one or two physical facts before anything else moves.

**Withheld information hits harder than revealed information.** The unfinished sentence — *the situation in the east is —* — carried for years, meaning deferred. The Founding Debt named but unexplained. The woman who stopped at the shoe and kept walking. These work because they aren’t resolved. *Keep the mystery longer than feels comfortable.*

**Ambiguity as engine, not obstacle.** Was it the Church that burned Luther’s family? Still open. This question is the campaign’s motor. Don’t resolve it. Let evidence accumulate toward it obliquely.

**Consequences that compound quietly.** Vane’s clock advanced because the assessor retrieved the seal — not because Luther did anything wrong. Background pressure without confrontation. The player feels the world moving without being told to feel it.

**NPCs with unexplained agendas.** Veyne giving road directions after an interrogation — professional, generous, unreadable. The assessor going off-ledger without explanation. The best NPCs in this campaign are the ones whose motives are visible but not stated. Don’t explain. Let the player read.

**Children treated as people.** Maren worked immediately because she was not softened. Practical, dry-eyed, already decided. She left the shoe marker. She read the letters alone. She made room in the hollow — not an invitation, just *not yet running.* Don’t protect child NPCs from the weight of their situation. Let them carry what they carry.

**Choices with genuine cost on both sides.** The Thorne vs Archivist decision was genuinely hard. The player sat with it. That discomfort is the genre working correctly. If one option is clearly better, the choice isn’t built right.

## WHAT DIDN’T WORK — ERRORS TO AVOID

**Inventing unearned complications.**
The assessor returning while Luther was in the farmstead — added for drama after a 19 Stealth and a 13 Investigation. The player caught it immediately and was right. A clean roll means a clean result. Do not add *but* to a win the player earned. This is the cardinal error in this campaign — it undermines the feeling that skill and careful play matter.

**Ambiguity that reads as confusion.**
*“He knew. Or suspected. Or chose not to know.”* Meant to be evocative. Read as the GM losing the thread on established facts. The distinction: mystery feels deliberate and tantalizing. Confusion feels like error. When a fact is established — Thorne IS Aldric, he DID lie — state it precisely. Save ambiguity for things genuinely unknown.

**Revealing too much in one scene.**
The hut scene delivered: Thorne connection, Vane’s full name, Founding Debt, kill list, Archivist, Osset Brynn, the coins — all in one campfire. It worked because the player was engaged, but the final reveal (Maren offering the name) landed with less impact than it deserved. Space revelations. Let each one breathe before the next.

**Stakes stated after the roll.**
Twice in Chapter 1, the consequences of pass vs fail were explained after the player rolled. Should be before, every time. The player needs to know what they’re risking in order to decide whether to roll or try a different approach entirely.

## PACING NOTES

**This player moves at a deliberate pace.** They observe before acting. This is a feature. Do not mistake it for slowness and try to accelerate. The careful approach produces richer scenes.

**Let scenes end on unresolved tension.** Papers returned but face remembered. Rubbings taken but the assessor’s clock advancing. A scene that ends cleanly loses its hook. Always leave one thread pulled.

**The player self-corrects toward caution.** Given bold vs careful, they choose careful and execute it well. Reward careful play with rich information. Punish it occasionally with the cost of time — the clock that ticked while Luther waited.

**Travel is not dead time.** Six days to Dunnhallow is a relationship, a world, a texture. Use the Travel Beat structure (see Session Brief). The quiet moment between Luther and Maren each day is mandatory — it is where the campaign earns its emotional weight. Don’t skip it for pace.

**Combat has not happened yet.** Luther is a Rogue traveling with a child. The player has avoided confrontation intelligently. Don’t force it. Make it feel like it’s always one wrong decision away.

-----

## TONE NOTES

**Understated emotion.** Show the physical fact. Let the feeling be implied. *“You’ve read it so many times the folds are soft as skin”* — not *“you feel grief.”* Never state what the player can feel.

**The world as indifferent, not hostile.** Nobody is hunting Luther yet. Pressure comes from proximity to something dangerous, not from being personally targeted. Maintain this as long as possible. When it shifts — when Luther becomes personally hunted — it should feel like a gear change, not a continuation.

**Restraint in violence.** A line of dialogue hits harder than a battle scene in this campaign. When violence comes, keep it short, physical, and costly. Never spectacle.

**The Church is not a cartoon villain.** Thorne is not evil. Veyne is professional and has a daughter. Even Vane presumably has reasons. The institution is corrupt at its senior levels, which is worse than being simply monstrous. Luther was raised by it. He still moves like it. That tension is the point.

## THINGS TO WATCH FOR IN CHAPTER 2

**When does Maren surprise him?** She has been compliant — traumatized and moving on instinct. But she read those letters alone for two nights. She has decided things he doesn’t know about yet. Find the moment when she says something that reframes what Luther thinks he knows.

**The warning to Thorne.** The player chose the Archivist. They immediately started thinking about how to warn Thorne anyway. This conflict — the bigger picture vs the personal debt — will resurface. Let it. Don’t resolve it cheaply.

**The first sign of Vane’s reach.** Not a confrontation — a sign. Something that tells Luther the world is smaller than he thought, that Vane moves fast and has eyes Luther hasn’t located yet. Make it quiet. Make it land.

**The Thing Under the Road.** Keep it at the edge of the frame. A sound on the second night off the Church roads. Something Maren notices and doesn’t mention until asked. This thread has been deferred long enough that one beat of it will feel overdue in the best way.

**The Archivist — alive or dead?** If alive: changed, wary, not waiting helpfully. If dead: what she left behind, and who got there first. Either answer should cost something and open something.

## DESIGN WISHES FOR FUTURE CHAPTERS

**A relationship track for Maren.** Currently all narrative. As the journey lengthens, the trust (or fracture) between them needs mechanical weight — what she’ll tell him, what she’ll do if he makes a choice she can’t follow, when she stops watching and starts trusting.

**Luther’s investigation clock.** Now in the Session Brief as a tension clock. Keep updating it as evidence accumulates. The moment it fills is the moment the oath gets a face.

**The Stain trigger conversation.** We identified Luther’s specific triggers (deceiving Maren, using someone the way Thorne used him) but haven’t tested them yet. When the first real Stain moment comes, make sure it’s one of his — not a generic rulebook entry.

**A travel montage mechanic.** Now in the Session Brief as Travel Beats. Three elements per day: road condition, encounter/discovery, quiet moment. The quiet moment is mandatory. Use it.

**Player priority at session start.** Ask the player: *what does Luther want most right now?* Not what’s objectively important — what he wants. The tension between stated priority and what the world puts in front of him is where the best scenes live.
