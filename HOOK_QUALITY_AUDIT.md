## Space Opera

### World-Building — Grade: A-

The Compact Collapse is a sophisticated central concept: not invasion or rebellion, but institutional rot — budget shortfalls cascading into beacon failures into isolation. The beacon corridor economy creates clear constraints on travel and power: whoever controls a beacon controls traffic. The five-faction ecology (Compact Remnants, Pirate Fleets, Corporate Blocs, Frontier Settlements, Rogue AIs) is well-articulated and creates natural conflicts.

**Strengths:**
- The Crew Economy (fuel, food, ammo, morale, trust all finite, captain allocates under uncertainty) is excellent thematic grounding.
- The Signal (mysterious broadcast from beyond the Rim, three expeditions sent, one returned) is a compelling mystery hook.
- Infrastructure-as-politics: the lore recognizes that distance is its own constraint and that politics is logistics.

**Weaknesses:**
- Rogue AIs are mentioned but underdeveloped — what are they doing? Are they hostile, indifferent, or alien in their goals?
- The Remnant's legitimacy problem is well-stated but lacks specific examples of how authority actually fails in practice.

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: A

Central tension: **Obsolescence and reinvention in a broken system.** Every species has a Compact-era role that no longer functions. Every faction is trying to position itself in a post-Compact galaxy. Hooks, origins, and classes all reinforce: "What do you do when the role you were built for no longer exists?"

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: A

Stands apart from generic space opera by:
- **No "evil empire" or "noble rebellion" dichotomy.** All factions are trying to survive with different strategies.
- **FTL constrained by beacon corridors** rather than trivial warp drive. Creates scarcity and geography.
- **Logistics and infrastructure failure** rather than military drama as the core tension.
- **Crew Economy** as the emotional center — the game is about managing people under scarcity, not about battles.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A-

- **Vocabulary:** Spacefaring terminology is precise (bulkheads, decks, berths, beacon corridors, FTL via beacons). Good grounding.
- **Tone:** Epic 40%, Gritty 30%, Witty 30% is well-balanced. "A shared joke during a crisis is worth more than a speech" is genre-defining.
- **NPC Voice Guide:** "Military officers: short declarative sentences. Engineers: precise, detail-oriented. Intelligence operatives: measured, say less than they know." Clear and actionable.
- **Weakness:** "AI exists but is distrusted" is a setup without payoff — mentioned but not developed in the factions section.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A

The strongest hook writing overall. Hooks are vivid, morally complex, and mechanically rich.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"Forty Thousand" (Driftrunner)** — Perfect tension: exact debt, exact payment, exact timeline. Forces a decision between self-preservation and helping a stranger. Masterclass in player agency.
- **"Old Colors" (Universal)** — Implied history of walking away from a unit, sworn colors, and a moral crucible all working together.
- **"Dead in Space"** — Brutally effective at forcing a choice between survival and conscience.
- **"The Restoration" (Human)** — Internal crew conflict with real stakes.
- **"Three Administrators" (Sylphari)** — Philosophically rich hook about exploitation.

**Weakest hooks:**
- **"Nominal" (Engineer)** — Station AI denying reality is vague. No clear dramatic hook or escalation. Feels narrow to the Engineer class.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: A

Five core species are exceptionally well-realized:
- **Human:** "Architects of the Compact. Lost their empire. Don't know who they are without it." Reframes humans as dealing with obsolescence, not default capability.
- **Vrynn:** "The network in a galaxy losing the ability to talk to itself." Communication specialists adapting to communication failure.
- **Korath:** "The builders in a galaxy that's breaking." Direct, contractual — Disadvantage on Deception flows naturally from the lore.
- **Sylphari:** "Holding the pieces together while wondering if they should let them fall." Burden of responsibility mixed with doubt.
- **Zerith:** "Thriving in the chaos." The only species that didn't lose something because they never had it. Freedom-as-exile.

**Shifted Origins are brilliant** — "Untethered" (Human), "Severed" (Vrynn), etc. are mechanically and narratively tied to the base species' advantages becoming liabilities. Vrynn Severed loses the signal network that defined them. Sophisticated design.

**Balance issues:**
- **Sylphari are too strong**: Advantage on Insight + starting at Favorable with a contact.
- **Zerith are underpowered**: Initiative/Reflex bonuses are niche. Disadvantage on CON in sustained cold is a rare penalty.
- **Human**: No racial network means genuine isolation in a galaxy where every other species has one.

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: B

**Stat distribution problems:**
- **CHA oversaturated**: 5 of 14 playbooks (Captain, Sovereign, Voice, Herald, Broker) are CHA-primary at 17. Too many leaders.
- **WIS underrepresented**: Only Wanderer is WIS-primary.
- **DEX reasonable**: Driftrunner, Shade, Corsair (3 playbooks).

**Overlap issues:**
- **Captain vs. Sovereign**: Both CHA leaders with authority. Captain rallies crew, Sovereign mediates. Same stat array, similar role — too similar.
- **Ghost vs. Shade**: Both DEX infiltrators. Ghost leaves no trace, Shade kills clean. Mechanically distinct, thematically brothers.
- **Hacking is ubiquitous**: 7+ classes have Hacking, creating sameness across builds.

**Starting equipment/credits imbalance:**
- Captain's personal shield (5 damage absorption) is extremely valuable.
- No standardized starting wealth baseline.

## Fantasy

### World-Building — Grade: A

Beautifully articulated. The Sundering (catastrophic knowledge loss — not a single event but an ongoing condition) is a sophisticated central concept: the world is *forgetting itself*. The Five Kingdoms are individually distinct: Thornwall (military, shield), Crestlands (trade), Meridia (cultural, Collegium home), Pale March (sparse, distrustful), Heartlands (oldest, deepest ruins). The Accord of Thorns creates inter-kingdom tension: it's binding but no one fully understands the legal language anymore.

**Strengths:**
- Magic as echo is inspired: "Magic in this world is not ascending; it is residue." Every spell draws on a diminishing reservoir.
- The Collegium (multi-kingdom institution preserving what it can't understand, losing knowledge faster each decade) is fantastic — a cultural institution in decline, genuinely trying, genuinely failing.
- Gods thinning their connection is a sophisticated theological concept. Ruins as evidence rather than dungeons.

**Weaknesses:**
- The Accord of Thorns is mentioned repeatedly but never meaningfully detailed. What does it actually say?
- Kingdom border dynamics aren't clear. Which kingdoms are friendly? Which are rivals?

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: A+

Central tension: **Entropy, loss, and the impossibility of recovery.** The Sundering is ongoing. Magic is fading. Gods are withdrawing. Knowledge is being lost. Every character is trying to hold onto something. The Dragonkin carry fragments of pre-Sundering knowledge but can't fully access them. Scholars study ruins they can't read. Institutions are engaged in defensive preservation. This theme runs through species lore, classes, setting, and mechanical design. Perfectly coherent.

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: A+

Stands apart by:
- **Knowledge loss as the central problem** rather than a sidebar. Most fantasy assumes knowledge is preserved. Here, it isn't.
- **The Sundering as ongoing** rather than historical. The world is actively degrading.
- **Magic as finite resource** rather than infinite power source.
- **Ruins as evidence of a civilization we don't understand**, not dungeons to loot.
- **Gods withdrawing, not advancing.** Divine power is fading, not fighting demons.

This is not generic high fantasy. It's post-apocalyptic fantasy, which is much rarer and more interesting.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A

- **Vocabulary:** "Grounded fantasy language: stone and timber, torches and lanterns, horses and carts. Magic has a physical cost — fatigue, nosebleeds, trembling." Prevents the setting from feeling generic.
- **Tone:** Epic 70%, Gritty 20%, Witty 10%. Right for this setting.
- **NPC Voice Guide:** "Nobles: formal, indirect, power through what they don't say. Soldiers: direct, rank-aware. Scholars: precise, irritated by imprecision." Good specificity.
- **Investigation Guide:** Conceptually strong (translation matters, fragments contradict) but could use more specific examples of how this plays out mechanically.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A-

Excellent hooks, slightly less punchy than Space Opera. Best hooks are eerie and mysterious; weakest are generic fantasy tropes.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"The Harvester" (Universal)** — Someone is extracting knowledge itself from minds. Genuinely disturbing.
- **"The Forgetting Grove" (Elf)** — Forces a choice between saving a sacred place and respecting its agency.
- **"The Sealed Hold" (Dwarf)** — Negotiating with people imprisoned by their own oath.
- **"The Old Tongue" (Universal)** — Eerie and mysterious language that shouldn't exist.

**Weakest hooks:**
- **"Silent Garrison" (Holdfast)** — Generic "garrison goes missing" setup. No twist, no urgency, no moral choice. Could be any medieval military game.
- **"The Moving Wood" (Warden)** — "Forest advancing because predator inside" is a well-worn cliché. Predictable resolution.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: A+

The strongest genre for species lore:
- **Human:** "Adaptable, politically dominant" but "no racial network. Other species look after their own; humans are on their own." Interesting vulnerability beneath the advantage.
- **Elf:** Patience as cultural discipline, not passivity. "Melancholy of long memory and the temptation to stop caring about things that will not outlast you."
- **Dwarf:** "Your word is a contract, and a contract is unbreakable." Disadvantage on Deception flows perfectly from lore.
- **Halfling:** "People see the size and stop looking. That's the advantage." Invisibility as tool, not limitation.
- **Dragonkin:** Possibly the strongest single species lore. "The Wyrm Kingdoms fell three centuries ago, but the fear hasn't." Inherited memory through dreams. "Most NPCs start at Wary — you're feared before you're known."

Shifted origins (Climber, Watcher, Bound, Named, Vessel) are thematically coherent: human ambition hardening, elf observation becoming paralysis, dwarf oath consuming the oath-maker.

**Balance issues:**
- **Dragonkin are mechanically broken**: Overpowered in niches (Intimidation + Initiative + Ancient Lore), punished for existing socially (double time to gain trust). Incoherent.
- **Human**: No network, no starting contact bonus. Isolated by default.
- **Elf**: Trust penalty lasts "twice as long" — significant.
- **Dwarf**: Solid. Thematic and balanced.
- **Halfling**: Perfect fit.

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: C+

The weakest class balance across all genres.

**Critical issues:**
- **Oracle is overpowered**: Precognition via "Clarity of Pattern" trait (auto-success on one Insight/Perception per day) trivializes mystery. Combined with high INT/WIS, this class breaks investigations.
- **Knight and Holdfast are redundant**: Both STR 16+, similar proficiencies (Athletics, Intimidation). Different themes but same mechanical role.
- **Shadow and Warden are both DEX rogues**: Forest vs. wilderness is not enough differentiation.
- **Multiple support classes cluster** (Caretaker, Keeper, Witness): All WIS-based support — hard to tell apart.
- **Arcana appears on 6 playbooks** — overspecialized for a game that might not emphasize magic.

**HP range concern:**
- Highest: Knight/Holdfast (12 HP)
- Lowest: Seeker/Herald/Caretaker (8 HP)
- 4 HP gap is significant in a d10 combat system.

## Cyberpunk

### World-Building — Grade: A

Six megacorps functioning as nation-states with private militaries and judicial systems. The Vertical Stack (wealth = altitude; upper = arcologies, mid = neon and hustle, lower/undercity = below tax line and law) is a simple but effective metaphor. The Net is segmented post-Compact Wars — corporate ICE controls designated zones, wild Net beyond has rogue AIs and ghost programs.

**Strengths:**
- Cyberware and humanity drift (not a switch, but a drift; empathy narrows, you don't notice, people around you notice first) is a sophisticated take on augmentation's cost.
- Trauma Team vs. NCPD gap (subscription-based emergency response vs. underfunded public law enforcement) is good inequality worldbuilding.
- Fixers as the shadow economy's connective tissue.

**Weaknesses:**
- While coherent, the worldbuilding is somewhat standard cyberpunk. Megacorps with militaries, vertical cities, neural implants, black markets — familiar tropes executed well but not transformed.
- The Compact Wars (mentioned as history that segmented the Net) aren't explained. An off-screen event that should be more present.

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: A-

Central tension: **Complicity and erosion.** No one is clean. Operatives depend on fixers. Fixers depend on corps. Corps depend on Trauma Team. Everyone is complicit because the system is what keeps them alive. The Ripperdoc installs chrome they know will erode people's humanity. The Corporate benefits from exploitation they can see clearly. The Unplugged maintain principled refusal that costs them daily. Theme is strong but could be tighter — less uniquely articulated than Fantasy's entropy or Space Opera's obsolescence.

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: A-

Distinct from generic cyberpunk by:
- **Cyberware drift as a long decline** rather than a dramatic choice. You don't decide to lose humanity; it leaks away.
- **Fixers as the narrative center** rather than megacorps. Focus is on crew and connections, not taking down a corp.
- **The complicity gradient** (everyone benefits from the system) rather than heroes vs. oppressors.

Good but not transformative. The genre is well-executed but fairly orthodox.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A

- **Vocabulary:** "Chrome for cyberware, flatline for kill, jack in for net access, meat for organic body, zero for nobody, corpo for corporate." Longer and more specific than most genres.
- **Tone:** Gritty 50%, Witty 30%, Epic 20%. "The grandeur is in small victories — surviving the night, keeping your crew alive."
- **Investigation Guide:** "Data is abundant but truth is manufactured; every source is compromised." Sophisticated worldbuilding that shapes gameplay.
- **Genre Heat (Attention System):** Tracking how much notice the player draws from power structures. Mechanically reinforces noir-ish paranoia.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A

Excellent hooks with real edge. Visceral, philosophically disturbing, and cinematic.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"Implanted" (Universal)** — False memories so good they were lived. Existentially disturbing.
- **"Stripped"** — Cybernetic bodies carved up while conscious. Visceral and horrible.
- **"Someone Home" (Netrunner)** — Negotiate with a consciousness holding you hostage.
- **"The Wellness Implant" (Corporate)** — A product that works as advertised while weaponizing children. Thematically dark.

**Weakest hooks:**
- **"Eleven Minutes" (Netrunner)** — Amnesiac netrunner who uploaded something. Too narrow (requires Netrunner class) and duplicates the "Memory Gap" universal hook's amnesia premise.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: A-

Five origins (all human variants, differentiated by social position):
- **Operative:** "You are pure capability for rent." Fixer and former client as starting contacts. The cost: "freedom is the pitch; dependency is the product."
- **Fixer:** "The connective tissue. You don't do jobs; you make jobs happen." Vulnerability: "you know too much, every connection is a potential leak."
- **Ripperdoc:** "You see what chrome costs up close." Installing chrome you know will erode someone — genuinely heavy moral weight.
- **Corporate:** "Inside the machine. You see it clearly. You benefit from it anyway."
- **Unplugged:** Ideological refusal of augmentation. Not a purity play — someone operating at a systemic disadvantage because of principled refusal.

**Shifted Origins are exceptional**: "Owned" (drowning in debt), "Burned" (exposed), "Hollowed" (losing empathy), "Apparatus" (became the machine), "Compromised" (selling out). Tragic and mechanically interesting.

**Balance issues:**
- **Unplugged are mechanically weak**: "Advantage on natural perception" is vague and "resistance to electronic warfare" is niche. Compare to Operative getting 2 favorable contacts.
- **All other origins** are reasonably balanced with 2 contacts each.

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: B

**Critical issues:**
- **Ghost is overpowered**: DEX 17 + "Zero Trace" trait (never leaves evidence) + Holo-Cloak (Advantage on Stealth) = should never be caught. Too clean, no meaningful weakness.
- **Netrunner vs. Analyst overlap**: Both INT-primary with Hacking. Netrunner has "Deep Dive" auto-succeed — strictly better. Needs differentiation (network infiltration vs. crime investigation).
- **Dealmaker is underpowered**: CHA-primary but trait is narrative-only ("Marker Called" = owed favors). Doesn't match the mechanical power of "Deep Dive" or "Adrenaline Overclocked."
- **Starting Credits catastrophe**: Netrunner starts with 1000 credits vs. most classes at 120-200. This is a 5-8x wealth gap in a gig economy game.
- **Hacking saturation**: 7+ classes have it, making it the universal skill rather than a specialization.

## Grimdark

### World-Building — Grade: B+

The Shattered Provinces is competent but less deeply articulated than Space Opera, Fantasy, or Cyberpunk. The Pact of Ashes (forty-year-old treaty from the War of Wyrms) is a reasonable setup, and the observation that it's "rotting from accumulated resentment" rather than breaking from a single cause is good. The Church of the Pale Flame spanning all provinces is a solid multi-kingdom institution. The Wasting (famine spreading from the south with a suspiciously precise pattern) is an active crisis hook.

**Strengths:**
- The mercenary economy loophole (Pact forbids standing armies but not mercenary companies) is a neat explanation for the player's role.
- The Church's lore (hospitals + orphanages + Inquisition; sincere mission + institutional power + the gap between them) is strong.

**Weaknesses:**
- The houses lack depth. "Veldran is merchant nobility, wealth is power" and "Sylvara is ancient woodland, patience is weapon" are one-liners, not deep lore. Compare to Fantasy's Five Kingdoms with geography, culture, and specific Accord language.
- The Church's role is briefly stated but lacks the detail needed to sustain long play.

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: A-

Central tension: **Complicity, corruption, and the moral cost of survival.** Mercenary work is morally compromising by definition. The Pact is rotting from inside. The Church does good and enforces power simultaneously. Houses are all scheming. There's no clean side. Coherent but less uniquely articulated than the top genres.

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: B+

Distinct by:
- **Mercenary economy focus** rather than large military powers. The player is always in morally gray positions.
- **Pact-based stability under pressure** (treaty fraying, not sudden war).
- **Magic as forbidden and costly** rather than a school system.

Good but less distinctive. Grimdark is inherently harder to differentiate because the genre itself is increasingly common.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A-

- **Vocabulary:** "Blunt, physical language: mud, blood, iron, rot. Violence has weight. Death is ugly." Good grounding.
- **Tone:** Gritty 60%, Epic 25%, Witty 15%. Appropriate.
- **Company Asset:** Five dimensions (Strength, Morale, Reputation, Intelligence, Provisions). Explicitly degrading between chapters without maintenance — creates resource pressure.
- **Investigation Guide:** "Evidence is coerced, not discovered." Strong noir-ish principle. Faction clock (longer investigation = more danger) adds pressure.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A

Gritty, morally complex, and mechanically rich. The best-balanced genre for hooks.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"Both Contracts" (Veldran)** — Manage opposing client demands. Perfect mercenary dilemma.
- **"Wrong Side" (Universal)** — Mercenaries captured after losing a war. Immediate desperation.
- **"The Mercy"** — Freed prisoners killed people, now the company owes debts. Moral weight.
- **"The Monastery"** — Choice between shelter and sharing with deserters.

**No weak hooks identified.** Grimdark has the most consistently high-quality hook writing.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: B+

Origins are mercenary factions, not species — appropriate for the genre. Mixed depth.

- **House Stonemark**: "Word is bond. Cannot lie (cultural, not magical). Bluntness reads as insult to everyone else." Has mechanical and behavioral specificity. Best origin.
- **Oathless**: "Refuse to take oaths in a world built on them. Free because they owe nothing. Distrusted because they swear to nothing." Good concept, underdeveloped execution.
- **House Veldran**: "Merchant nobility. Politically dominant." Underdeveloped — what makes a Veldran distinct in behavior or culture?
- **House Sylvara**: "Long-lived, keen-eyed, patient to a fault." Generic.
- **House Ashfang**: "Feared and distrusted, survive in exile." Thin on detail.
- **Pale Flame**: Church-trained, doctrine-driven. **Red flag: one description mentions "training and controlling Resonants" — this is Epic Scifi terminology, suggesting a copy-paste error.**

The asymmetries are intentional and well-designed. Different houses have different advantages. Oathless are weakest by design (friendless is the price of freedom).

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: A-

The best class balance across all genres.

**Faction breakdown:**
- **Veldran** (Broker, Assessor, Outrider, Factor): Minor overlap between Broker and Factor (both negotiate), but Broker manipulates while Factor manages trade.
- **Sylvara** (Arbiter, Watcher, Thornguard): Intellect, perception, and brawn — fully distinct.
- **Stonemark** (Pactmaker, Holdfast, Sapper): Contracts, defense, and engineering — distinct professions.
- **Oathless** (Blade, Scavenger, Whisper): Combat, stealth, and manipulation — distinct.
- **Ashfang** (Ember Scholar, Charblood, Exile Voice): Knowledge, power, and advocacy — thematically strong.
- **Pale Flame** (Inquisitor, Chaplain, Crusader, Shepherd): Minor overlap between Chaplain and Shepherd (both WIS support), but role distinction is clear.

**Minor notes:**
- CHA is slightly overloaded (7-8 playbooks). Appropriate given the mercenary/political focus.
- DEX is underrepresented (2 playbooks: Outrider, Scavenger).

## Noire

### World-Building — Grade: A-

A city that runs on money, secrets, and careful distribution of both. Police are overworked or bought. The wealthy are untouchable until they aren't. The streets have their own justice. The emphasis on procedural corruption (DA office politics, fixed trials, connected cops) rather than cartoonish villainy is good.

**Strengths:**
- The Office asset (Reputation, Files, Network, Security, Equipment) is well-designed — represents accumulated investigation infrastructure.
- The Attention system (tracking how much heat the player draws from power structures) is mechanically smart and reinforces paranoia.
- Investigation guide (evidence is abundant but truth is manufactured; every source is compromised) is more sophisticated than typical noir.

**Weaknesses:**
- The city itself lacks texture. There's a DA office, hotels, bars — but no neighborhoods, geography, factions, or specific institutions with names and power. Compare to Cyberpunk's Vertical Stack or Fantasy's Five Kingdoms. Noire is location-generic: it could be any city from any era.

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: A

Central tension: **Corruption is systemic, and the more you investigate, the more complicit you become.** Every case requires crossing a line. Every line crossed makes you more like the people you're investigating. The truth is always uglier than the lie. Tight and runs through all mechanical and narrative elements.

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: A-

Distinct by:
- **Investigation as the core gameplay loop** rather than combat.
- **Tracking attention/heat as a mechanic** that shapes what risks are worth taking.
- **Systemic corruption** rather than localized villainy.

Strong and well-articulated, but the setting itself (generic city, time-period agnostic) could be richer.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A

- **Vocabulary:** Good noir terminology (tailing, stakeout, mark, grift, fall guy). Places have character.
- **Tone:** Gritty 50%, Witty 35%, Epic 15%. "The grandeur is in the revelation — the moment the case clicks."
- **Attention System:** Genre Heat tracking is excellent mechanical reinforcement of noir paranoia.
- **Office Asset:** Five dimensions representing investigation infrastructure. Sensible and well-integrated.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A

Strong hooks with real mystery. Every hook presents a moral choice with consequences.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"Five Names" (Universal)** — A dead stranger left your name in a will, plus a list of names running out. Exceptional tension.
- **"Fifteen Minutes" (Lawyer)** — Defendant claims the prosecutor is the killer, 15 minutes before arraignment.
- **"The Client's Lie" (PI)** — Hired to find someone who doesn't want to be found. Classic noir moral ambiguity.
- **"Killed Story" (Reporter)** — Editor suppressed a story about nine dead people. Institutional corruption.

**No weak hooks identified.** Some hooks ("Wrong Body," "Missing Spouse") are universal noir tropes, but that's a feature of the genre.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: A

Origins are professions (PI, Lawyer, Criminal, Enforcer, Reporter) — perfect for noir:
- **PI:** "You find things people want hidden. Breaking silence is the job." The cost: every case requires crossing a line; the lines accumulate.
- **Lawyer:** "You operate the system's machinery. You know how silence is manufactured." Three distinct moral positions (defense, fixer, prosecutor turned).
- **Criminal:** "You are the silence — the economy that functions because nobody talks about it." Not a rebel but part of the machine.
- **Enforcer:** "You maintain order through force. The question noir asks is: whose order? At what cost?"
- **Reporter:** "The genre's moral compass and its most dangerous position." Expose things, sources are expendable, every publication makes you a target.

Shifted origins (Compromised, Entangled, Marked, Cold, Exposed) capture degradation and corruption well. Intentionally asymmetric — different social positions create different gameplay.

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: A-

**The most balanced stat distribution across all genres:**
- INT-primary: 3 classes (Methodical, Prosecutor Turned, Investigative Journalist)
- DEX-primary: 3 classes (Thief, Grifter, Street PI)
- STR-primary: 3 classes (Enforcer, Private Muscle, Mobster)
- CHA-primary: 3 classes (Information Broker, Columnist, Fixer Lawyer)
- WIS-primary: 2 classes (Community Protector, Desperate)

**Starting credits are consistent** (~100-200 baseline). No wealth gap issues.

**Proficiency saturation is genre-appropriate**: Investigation (6+), Insight (6+), Persuasion (4+), Deception (4+) all make sense for noir.

## Epic Scifi

### World-Building — Grade: B

A thousand-year Hegemony held together by Resonants (humans with Drift attunement, treated as property). Great houses compete for Resonant allocation. The Synod (church of the Pale Flame) controls Resonant supply. The Throne balances both.

**Strengths:**
- The testing apparatus (mandatory screening at age 7, ~1 in 10,000 are latent, 60-80% die in initiation, ~1 in 100 reach mid-tier) is detailed and realistic.
- The Dimming (cognitive degradation from sustained attunement) is a sophisticated cost.
- Resonant Demographics section thoughtfully explores what 1,000 years of breeding a small population means.

**Weaknesses:**
- The setting feels procedural rather than visceral. We understand the mechanics of the Hegemony but not what it *feels* like to live in it.
- Great Houses are mentioned but not individually named or differentiated (only Vael appears in starting contacts).
- The Drift itself is intentionally mysterious, but we need more sensory detail — what does attunement feel like? What does Dimming look like? What are Resonants like as *people*?

### Thematic Coherence — Grade: B+

Central tension: **Complicity and the machinery of oppression.** Everyone in the Hegemony is complicit: farmers use Resonant infrastructure, nobles benefit from allocation, soldiers enforce it. The Synod believes sincerely in its mission while doing harm. Present but less uniquely articulated — "the system is evil and everyone is complicit" is a common sci-fi theme. What makes this *specific* Hegemony distinct?

### Setting Uniqueness — Grade: B

Distinct by:
- **Humans as the oppressed resource** rather than aliens or an underclass. Creates complicity questions.
- **Resonance as involuntary labor** rather than a chosen power or gift.
- **Millennia of stagnation** rather than rapid change.

Good but not transformative. Reads like a more detailed version of standard feudal space opera.

### Prompt Sections — Grade: A-

- **Vocabulary:** Feudal-imperial terminology (house, sworn, tithe, allocation, mandate, Conclave, dispensation). Good specificity.
- **Tone:** Gritty 40%, Epic 40%, Witty 20%. Balanced.
- **NPC Voice Guide:** Specific faction behaviors (nobility, Synod, officers, Undrift, retainers, Resonants). Actionable.
- **Retinue Asset:** (Sworn, Intelligence, Household, Drift Capacity, Reputation) — good alternative to a ship, made of people with loyalty and limits.

### Opening Hooks — Grade: A- (with caveats)

Cosmic scale with strong moral choices, but repetitive patterns.

**Standout hooks:**
- **"The Allocation"** — 14-year-old with psychic powers, family begging for return, Synod says comply. Immediate moral conflict.
- **"The Real Curse"** — Village killed the wrong healer, the curse is real, you're the kind of help they fear.

**Pattern problem:**
- **"The Allocation" and "Quiet Disposal" are structurally identical** — both are "Synod authority pressures you about a Resonant, do you comply or defy?" Half the hooks follow this authority-vs-conscience pattern.
- Missing: pure exploration, pure combat, pure mystery hooks. Most hooks are ideological/moral conflicts.

### Species/Origin Lore — Grade: B

All human, position-based rather than cultural. Origins are thin:
- **Raised (Low-Tier Resonant):** "At the bottom of the ladder." Thin on detail.
- **Choir (Mid-Tier Resonant):** Status within the system, trained by Synod. Also thin.
- **House-Born (Noble):** Unspecified role within house structures. Very thin.
- **Choir Defector (Undrift Escape):** Escaped the Synod. Most developed, still brief.

Compared to Fantasy's five species with deep lore or Cyberpunk's five origins with distinct conflicts, Epic Scifi's origins feel underdeveloped. There's no "this origin's culture makes them distinct in behavior" moment. It's all about institutional position, not lived experience.

**Terminology concern:** The "Pale Flame" church name is shared with Grimdark's medieval church. Using the same institution name across two completely different settings suggests either a copy-paste error or incomplete refactoring. **This is a quality control red flag.**

### Classes/Playbooks — Grade: Incomplete

- Possibly underweight on STR military specialists

## Critical Issues

### "Pale Flame" Copy-Paste Error

- In **Grimdark**, Pale Flame is a medieval church with hospitals, orphanages, and an Inquisition.
- In **Epic Scifi**, Pale Flame is the church that controls Resonant breeding and training.
- In Grimdark, one origin description mentions "training and controlling Resonants" — which is Epic Scifi terminology.

This is either a serious refactoring error (institution was copied but incompletely updated), a terminology clash (same name for unrelated institutions), or an incomplete merge. **Needs to be caught and fixed.**

### Epic Scifi Lacks Visceral Specificity

The setting is mechanically sound but emotionally distant. Compare:
- **Fantasy says:** "The Sundering erased the connection between the present and the deep past. You feel it as knowledge slipping away, gods going quiet, magic failing."
- **Epic Scifi says:** "Resonants are identified at age 7, 60-80% die in initiation, survivors are allocated to houses based on tier."

One is visceral; one is procedural. Epic Scifi needs more sensory detail about what Drift attunement feels like, what Dimming looks like, what Resonants are like as people.

### Grimdark Houses Are Underdeveloped

Five houses introduced with minimal lore — mostly one-liners. Compare to Fantasy's Five Kingdoms with geography, institutional roles, and specific tensions. Grimdark's houses need historical grievances, current conflicts, economic bases, and cultural distinctions.

### Noire's City Lacks Geography

Procedural corruption is well-described but the city has no shape — no neighborhoods, no named institutions, no specific power structures. Every other genre has spatial grounding (beacon corridors, vertical stack, five kingdoms, shattered provinces). Noire is location-agnostic, which is flexible but makes the setting feel generic.
